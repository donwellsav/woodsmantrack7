Harmony Park Again

September got into Harmony Park quietly.

Not by turning Atlanta generous. Not by wiping the city clean and starting over. The park still smelled of wet mulch, dog breath, bus exhaust blowing off Moreland, and the fountain's metallic spit. Somebody somewhere nearby was already frying something in old oil. A MARTA bus sighed at the curb outside the park, knelt, and hauled itself back up. A dog tag chain jingled. A bottle rolled once in a trash can and hit metal. But the air no longer came at Joey's chest like a bill collector. Morning sound traveled farther. The fountain sounded less like labor and more like water. Even the traffic had a little more room in it.

He heard all of it before he let himself believe it.

Then he saw the bench.

Same slats. Same scar where somebody had started carving a heart years ago and quit halfway through the hard part. Same end piece rubbed silver by too many people sitting in the same tired posture and calling it rest.

He spread the red flannel blanket over the wood because some rituals stayed useful after their reasons changed. Then he set the case at his feet and opened it.

The notebook went on the bench beside him, spiral binding catching the slat edge. Most of the pages were still blank. The one song lived in the middle of it, surrounded by false starts and crossed-out lines and margins where the work had gotten too honest for him to keep and too real to throw away. He left it open to a clean page because that was the part that mattered now.

Seed money first. Coins. A couple of ones. Enough to tell strangers they would not have to be the first person in the world to believe in him that morning.

Then Randall's card.

He slid the white rectangle under a pick so the breeze could not flip it over. The stock was too clean for the inside of the old case. Too square. Too sure of its corners. For one quick second the feel of that paper against his thumb pulled up Pike's notice instead: thin cheap sheet, red print, his gut's old bracing reaction arriving before his mind even finished reading the date. The memory flashed and went. What sat in the case now was not safety. It was only not the same panic. Under the card he tucked the folded song page from showcase night, creases gone soft, true still penciled in the margin.

That changed the look of it.

No rent envelope.

No folded warning riding in the felt like a second set of strings.

The empty space where the notice had lived for weeks made the case look underdressed and hopeful at the same time, and Joey distrusted hopeful things on general principle. Still, he left the card where it was. Let possibility sit in the same slot panic had burned through all month. Let the morning look at that.

The gold coin rode in the watch pocket of his jeans.

He had moved it there while dressing, wanting its weight somewhere small and separate. It still made no practical sense. It would not buy detergent or bus fare or a decent breakfast at Mae's. It stayed what Veritas had made it: pressure in the shape of payment. He left it where it was and sat down.

The wooden guitar settled into him with the old plain rightness that had outlasted every prettier argument the month had tried to make. The neck fit his hand. The body pressed against his ribs. A worn notch in the finish below the strings grazed his wrist, and the touch threw up a memory so fast it barely had time to become one: his father at the sink, thumbnail tapping the rim of an enamel mug while he counted himself in under his breath before singing to nobody but dishes and morning light. Then it was gone. The guitar stayed.

Good. Let the wood stay wood.

Nothing about it had become more glamorous because a room had heard the truth come through it.

The park at this hour was still the park.

Dog walkers doing leash negotiations in voices more patient with animals than with people. A man on the next bench over trying to lose an argument with his phone and failing. A woman in navy scrubs taking the perimeter hard before a shift, one earbud in, one hand around a paper cup, face already flattened by fluorescent light and too little sleep. A little boy dragging at his mother's wrist with the boneless insistence of children who had not yet accepted clocks as a form of violence. A city worker on the far path with a grabber stick and a trash bag, moving slow and official through wet leaves and cigarette ends.

Joey rested his right hand on the strings and waited for the old reflex.

It had governed most of the summer. The little inward flinch that rose whenever his own melody got too near the surface and needed covering. An inherited song as a lid. A standard as camouflage. Wayfaring Stranger dragged gently over the mouth of anything that sounded too much like him.

He felt the place where the reflex should have been.

Then he felt it fail.

Or maybe it was still there and he had simply stopped letting it make rules.

He let his thumb find the opening shape. Not the fragment this time. Not the half-born line that used to slip out when his hands got more honest than his head. The full song. The one he had written with torn notebook pages underfoot and his father somewhere in the bones without taking his father's mouth.

He played it for the park.

The sound took differently out here. No stage lights. No old church rafters. No Duke-built hush purchased with tickets, whiskey, and crooked belief. Just morning air, fountain mist, damp earth, bus brakes, coffee lids, strollers, shift changes, and people with places to be.

That made the song smaller.

It also made it harder to hide inside.

Harmony Park made a room of itself the way public places sometimes did, not by going quiet but by shifting its small noises half a step aside. The man on the next bench lowered his phone. The woman in scrubs lost half a step, then another. A dog stopped pulling and sat down on its own. The city worker with the trash bag paused with his grabber half raised, can tab hanging from the teeth of it. The little boy gave up tugging at his mother and turned toward the sound as if somebody had called his name from inside the strings.

Joey kept going.

His voice felt different in daylight now that it no longer had to sneak itself into the world. Not bigger. Cleaner. The melody moved through him with that unnerving mix of effort and recognition he still did not trust enough to call ease. He could hear where his father's line ran under it. He could hear the places where the song had come from other rooms and other mornings and still ended up sounding like his own mouth.

The woman in scrubs came near enough to the case to drop a dollar without breaking stride completely.

The bill hit the felt with a soft dry slap.

She looked at him over the rim of her cup. "That yours?"

Joey did not stop strumming. "Yeah."

She tucked the loose earbud into her pocket. "Thought so."

Then she kept moving, not smiling, not lingering, giving him the plain kind of witness that cost almost nothing and therefore meant more than performance.

A man in a faded Braves cap dropped two dollars after that. He never looked directly at Joey. Just bent, let the bills go, and walked on with his shoulders set in the tired shape of somebody already late to work.

The little boy's mother hauled him gently onward. The child stared back over her shoulder until the path turned and took him out of sight.

The old panic was still in him.

He needed that to stay true.

He could feel it waiting under the music like a clerk with green eyeshade and a sharpened pencil, ready at all times to start itemizing disaster. Randall's card not a deal. Pike's thirty days not rent. A room hearing you not a career. One song not the next four Randall had asked for. One dollar and two dollars not enough for anything except one dollar and two dollars. The song had not killed that part of him. It just did not get first vote anymore.

He went into the second verse.

By then the park had settled into listening without admitting it was listening. A stroller wheel squeaked in time for half a bar before rolling away. The fountain kept its own silver chatter under the vocal. A bus opening its doors at the curb let out a long hydraulic breath that fell between lines as neatly as if somebody had scored it. The city worker resumed moving but slower now, giving the song the extra measure without ever turning his head enough to make it ceremonial. Even the man on the next bench, back on his phone, kept the volume down as if the park had quietly agreed to behave itself for three minutes without needing to say so.

The Landmark had taught him that much, and the park was proving it in cheaper light.

Rooms did not answer only with applause.

Sometimes they answered by shifting the ordinary noise a little to one side until the song could stand.

His right hand had begun to believe him by then. The first pass through the song always carried the old raw edge of argument, as if some part of him still expected to be challenged on ownership. The second pass felt more like work. Bench under him. Morning on his forearms. Damp cotton at the back of his neck. Randall's card white in the case. Veritas's coin useless and steady against his thigh. The small stack of bills growing in front of him by public faith and public indifference in equal measure.

Which was why he knew, instantly and bodily, what five dollars was when the hand reached in.

Teenage hand. Quick, practiced, shameless only because shame wasted time. The boy came in from Joey's left in a gray hoodie too heavy for the weather and a backpack hanging off one shoulder. He did not look mythic. He did not look desperate enough to clean up what he was doing. He looked like a kid making a decision inside one second and trusting his body to finish it before his conscience could get ideas.

His fingers closed on the five and pulled back.

Joey saw it.

The boy saw Joey seeing it.

They hung there for one beat in the middle of the song.

Five dollars in the boy's fist.

Five dollars gone from the case.

Five dollars was coffee and eggs at Mae's if he behaved himself and did not ask for anything extra. Five dollars was detergent from the place by the laundromat and still enough left for a bus ride if he counted right. Five dollars was a small thing. Five dollars was not a small thing. It mattered exactly as much as five dollars mattered, which was to say first in the stomach.

The old part of him came up hard.

Chase him.

Say something sharp enough to make the morning admit what it cost you.

Do not sit here and let another thing be taken in public.

His strumming hand nearly missed the next string.

Then it didn't.

He looked at the boy a second longer.

Bad skin. One shoelace undone. A scab on the chin. Somebody else's five folded so tight in his fist it had already stopped looking like money. It looked like fear. Not hungry enough to sanctify. Not cruel enough to mythologize. Just human, and fast, and wrong.

Joey closed his eyes for half a beat and kept playing.

The choice did not feel noble.

It felt expensive.

The boy stayed frozen long enough to register surprise, insult, maybe both. Then he ran. Shoes slapping the wet concrete. Backpack knocking against his spine. Gone around the bend in the path before the woman in scrubs turned her head enough to understand movement had happened.

Joey played through the place where he would once have stopped.

There it was.

Not that the loss had stopped stinging. The five left its exact shape in him. He felt the absence in his knees and then his gut, the way a body felt a missed stair. Not that he had become some saint of abundance to whom a stolen bill meant less than principle. He had not. The money mattered. The insult mattered. The public smallness of it mattered. But the missing five did not get to drag the whole morning behind it. It did not get to pull the song back down into grievance.

"For the people who need it," his father had said.

This morning the line sounded plainer than it ever had.

The park kept listening in the sideways public way parks did. Nobody gathered in a holy semicircle. Nobody turned the morning into a testimonial. People slowed. People looked. People pretended they were not slowing or looking. The city worker gave up on dignified pretense altogether and leaned on the grabber stick through the end of the chorus. The man on the next bench set his phone face down on the wood beside him. A dog whined softly at its owner as if asking permission to stay put. Beyond the fence, a bus pulled away from the curb and its low diesel growl slid under Joey's last line like weather.

He kept the song moving.

By the bridge it had settled deeper into his body, less like an announcement and more like labor. Bench under him. Sweat drying under his shirt. The lighter case at his feet. The coin in his pocket. The card in the case. The thirty days Pike had moved for him and the thousand things thirty days did not solve.

Good.

Let it stay exact.

The notebook sat open on the bench where his hip kept the wind from turning pages. Clean page. Morning light on it. Not a promise to write another song today. Just the notebook, present, the way a carpenter's pencil stayed behind the ear even during lunch.

The song had to live here or it did not live. And the next one had to come from the same place or not come at all.

He opened his eyes on the last pass and looked toward the place where the boy had disappeared. The five was gone. The path was still there. The fountain still struck its little metal note under the water. Randall's card still held under the pick. His right hand still knew what to do. The sound was still moving away from him into air that did not owe him anything and was carrying it anyway.

A woman with a stroller dropped one more dollar into the case.

Joey nodded once.

The case was five dollars lighter.

Same bench. Different song. His right hand found the next shape.

Joey kept playing.
