The Gold Guitar

If anybody had asked, Joey would not have called it a breakthrough.

He would have called it bad timing. Carelessness. What happened when a tired man forgot the difference between a line check and confession. Duke had wanted clean levels. Joey had given the room the front end of the song instead.

By the turn, the room had taken on another shape.

The sound guy had gone still with one hand on the board. A bartender stood with a glass half dried in his towel. Duke had stopped in the back in that particular Duke way, warm face emptied out while he decided whether what he was hearing belonged to him yet. Joey had not joked or apologized. He had tuned, played, and kept his eyes off every face in the room. Then he had stopped before anybody could get both hands around it.

One honest thing in public started trouble fast.

Every name for it started scrambling at once.

He left the wooden guitar leaning in the backstage pocket and went outside because if Duke gave him one careful sentence right then, Joey was not sure he would stay civil.

Atlanta hit him full in the face. Rain-warmed air, thick as breath. Traffic hissing through the gutter. Bus brakes crying on Moreland. Fryer grease rolling out of somewhere close. Wet brick. A siren farther downtown trying to insist on its own emergency. The block kept moving as if nothing inside the Landmark had just shifted under his feet.

That fit the city better.

The city was not going to pause because something in him had tipped.

He went east without deciding to. Past the smoke shop. Past the bus-stop bench still wet on one end. Past two girls under an awning sharing a vape and discussing somebody else's bad character with the gravity of city council. His body had not caught up to the fact that the song existed in more than one room now. He could still feel the first line rough in his throat, the turn of the phrase in his hands, Duke's attention on the back of his neck like another heat source. Every few steps the folded page tapped his chest like a second pulse.

Old wood, he thought.

That had been the part nobody got to argue with. Not Duke. Not Cass. Not the room. The song had come through the beat-up wooden guitar, not the gold miracle he had sold and not the silver one still waiting under the bed. The gold had paid for the door. The wood had done the talking.

That thought carried him far enough down the block that the pawn-shop sign found him before he fully noticed where his feet had taken him.

He stopped.

The fluorescent buzz behind the front glass was unmistakable. So was the old metal-and-dust smell that somehow leaked through the doorframe. Joey had not meant to come near the place again this soon. He had spent enough nights replaying the sale without volunteering to stand in front of the set again.

Then he saw Cass go in.

Black shirt. Black jeans. Boots. Hair and face part-done for stage, which made the errand look stranger, not cleaner. Not because musicians never ran practical errands in performance clothes. They did. Nobody with rent got to divide life neatly enough for wardrobe to mean purity. But the direction of the errand caught him. Showcase night was gathering itself behind him, and Cass was walking into the one room on the block that knew exactly what he had turned the gold guitar into.

Joey did not follow her to the door.

He stayed where he was and let the block choose the angle. A bus sighed up to the curb and knelt between him and the storefront. When it pulled off again, he could see through the glass well enough to catch the shape of what was happening and not well enough to step in without making a choice.

The owner was behind the counter.

He reached up.

And there it was.

The gold guitar caught the sour shop light and made the whole fluorescent room look guiltier for holding it. Joey felt the old stupid recognition in his teeth. Not because he wanted it back as an answer. He didn't. He knew too much now about answers made of shine. But a body did not like seeing its own compromise rehung as merchandise.

He could smell the sale again from across the glass. Dust, old cloth, hot ballast. The owner's dry thumb turning the neck under bad light while miracle got translated into inventory because Pike's clock had been real, groceries had been real, Duke's showcase fee had been real, and for one ugly week the whole city had turned into an adding machine.

Inside, Cass did not make a show of the thing. She put one hand on it. The owner watched her. She said something Joey could not hear.

Then a man in cargo shorts came out with cheap headphones swinging in a bag, holding the door just long enough for one struck chord to spill onto the sidewalk.

Only one.

It was enough.

The note made the shop sound smaller than it had a second before. Joey saw the owner's face change by half a degree and knew, with a mean little flicker of satisfaction, that the guitar had not gone ordinary on the man no matter how neatly he had priced it.

The door shut again.

Inside, Cass counted money onto the counter.

Joey could not hear the bills land, but he knew that rhythm. Gig money. Tip money. Maybe rent money. Maybe the small float that kept groceries from turning into theory. City paper always sounded drier when it was being fed to something.

He ought to have left.

If Cass wanted the errand unwitnessed, he had no right to make himself witness. But his feet stayed put, held there by history and shame and the plain exhaustion of not having enough grace left to move.

The owner bent below the counter.

When he came up again, Joey's old case was in his hands.

That hit harder than the guitar had.

Stickers worn down to ghost language. Corners gone soft. Left latch still a little wrong. He knew the handle by sight, the bite of it against his palm, the way the case always looked as if one more bad summer ought to finish it and somehow never did.

Three fast object-memories opened and shut.

First Thursday, standing in the Landmark wings with the case clamped to his leg as if one handle could keep him from looking as scared as he was. The pawn-shop counter under bad light. The apartment stairs, hauling the case up one landing at a time through a stairwell that smelled of wet cardboard and old grease, back when he had not yet learned how tired a man could get and still keep choosing music.

Inside, Cass opened the case.

Joey could not see every detail from where he stood, only enough. Gold flare. Old lining. Her hands moving with more care than the counter deserved. When she shut it again, the case looked different. Not fixed. Not absolved. Just reassigned.

Evidence, he thought.

That was the case's job now.

A bus hissed past in the gutter. Somebody laughed under the awning behind him. Somewhere nearer the Landmark a server dumped bottles into a recycling bin with the kind of force a paid shift sometimes required. Atlanta kept at its little public chores, proving again that the world would not narrow itself to one man's symbols no matter how badly he wanted it to.

Cass came back out carrying the case over one shoulder.

She did not see him.

Or if she did, she was kind enough not to make him deal with it there on the sidewalk.

She set the case down once by her leg and looked at it as if checking whether it had turned into a different problem while she was inside. Not redeemed. Not sentimental. Just real, and heavy.

Then she took out her phone.

Traffic covered most of it. A MARTA bus groaned at the light. Somebody across the street leaned on a horn because patience had once again failed to take root in Atlanta. But Joey caught pieces.

"No," Cass said. "Listen to me."

A beat.

"It changed."

Another beat. Her jaw tightened in that tired, controlled way he had learned meant she was most serious when she sounded flattest.

"That's paperwork," Cass said. "Act like it changed."

Joey did not know who was on the other end.

He knew enough anyway.

He could have walked over then. Asked why she had gone back. Asked what debt she thought she was paying. Asked whether the old case on her shoulder meant witness or apology or some other name he did not have for it yet.

He did none of that.

Because the errand had already happened without him.

Because he still did not know how to stand beside the sight of his own case on her shoulder without turning the block into a scene.

Because whatever the night had become, it was still moving faster than his mouth.

Cass snapped the phone shut, hitched the case higher on her shoulder, and headed back toward the Landmark with the quick practical stride of a woman who still had a set to play and no belief that private revelation excused anybody from call time.

Joey stayed where he was long enough for the wet sidewalk to cool through the soles of his shoes.

Then he followed at a distance that did not ask to be noticed.

By the time the Landmark sign found him again and the front door started breathing beer and light onto the block, the old case had become one more live fact the night was carrying. Not answer. Not rescue. Not reversal. Just fact.

He went back in without trying to name it first.
