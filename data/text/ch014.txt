First Thursday

He spent the last of Thursday daylight in the apartment without finishing a single song.

The room kept offering substitutes instead. The refrigerator hummed like it had opinions. A MARTA bus dragged its brakes up Moreland. Somebody in the building dropped something heavy and swore at it afterward. Clarence's record sleeve gave a papery tick against the turntable stand whenever the fan rattled hard enough to reach that corner. The place darkened by degrees, the way it always did, not because evening softened it but because the apartment got tired of holding the day off.

Duke's voice was still in him.

Not the sentences exactly. The pressure under them. The way the man had made old beams and stale air sound like admission into a history instead of one more way to sell a room.

Joey stood by the bed in his undershirt and looked down at the silver case under it. The latches caught the remaining light and threw it back too cleanly. The thing looked like it belonged to a man who got his shirts pressed and never had to wonder whether five dollars was dinner or bus fare. No history on it he trusted. No sweat gone dark in the handle. No old nick in the edge where a doorway had once caught it because somebody had been leaving in a hurry.

The wooden guitar leaned in the chair by the window, one tuner a hair off. The worn place beside the sound hole had gone pale from years of the same right hand coming back to it. Looking at that little crescent of rubbed wood gave him a fast, hand-sized memory: his father at the kitchen table, not playing yet, only testing strings with his thumb while the bacon grease cooled in a jar by the stove, saying, Hands tell the truth faster than your mouth. Then the memory was gone and the chair was only a chair again and Atlanta was still hot.

Clarence's record waited by the turntable where Joey had left it after coming back from the Landmark that afternoon. He had handled the sleeve by the edges because once, when he was small enough to still be corrected with patience, his father had tapped his wrist and said not the middle, son, the edges, oils go where the music is going. The phrase came back now so clearly he almost looked up to answer it. He still had not turned the sleeve fully over and read the damn thing like it might ask something of him. The rougher pressure in side B felt close enough already, that thumb-worn patch on the track list like somebody's nail worrying the same place in a board. One more hard look at his father was not what he needed before Thursday.

In the kitchen drawer under dish towels and old takeout menus, the pawn-shop envelope sat with too much money in it to feel safe in a place this thin-walled. Pike's notice was still on the table in the next room, flat and patient.

He opened the drawer, looked at the thick fold of cash, and smelled the pawn shop again without wanting to. Fluorescent dust. Old metal. The owner's dry thumb turning the gold neck under bad light, miracle translated into inventory while Joey stood there pretending he was doing something practical instead of something that would sit under his ribs for weeks. He took one twenty from the envelope and shut the drawer. Whatever he learned tonight, Pike would never count it as payment.

He was not carrying twelve hundred dollars into a room built to make a man confuse wanting with judgment.

He pulled on the blue floral shirt because it was the least wrinkled thing he owned that did not make him look like he was headed to court, then reached automatically for the wooden guitar. The hidden fragment hovered somewhere below thought now, tangled up with that unresolved second-side pressure, like a word he kept not quite hearing in the next room.

He did not follow it.

He checked tuning instead. Tightened one string. Loosened another. Ran the progression from one of his father's dependable songs without finishing the phrase. Stopped. Started over on a different tune. The guitar answered him faithfully enough to feel accusatory.

By the time he stepped outside, Thursday had gone fully over to night.

Atlanta had not cooled. It had only changed texture. The heat no longer sat straight down on the city the way it did at three in the afternoon; now it came up off brick and pavement and bus roofs and the hoods of parked cars, a used heat worked over by traffic and fryer vents and bodies. Moreland hissed. A cluster of smokers outside the bar down the block laughed in bursts that sounded tired before they sounded happy. Incense from the shop two doors over tried and failed to beat grease, beer, and old exhaust.

He heard the Landmark before he trusted his eyes on it.

Ice dumped into a metal well. Fryer hiss. A microphone tapped once and then, impatiently, again. A laugh from somewhere deeper in the room, followed by the short high crack of glass meeting wood. The same building that had smelled like mop water and church bones that afternoon was breathing out beer, grease, old varnish, body heat, and cheap electrical bravado through the front door.

Inside, the holiness had been pushed down under bottles, fryer grease, and paid attention.

The church bones were still there if a person bothered to look up, but night trained the eyes lower. Toward the sweating bottles behind the bar. Toward the narrow stage under amber light. Toward knees under tables and server aprons cutting quick lanes through them. The ceiling withdrew into shadow. The stained-glass shapes along the wall had gone black. The floor Terrence had mopped in daylight now tugged faintly at the soles of shoes where old beer had dried and fresh beer had been walked over it.

The stage looked smaller with people in front of it than it had when it sat empty.

That did not comfort him.

Duke was at the same corner table with the same ledger and the same glasses, but daytime Duke and nighttime Duke were not the same man.

Nighttime Duke had traffic around him.

A woman in a black jumpsuit leaned over one elbow smiling the kind of smile people used when they needed to stay liked while asking for something. A man in a sport coat stood beside the table with a check presenter in his hand and a patient expression that did not look patient enough to last. A server bent close to hear Duke over the room noise. Duke looked up once when Joey came in and gave him a two-finger acknowledgment that contained almost no welcome at all. It was efficient, nearly clerical. Then he turned back to the server.

No coffee. No storytelling. No private church history.

Just a man keeping checks moving, names moving, drink tickets moving, all of it paid for by other people's need.

The bartender jerked his chin toward a yellow legal pad clipped beside the register.

"Thursday list."

Joey stepped up and looked down.

Different pens. Different hands. Some full names, some first names with last initials, some names crossed out hard enough to show through the page. Two stars by somebody called Hannah K. that could have meant anything from favorite to problem. He wrote Joey Woodsman in the next open space and felt his stomach give one small private drop when the name hit paper and went ordinary.

Between Marcus R. and Lena.

That was all.

In daylight Duke had made the surname sound like a neighborhood rumor with a little voltage left in it. On the legal pad it looked like any other person trying to get called before the room got too drunk to bother.

Somebody down the bar asked if Cass was on later. The bartender said no without looking up, and the disappointed little groan that followed told Joey more about the room than any flyer had.

"You playing?" the bartender asked.

"That's the ambition."

"Then buy something and let the ambition pay rent."

Not unkind. Not warm either. About as close to welcome as the room offered for free.

Joey ordered the cheapest beer. Four dollars gone. Four dollars not going anywhere near Pike. He left a dollar on the bar before he could talk himself out of the gesture and took the bottle to the end of the counter where he could see the stage without advertising how badly he wanted to study it.

The front table sat directly under the lip of the stage, close enough that a singer could see the wet shine on wing bones and the salt drying on a glass.

Four people. Two women. Two men. Office clothes collapsing toward evening. One woman had a lanyard wrapped around her wrist like she had forgotten it was still there. The man beside her had his tie off and his sleeves rolled and was eating lemon-pepper wings with total concentration, as if appetite were the only honest thing he had done all day. The other woman talked with both hands and the confidence of somebody replaying a fresh injustice for an audience she trusted to take her side. The last man, broad-shouldered in a Braves cap, laughed with his whole chest and checked his phone every time the story paused for breath.

They were not rowdy.

That was the problem.

Rowdy was simple. Rowdy admitted the stage existed and decided to fight with it. These four were just spending the rights they believed their money had bought them. Drinks. Food. Their own conversation. The music, if it wanted to matter, had to enter that arrangement and alter it without sounding like it was begging.

In the park, anybody who stopped had already given him the first inch.

In here nobody gave an inch because they thought they had already bought the night.

A woman with a mandolin was finishing a song onstage, smiling in the strained hopeful way singers smiled when they could feel effort coming off them too clearly. She got applause from three separate corners that never became one thing. She thanked the room twice, then a third time because the second thank-you had not fixed the first one, and moved off the stage sideways through a lane that only barely opened for her.

The front table did not break stride.

"No," the lanyard woman was saying, "if he writes 'circle back' one more time I am forwarding the email to God."

The others laughed. The man with the wings asked for more ranch without lowering his voice.

Joey took a swallow of the beer. Metallic first, cold second. The kind of beer built to disappear into an evening instead of asking to be noticed. He set it down and looked toward Duke.

Duke was listening.

Not to the stage in any noble sense. To the room as a whole. Timing, faces, half-empty glasses, checks, trouble, promise, who might stay, who might close out, who needed the illusion of being seen. He seemed no less in love with the place than he had in daylight, but at night the love wore a ledger's face.

Joey understood then, with a clarity he did not enjoy, that Duke had not invited him around the machinery. He had invited him into it.

The bartender tapped the legal pad with one finger and pointed stageward.

"Woodsman."

No introduction. No practiced host voice lending importance to the name. No mythology.

Just Woodsman, you're next.

He set the beer down half finished and took the wooden guitar up with him. On the bench it had never felt this heavy.

The microphone was a little too high. He adjusted it, then adjusted it again because his hands needed a task. Somebody at the bar set down a glass hard enough to ring. A burst of laughter came off the front table and crossed the stage diagonally, as physical as a draft.

He hit the first chord too hard.

The dangerous thing was that the room carried it perfectly.

The note went up clean, touched the beams, came back to him with more body in it than he had put out. Honest bones, Duke had said. Honest enough to tell him at once that he had started in attack mode, as if volume were authority and urgency could pass for command.

He chose one of the dependable father songs, a tune that usually caught at least one passerby by the second line if the weather had not beaten all softness out of them. In the park he sometimes played it a little faster than his father had, not enough to insult the original, only enough to catch the moving world before it moved on. Some dumb private part of him still believed the old song ought to arrive carrying a little inherited authority with it. Not enough to win the room. Enough to keep from getting ignored by a table eating wings.

That was what his hands did now.

Too much, too soon.

The rhythm got out in front of his breathing. His right wrist set a pace his voice had to chase. By the second verse he was no longer inside the song; he was hauling it. The room told him so in a hundred little refusals. A woman at the bar looked up on the first line and was back in her drink by the third. Somebody near the back tapped time for two measures and then stopped because the song had not made the case. A server paused with a tray balanced on one palm, listened just long enough to confirm he was not yet the problem at the table she was headed toward.

The front table kept talking.

Not even louder than before. That would have given him something clean to resent. They simply maintained themselves, which was worse. Their sentences ran beneath his chorus as if his voice were part of the room's ventilation.

He reached the end of the song and got what the Landmark gave people who had technically finished.

Three claps from somewhere near the jukebox.

A polite patter from the bar.

One nod from a man who looked as though he applauded every song out of civic hygiene.

That was all.

Joey smiled because singers smiled when they were not sure what had just happened and did not want a room to watch the question arrive on their face. He tightened the strap on his shoulder, leaned toward the microphone, and called the next tune before the silence could finish turning into judgment.

Wrong move.

The second song was bigger, or was supposed to be. Louder chorus. More right-hand drive. Another inherited number that had done clean useful work in open air where momentum counted as intimacy if a stranger only had thirty seconds to decide whether to stop.

He attacked it like momentum could still save him.

For eight bars he almost believed in it. The wooden guitar barked. His boot found the boards. The monitor fed him enough of his own voice that he could briefly mistake volume in his ears for presence in the room.

Then the front table adjusted.

Not maliciously. By instinct.

The lanyard woman leaned in and raised her voice just enough to get back on top of the sound coming at her.

"I said if he wants synergy he can marry it."

The Braves-cap man nearly knocked his beer over laughing. The server arrived with another basket of wings, set it down during Joey's chorus, and said, "Y'all still good on ranch?"

All four answered at once.

The room took their side without having to choose.

That was the part the park had never taught him. Open air was neutral until you won a person over or lost them. A room came preoccupied. With cutlery, bar tabs, office gossip, date-night fatigue, somebody's half-finished grievance, somebody else's phone lighting up with bad news from home. He had spent years learning how to throw sound into motion and snag a stranger before life closed over them again. None of that helped when the competition came with lemon-pepper grease on its fingers and the full blessing of the house.

He strummed harder.

They talked harder.

It became a war nobody at the table knew they were in and nobody on the floor felt obligated to referee. The stage lights warmed the sweat under his shirt. The wood under his hand grew slick. He heard the room's acoustics doing him the backhanded favor of making every overhit chord bright enough to expose itself.

Halfway through the second chorus he made the mistake of looking at Duke.

Duke watched from the corner table with a pen in his hand.

That was all.

No rescuing gesture. No public hush. No host's announcement that this young man ought to be heard because history had touched his surname or because the room's bones had spoken to him in daylight. Duke had sold the door, not what happened once Joey walked through it.

He finished the song because songs ended whether dignity was participating or not.

The applause afterward was thinner than the first round. The kind that might have been for completion rather than effect.

He could have stopped there.

Later he would know that stopping there would probably have been the least damaging choice available.

Instead he went where he always went when he was cornered and could feel himself losing: backward.

Another father song. Softer this time, or meant to be. He wanted gravity now. Restraint. He wanted to show the room he had some chamber other than force. He wanted, though he would not have said it out loud, to make his father matter in the building after all.

His thumb found the worn place near the sound hole as he started, and a small kitchen memory flashed up from the wood itself: his father playing low while dishes clinked in the sink, not trying to beat the room, only laying the song down flat and letting the room decide whether it needed it. The memory was no help. It only made Joey feel how far he was from that kind of ease.

By the first held note his throat betrayed him.

It was not a dramatic crack. Nobody laughed. Nobody turned. It was worse than that. The note frayed in the middle, went papery, then came back thinner, and because the room carried sound honestly the damage came back to him clear as if somebody else had leaned in and done it for him.

The next line arrived already guarded. He started protecting the voice instead of using it. The song, which he had played often enough that his hands could have found it in a fever, suddenly felt overhandled. Borrowed. He could hear the use on it. Could hear that the room could hear the use too.

Under his fingers, humiliatingly, the hidden fragment stirred.

Not even a full phrase. Just the shape of one. The same rough pressure side B had left waiting in the apartment, now surfacing under the chord change as something that was his and therefore intolerable in that moment.

He killed it at once.

He was not going to be first-time honest under amber lights in front of people ordering condiments.

So he limped the rest of the song home on technique, memory, and one remaining strip of pride.

When it ended, the room did not punish him.

It did something worse.

It resumed.

The bartender was already reaching for the aux cord. House music slid in under Joey's last movement off the stool. The woman at the front table said, "No, listen, this is the part where human resources suggested mindfulness," and the other three turned toward her with the relieved concentration of people returning to the thing they had actually come out for.

Joey got the guitar back in the case without fumbling, which felt like the last available form of professionalism.

He looked once toward Duke's table.

Duke met the glance. He did not nod. He did not harden either. He only looked at Joey with the calm of a man who had watched exactly what he needed to watch and saw no use in decorating it.

Joey went to the bar.

The beer sat where he had left it, warm now, foam dried to the inside of the bottle in a pale ring. He pushed it aside and said, "Cheapest thing you've got."

The bartender poured him a well bourbon the color of old varnish.

Joey paid five dollars, tip folded into the bill this time because he no longer felt ceremonial about anything. Ten dollars total for finding out the room had not been waiting on him. Pike would not have cared what education he had bought with it.

He held the bourbon in both hands for a moment before drinking.

It tasted like pennies and a threat.

His phone had no messages.

The park at least let failure disperse into weather. In here it sat under lights while the room kept eating around it. So this was Duke's creed in practice. The room didn't lie. It just told the truth in public and left you standing in it.

"Hey," the bartender said, wiping the counter in front of an empty stool. "You want some advice?"

Joey took another swallow first.

"Not especially."

"Fair." The bartender folded the rag once. "You were chasing the room."

Joey stared into the whiskey.

"That obvious?"

"Only if you've watched it happen a thousand times." The bartender nodded toward the stage without turning his head. "You got up there like you were still outdoors. Out there you got to catch somebody before they keep walking. In here they're already seated. Whole different animal."

Joey said nothing.

The bartender kept going, correctly reading the silence as permission.

"Room's a dog. You chase it, it runs. You sit still long enough, it wanders over and decides maybe you belong in the yard."

Joey looked at him despite himself.

"That is annoyingly good."

"I know. Hate when I sound wise on company time." He set a glass under the tap, filled it, handed it off to a server without looking, and came back to Joey. "First song, you started too hot. Room told you that and you argued. Second song, that table kept talking and you decided volume could shame them into attention. It can't. They paid to be dumb in public."

Joey gave a short laugh through his nose.

"And the third one?"

"Third one you tried to make us forgive the first two."

That landed lower than he wanted it to.

The bartender saw it land and did not soften.

"You don't beat cutlery. You don't beat office gossip. You sure don't beat ranch. You give the song more space than they think it's going to get. Slower than your hands want. Quieter than your nerves like. Let them hear what they're stepping on."

Joey rolled the glass between his palms.

"So I was bad."

"You were new." The bartender shrugged. "Bad's louder."

Joey looked down the bar. Somebody was feeding the jukebox. Somebody else was arguing gently about whether to close out now or after one more round. The front table was still down there beneath the stage, not mean, not guilty, simply continuing with the night they had bought. He hated how much sense the bartender made.

"What's the worst you've seen?" he asked.

The bartender's face improved immediately.

"Guy brought a tuba once."

Joey looked up.

"A tuba."

"Open mic. Pure civilian chaos. Not even showcase week, just one of those nights where the city decides to test whether God has quit. He comes in carrying this thing like he's reporting for military duty. Gets onstage. Plays the national anthem."

"That's not that long."

"All the verses."

Joey blinked. "There are verses?"

"There are if your evening is cursed." The bartender leaned both forearms on the bar now, pleased with the memory. "Nobody in here knew there were verses. Learned the hard way. By verse two people had that look like maybe freedom itself had become a hostage situation."

Against his will, Joey smiled.

"That does make me feel a little better."

"That's what the tuba story is for." The bartender straightened. "Not to comfort you. To remind you there are deeper holes than whatever happened to you ten minutes ago."

Joey finished the bourbon. It did not improve anything. It only drew a darker line around the edges of the embarrassment.

The bartender took the empty glass.

"Come back next week," he said. "And next time don't get up there like you're asking permission to exist while also trying to whip the room into granting it. Pick one lie."

Joey stood with the guitar case in hand.

"That one was meaner."

"Mean's free on Thursdays."

That got another unwilling laugh out of him. Small. Enough to hurt.

He stepped back from the bar and looked once more toward the stage. Some other poor bastard was up there now, already halfway through a song. The front table kept talking, though one of them had lowered their voice without seeming to notice it. Duke still sat at the corner table with his pen in hand, alive to the whole machine.

Nothing in the room suggested Joey's set had mattered.

That stayed in him harder than the crack in his voice. The room had not thrown him out or booed him off. It had done the meaner thing and reduced him to scale.

He pushed through the front door into the night.

Outside, Atlanta had gone from punishing heat to tired heat. Cars hissed over old asphalt. Somebody laughed across the street. A bus exhaled at the light and pulled away. Music leaked out of the Landmark every time the door opened behind him and got cut off every time it shut, as if the building were undecided about what to confess to the sidewalk.

He stood there for a moment with the guitar case in one hand, the room's stale cold still trapped in his shirt.

Then he started walking home with the wooden guitar on his back and the bartender's tuba story stuck in his head, stupid enough to repeat and sharp enough to keep. Under the joke sat the worse part, already refusing to shut up: slower than your hands want, quieter than your nerves like. He hated the room for being right before he knew how to use it.
