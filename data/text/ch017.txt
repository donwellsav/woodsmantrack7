Cass

On Friday night he went back to the Landmark without the wooden guitar.

That alone made him feel like he had left a hand at home.

Not the whole hand. Just the thinking half of it. The callused part that knew where to settle when he got uncertain, the old wood-shaped intelligence that usually sat between him and the world and kept him from feeling so skinned by it. Without the case on his shoulder, he felt too much of himself moving through Atlanta at once. Shirt sticking between his shoulder blades. Wallet light in his back pocket. Keys ticking his thigh. No instrument there to explain him if somebody looked too long.

Pike's notice sat on the apartment table with six days left before filing. The silver case under the bed kept its clean little shine. The pawn-shop envelope stayed hidden in the kitchen drawer. Joey left the wooden guitar by the chair anyway.

He told himself he was going for the acoustics.

That was not a full lie. After the second Thursday, after Duke's tapping pen and the on-the-house bourbon and the way the old man had used his father like a house key he already knew would fit, Joey wanted to hear the room from the far end for once. Not the stage. Not his own pulse. The room. He wanted to know what it sounded like when it was not busy deciding whether he belonged inside it. He wanted to know whether the shift he had felt Thursday had been real or just the kind of borrowed mercy a tired man could mistake for progress.

He also wanted to hear Cass.

He kept calling that acoustics so he would not have to call it wanting. He had heard her name before he saw her. Heard it in the way patrons asked after her with proprietary disappointment, as if the room owed them her face and not just her set. Heard it in the bartender's tone. In Duke's timing. In the way Cass seemed to function inside the Landmark as both promise and answer. Joey had come prepared to dislike whatever musician the room had already decided to love. Safer that way. Easier to keep his categories standing up.

Little Five Points on a Friday carried money louder than Thursday did. More car doors slamming. More cologne pitched into heat already crowded with fryer grease, cigarette smoke, and the damp brick smell left by a storm that had threatened all afternoon and never broken. More people walking like they had paid for a certain kind of night and meant to collect it. Joey walked from the apartment because spending bus fare to watch somebody else work felt too close to self-harm. By the time he crossed Moreland, his shirt had gone tacky down the spine and the city was still humming in his ears through brakes, motorcycles, a burst of bass from a car idling wrong at the light, and a street-corner sax player trying to make "Ain't No Sunshine" cover rent one bent note at a time.

The Landmark door gave under his hand with its usual sticky reluctance, like even the entrance believed in thresholds.

Inside, Friday sounded different from Thursday. Thursday was hungry in public. Friday came arranged in advance. More date-night chairs. More reserved tables. More people who had already decided the parking headache, the cover, and the traffic on Euclid were going to become a story worth telling tomorrow. The room still smelled of old wood, fryer grease, bleach, and beer gone warm at the edges, but Friday carried a little money in the air with it, and money changed the way a room listened. Not better. Just touchier.

The bartender looked up when Joey came in empty-shouldered.

"No guitar tonight?"

"Just ears," Joey said.

"Those cost four dollars too."

Joey laid four singles on the bar and took the cheap beer. Four dollars gone with Pike six days out. The bottle started sweating in his hand almost at once. Cold glass, hot room. For one quick second it brought up the jelly jars his mother used for water in August, wet rings on the oilcloth while his father tuned in the next room and pretended not to be listening for whether there was enough food in the kitchen to make the week feel less breakable. Then the memory shut again. Objects did that when he was tired. Handed him an old room. Took it back before he could step all the way in.

He carried the beer to the back wall and leaned there beside the old poster case, where the glass had been cracked so long it counted as architecture. From that end you got the PA and the room together. Front-row people liked to think closeness was a kind of authority. Mostly it got them speaker. The back gave you the whole lie and the whole truth in the same mouthful.

The poster case held three faded flyers and a yellowing house-rules sheet curled at the corners. One flyer showed a singer smiling over an acoustic guitar with all the determined sincerity of a man whose merch table probably sold incense. Joey only had to catch the cheap gloss to think of the stack of old gig handbills still living in one of his father's record crates, dates and venues gone half brown with time, each one proof that a night could matter deeply and still fit in a box no bigger than laundry.

Duke moved through the room wearing Friday's version of his warmth. More host than Thursday manager, but only because Friday paid better for ceremony. He touched shoulders, said names, laughed with his head tipped back just far enough to read generous from a distance, then sent the same counting look over the tables he always did, taking inventory of glasses, bodies, expectations, and what could still be turned into more of all three.

Joey stayed where he was and let the room explain itself.

A singer with too much hat and not enough song went first. Competent. Dead on arrival. The room let him happen to it politely. Forks kept moving. A woman in green at the front talked through the second verse without lowering her voice. The bartender polished the same glass all the way through the chorus. When the man reached for a big note, the room did not refuse him exactly. It just declined to reorganize around his need.

The duo after that did harmonies so clean they sounded laminated. Not bad. Worse than bad, maybe. Safe in a way that made every line arrive preforgiven. Joey listened harder than he would have a month earlier. Hard enough now to hear not just failure or success but mechanism. Where a pause was fear instead of timing. Where a chorus had been built to sound bigger than the bodies inside it could honestly carry. Where the room, out of old manners, gave back encouragement it had not actually felt.

It was humiliating how much easier he had become to teach.

He tapped two fingers against the beer bottle in a pattern that did not belong to the song onstage. The hidden fragment, or part of it. The same rough little seam side B had left in him. The bottle rang thin and private under his knuckles. He stopped at once and wrapped both hands around the glass as if he were only cooling them there.

Then Duke went to the microphone himself.

He did not make a speech. He did not need one. He said her first name the way some men said headliner and some men said proof. The room changed around it. Not all at once. Enough. Two women near the bar put their phones down. A man halfway to the bathroom turned back before he reached the hall. Even the waitress coming out with a tray of fries eased her shoulder angle, giving the room time to look where it had already meant to look.

Cass walked out carrying a dark guitar with the strap already set to working height. Black boots. Black jeans. A sleeveless top the color of stormwater where the light hit it. Silver hoops. Eyeliner sharp enough to read from the back wall. Not dressed for innocence. Not dressed to apologize for not performing it either.

The first thing Joey noticed was that she did not come out hungry.

Most people at the Landmark brought their need onstage and tried to make confidence wear it like cologne. Cass had put hers somewhere the room could not smell it. She came out looking less like somebody asking to be chosen than somebody already occupied with the job. Not arrogant. More exact than that. Every move calibrated enough to pass for instinct. She adjusted the mic once, not twice. Set her water glass on the amp without looking. The ring it left flashed pale in the stage light and vanished.

She gave the crowd a smile that read easy until you watched long enough to see how little of her it actually gave away.

Useful, Joey thought.

Armored, a beat later.

Then she started playing, and the judgment had to find a new job.

The first song came out bright and clipped, pop-shaped with something mean folded small inside it. Not loud. She did not need loud. She set the voice in the mic with the kind of control Joey had only just begun to understand in himself, except hers was farther along and made to look easy. The first verse rode just ahead of the beat, enough to keep the line tight without advertising the choice. Then she laid the chorus back half a breath, and the room felt it settle. Not because she told it to. Because she gave it a place to land.

The cracked glass in the poster case behind Joey answered certain low notes with a dry little buzz. The ice bin at the bar stopped clattering for the length of a phrase. A front table that had spent the earlier sets talking over everything went still one chair at a time, like attention moving through furniture. Cass did not chase any of it. She acted as if the room's turn toward her were the least surprising thing in the building, which made it easier for people to keep giving it.

The crowd turned quicker for her than it had for him. That stung for about three seconds, then quit mattering because the real thing was in the details.

She knew exactly how much brightness this room could take before bright turned decorative. She kept the guitar one shade darker than Friday would have rewarded. Left a little scrape in the pick attack instead of sanding it into shine. A singer trying only to please would have opened the sound wider and cleaner. Cass kept a burr in it, a lived edge, as if she needed the song to remember a body was making it. There was none of the silver guitar's showroom cleanliness in that sound, and none of the wooden guitar's inherited wear either. It felt chosen, then worked on until choice became muscle.

A laugh landed at one of her between-song lines from the front table, exactly where it was supposed to. Joey had the immediate unpleasant sense that she had known where that laugh would come before she gave them the sentence.

That should have made her seem fake. It did not. It made her seem ready.

By the second song the room was no longer deciding whether to listen. It was listening. Cass did not woo them into it. She let the attention sit on her without shrinking from it or leaning into it. Joey hated how well that worked. Respect got there first, though, and hate missed its cue.

The second song was slower, harder in the bones than the first, and that was where he heard the real intelligence under the casing. She phrased like somebody who understood restraint was not the opposite of feeling. It was how you delivered feeling without bruising it to death. She gave one line less than a bigger singer would have, and it hit harder because she trusted the room to cross the last inch by itself. Not the whole room. Never the whole room. But enough of it to matter.

He found himself watching her right hand.

Not because it was flashy. Because it was economical. No big sincerity strums for the cheap seats. No flourishes thrown in to reassure the back tables she was definitely Doing Music now. When she wanted the sound to widen, she changed angle and pressure instead of getting louder. When she wanted a phrase to bite, she let the pick drag half a second across the wound of the string and moved on before the effect could harden into trick. She was thinking at the instrument all the time. Choosing. Revising. Protecting certain edges. Offering others up.

He had come in carrying one of his stupid clean sets of bins. Park and room. Truth and hustle. Real and arranged. Cass stepped onstage and made them all look homemade. Nothing in her set was innocent of the room, but none of it was passive either. She was making decisions inside the songs every few bars, and the worst part was how quickly he recognized the labor. Part of what he loved about inherited material was that the hardest decisions had already been survived by dead people.

The third song started gentler than the first two, almost plain. For a few measures he thought maybe she had saved the most ordinary thing for last on purpose, something that would let the room out easy and leave everybody feeling tasteful about themselves.

Then the bridge arrived.

He heard it before he quite trusted that he had heard it.

Any sensible version of that song would have lifted clean there. Whole step. Little flare of triumph. Chorus coming back with its dress ironed. Cass took it up a minor third instead. Just enough darkness smuggled into the rise to change the whole feel of the song without making the move flashy. The song did not get bigger. It got riskier. It put a bruise under the shine and trusted the room to feel the bruise even if it could not have named the note that made it.

The response in the room was small and total. A man at the bar who had been looking at his phone looked up as if somebody had said his name. The waitress with the fries shifted her tray to her hip and forgot to keep walking for half a second. The cracked poster glass buzzed once and then went quiet. Nobody in the room, as far as Joey could tell, caught the move in the language his ears caught it in. They just felt the floor tilt under the song and took the feeling as mood.

Joey said "goddamn" to his beer before he remembered not to do it out loud.

The woman beside him glanced over, then back to the stage. Cass, up in the lights, had already moved into the last chorus as if she had not done anything worth remarking on.

That one move ruined Joey's easy version of her. Whatever else the room had made of Cass, she had chosen risk at the exact place tidy would have paid better.

The song ended. The applause was immediate and deserved. Not all friend noise. Not Duke noise either. Real applause from people who had been given something and knew it. Even the room's regular cynics had lost a little posture around the mouth.

Cass stood in it exactly long enough, nodded once, and came offstage without milking a drop of it.

Joey looked down at his bottle too late.

He had the ridiculous hope that if he kept his face occupied with cheap beer, she would not realize he had been listening at the level of bridge substitutions and right-hand pressure. Instead he had apparently chosen the exact kind of not-looking that made him easier to spot.

He heard her before he saw her. Boot heels on old floorboards. The soft tick of a pick against the neck of the bottle she set down at the bar. Then her voice from just to his left.

"You were in the back."

He looked over.

Up close, with the stage lights off her and the room not needing anything from her for one minute, the persona did not vanish. It loosened. The smile was smaller now, less uniformly useful. Tiredness showed at the corners of her eyes under the makeup. So did intelligence. So did the fact that she had already clocked him and wanted the answer before he had time to invent a nicer one.

"Good acoustics in the back," he said. "Everybody fights for the front like closeness is a moral achievement. But up there you're inside the PA and half the room's own noise. Back here you get the mix. Hear it the way the room hears it."

She raised one eyebrow.

"Most people try to get closer."

"Most people are wrong."

The corner of her mouth moved. Not a smile yet. More like she had found the edge of one and decided not to spend it.

"Strong opening philosophy," she said.

"I only use it on matters of sound and municipal corruption."

Now the smile showed for real, small and unwilling. It changed her face just enough to make the tiredness under it easier to see.

She waited.

Joey knew enough to understand what he had been handed. Stay cute. Or say the actual thing.

"Minor third on the bridge of the last song," he said. "Was that planned?"

She blinked once.

That was all. But it told him what he needed. Nobody usually asked her that in this room.

"Who are you?" she said.

"Joey Woodsman. Thursday list, currently. Folk guitar. Or front-porch music, if you had asked my father, though he also wore cargo shorts to a funeral, so I don't consider all his branding advice binding law."

The laugh got out of her before she could stop it.

It surprised both of them a little. Joey could see that in the quick glance she gave her own bottle afterward, like maybe the sound had come from the glass. He heard himself doing what he always did when grief came too close in company. Turning it sideways. Giving it an ugly practical detail so nobody had to look straight at the body.

"He passed a few years back," he added, because leaving it at the joke suddenly felt cheap.

The quickness left her face.

"I'm sorry."

She did not lay anything warm over the sentence. No soft head tilt. No room voice. Just the plain thing said plainly.

"Yeah," Joey said. "Me too."

He took a swallow of beer. It had gone warmer while she was onstage. He nodded toward the stage.

"He would've liked that bridge move. He liked anybody willing to make a song less polite than it started."

Cass watched him for a second, and he had the sense she was deciding whether that line had been for her, for his father, or for himself. The answer was yes to all three and not cleanly to any of them.

"Most people don't hear that stuff in a room like this," she said.

"Most people were busy looking at you."

"That helps too."

Flat enough to count as true.

He heard the fatigue in it before the vanity, and that changed something again. Not because it made her innocent. Because it made the room around her look pricier.

"You don't chase the room," Joey said.

"No."

"You don't let it think you need it."

That got a different look from her. Not offended. Measured.

"Need is a bad smell onstage," she said.

The line came out too fast to be theory. Somebody had already paid for that sentence. Cash, sleep, pride, maybe all three.

Joey let out a breath through his nose. "I learned that the furniture way."

"Thursday?"

"Very Thursday."

She smiled again, a little. It made her look younger for half a second and more tired when it went away.

"I heard about that," she said.

"That I'm furniture?"

"That you got better."

He was not prepared for the small clean strike of pleasure it made in him. He disliked that she could hand him that much with six words. He disliked more that he wanted the next six.

"Depends how strict the grading scale is," he said.

"You held the room some."

"Some felt like a miracle."

"Some is what rooms are made of."

That landed in him because it was true and because it sounded like a sentence learned the expensive way. Not slogan. Not wisdom for its own sake. Shop talk from somebody who had been overcharged for the lesson and remembered every dollar.

Behind them, the next act started tuning in public. A low E bloomed through the speakers, came back too hot, and shaved the edge off the room for a second. Joey and Cass both winced almost invisibly. The shared reaction hit him with a small private force. Not intimacy. Recognition. Which, in a room like this, could be worse.

He nodded toward the stage.

"You held it all the way through."

"No," she said. "I held enough of it."

"That sounds like a distinction only musicians and cult leaders make."

"Cult leaders have better merch."

He laughed then, despite himself, and this time the laugh did not have to be hauled out. The room behind them swelled and thinned around the next act loading in. Somebody at the far table knocked silverware onto the floor. Duke's voice rolled up from the front of the room, laughing too warmly at something Joey doubted deserved it. Cass took a sip from her bottle and leaned one shoulder against the wall beside him, not relaxed exactly but off duty enough to fake it.

"What kind of folk?" she asked.

It was not dismissive. That made him answer differently than he might have.

"Old stuff, mostly," he said. "Appalachian bones by way of Georgia. My father's songs, his father's before him, and older things that belong to nobody living enough to argue. Hymns, murder songs, drift songs. Stuff that survives because people keep carrying it after the world quits making room."

"And that's what you play on Thursdays?"

"That's what I play everywhere."

"Brave."

"Or lazy."

"Those aren't the same thing."

"No," Joey said. "But they can share a jacket."

She looked at him the way a person looked at a line that had arrived smarter than expected from the mouth delivering it.

"You write anything of your own?" she asked.

The question was simple enough that it nearly slipped past his defenses before he understood what it was.

His thumb found the raised seam in the beer label and worried it there. Paper edge under skin. Cheap glue. The feel of it put him, against his will, back at the kitchen table over the spiral notebook, strike-throughs marching down the page like failed alibis. The fragment had lived under his hand all week, pressing at him on bus rides, over dishwater, under songs older than the country. Every time that worn seam on side B sat by the turntable in the apartment like a dare, he still had nothing he could say aloud to another musician without sounding fraudulent or precious.

"I come from a long distinguished line of men with poor instincts around self-advertising," he said.

"That a no?"

"That's a depends."

"On what?"

On whether a thing counted as yours before another human heard it. On whether muttering a line into bad apartment air counted as writing or just privately humiliating yourself. On whether making your own song meant honoring the dead or stepping out of rank with them.

Instead he said, "On how strict your definitions are."

Cass took that in without helping him out of it. He liked her a little for that and distrusted it too.

"Most people hear a room like this and think volume," Joey said, because changing subjects was one of the family arts. "You hear where it bends."

She tilted the bottle once toward the stage. "Every room bends somewhere. You learn the give or the give learns you."

"Sounds expensive."

"The tuition is terrible."

That landed like shop talk and hit like a bill of sale. Joey had the sense she had been inside enough rooms to sort them by what they cost rather than what they promised, and that the sorting had left marks she was not interested in showing him yet.

He thought about the minor-third bridge. About the way she had darkened a Friday song at the exact place Friday would have preferred polish. About the way she had made a room full of people feel the bruise without announcing the trick.

"That bridge move was the best thing in your set," he said.

She went still.

"The last song?"

"Yeah."

"Most people mention the hook."

"Most people are wrong."

This time the laugh came softer and stayed longer. Not stage laughter. Not the efficient room sound she had used from the mic. Something narrower. Something that would not have traveled.

The act onstage hit their first chord too hard and the PA spit it back with a square clipped edge. Joey heard Cass hear it too. Her face changed by almost nothing, but the almost nothing was exact.

"You hear too much," she said.

"Occupational defect."

"What's the occupation?"

"Currently? Making four-dollar beers disappear while pretending I understand my own life."

"That pays terrible."

"The advancement track is insulting."

She turned the bottle once in her hands. He noticed then the real work in them. Callus on the string fingers, not cosmetic and not new. A small healing nick by the thumb knuckle, maybe from a string change done too fast. Labor under the rings and clean nails. He had not needed proof, not after the bridge, but seeing the work in her hands made resentment harder to keep tidy.

Cass glanced once toward Duke's corner. Duke was half turned toward a table of men in rolled sleeves, smiling with his full sermon face on. From the stage that smile probably read generous. From the back wall it looked occupied.

When she looked back at Joey, the useful smile was gone again.

"You really heard the bridge," she said.

"Yeah."

"And you still came over here to talk to me anyway."

"I don't know if you've noticed," Joey said, "but my judgment has an intermittent short."

"I noticed."

The next act started telling a story nobody had asked for. Joey and Cass both turned toward the sound and then back at each other wearing the same expression: patient dislike, professionally familiar.

That, more than the laugh, felt like a beginning.

Cass shifted off the wall.

For a second he thought she was going to say something ordinary and room-safe. Good set. See you around. Good luck Thursday. One of the many exits people used when recognition had gotten a little too real and they wanted to get back under cover before it cost them anything.

Instead she looked past him toward the back hallway by the bathrooms, where the light was always dimmer and the truth in bars tended to gather whether anybody wanted it to or not. Then she looked back at him with a face that was no longer performing ease for anybody.

"You want to see something?" she asked.

The room behind her kept moving. Glass clinking. Somebody laughing too loud. A bad chord progression. Duke's warm voice somewhere near the front, busy turning everything into part of the evening.

Joey could not tell if he was being invited deeper or tested.

He could tell it mattered.
