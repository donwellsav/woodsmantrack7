Hallway Truth

He said yes before he had time to sort whether yes was bravery, appetite, or the plain inability to leave a question alone once it had spoken in his direction.

Cass turned without checking if he meant it.

That, more than the invitation, made him follow. Somebody who still needed persuading was still half onstage. This looked more like a person deciding there was one place in the building where a thing could be said straight, then moving before she lost the nerve for it.

He set the beer on the back ledge by the poster case and went after her.

The music followed them halfway down the narrow hall by the bathrooms and then failed to come with them. Not vanished. Flattened. What had been song out front got pressed by cinderblock and old plumbing into bass thud, cymbal hiss, a voice turned nasal through drywall, applause arriving as weather instead of judgment. Behind that wall the Landmark lost most of what it sold people about itself. No stage glow. No old-church charm. No amber bulbs making bourbon and bad choices look historical. Just a buzzing overhead tube, cinderblock painted a tired cream, a yellow mop bucket kicked crooked beside a stack of folded bar towels, and a faded poster for a 2009 show whose bands had either broken up, found jobs, or learned to answer to new names.

The place smelled of bleach, stale beer, damp paper, fryer grease gone cold at the edges, and the wet-rag underside of labor. Somebody had mopped recently, but Friday kept tracking its own dirt back in. Every time the kitchen door shoved open at the far end, a strip of hotter air slid through carrying grill smoke and onion, then snapped shut again. Under all of it sat the humid city itself pressing through the brick. Moreland traffic. A motorcycle taking the corner too proudly. The thin ambulance whine that belonged to Atlanta as faithfully as cicadas in August.

Nowhere to pose.

Joey understood immediately why she had brought him there. Whatever truth people told in bars, they usually told it in the parts customers were not meant to romanticize.

His eyes caught on the 2009 poster. Cheap gloss gone dull. One singer frozen mid-vowel with an arm lifted like the room had already agreed to keep him. Joey thought of the stack of old gig flyers still living in one of his father's record crates, dates and venues browned at the edges, a few marked in his father's blocky pencil, proof a room could go dead and still leave handwriting behind. You could hold a whole vanished little world in one hand. A room. Four names. A cover charge. Then time turned it into attic weather.

Cass leaned back against the wall and let the stage come off her.

Not dramatically. No collapse. No sigh arranged to sound unarranged. More like somebody setting down something useful and too heavy. The easy smile went first. Then the calibrated stillness. What stayed was the woman under it, sharp enough to resent how often tiredness had started making decisions for her.

She looked at him once to make sure he was listening and not merely occupying the same hallway.

"I'm about to tell you something," she said, "and I need you not to do that thing where you get funny and historical and act like you're above being surprised."

The accuracy of it annoyed him because it was accurate.

He opened his mouth anyway.

She lifted one finger.

"See. That."

He shut it.

A laugh from the room hit the wall and thinned out. Whoever was onstage had just landed one of those between-song lines people rewarded out of relief more than delight. Joey felt himself wanting to explain that his humor was not arrogance so much as packing material around a bruise. That was already too much explanation. She would count it as evidence.

"I can try," he said after a beat.

"Try harder."

That almost made him smile. He kept it in. The fluorescent buzz above them had the flat mean persistence of a cheap transformer. Somebody went into the women's room, let a stall door bang, then went quiet.

Cass crossed one boot over the other and looked at the mop bucket instead of him, as if plain objects made plainer witnesses.

"The buy-in goes to Duke," she said. "Not production. Not promotion. Not the house band. Duke."

Joey waited.

He had known that already in the vague animal way you knew when a smile had a cash drawer behind it. Hearing it named still did something mean and clarifying inside him. Not surprise. Weight.

"The scout's real," she said. "The people in the room are real. The stage is real in the sense that wood is wood and a microphone's still a microphone. The competition is the lie."

There it was. Not the outline of it; he'd already half seen that from the floor, in the way Duke spent warmth selectively and certain names got introduced like conclusions. The clean naming of it. The relief in that naming was uglier than he wanted it to be. Lies were easier once they had edges.

"Rigged how?" he asked.

"He picks before the first person pays," she said. "Maybe earlier. He knows the kind of night he wants, the kind of story he wants told about the night, and then he spends the month arranging it so it looks like discovery when it gets to the stage."

Joey kept his eyes on her face.

"Who'd he pick?"

She gave him a look like he'd asked what day came after Thursday.

"Me," she said.

The word sat in the hall with the bleach and the mop bucket and the bad-wire hum and became one more practical object.

"Okay," Joey said.

Cass blinked.

"Okay."

"I'm processing."

"That's your processing face?"

He glanced back at the poster. "No. My processing face is worse."

She let that die on its own.

The poster's corners had curled away from the wall in the heat. Somebody had once taped them down and stopped caring. Four bands, one local festival, a sponsor logo from a beer brand that had probably rebranded twice since. He had the brief absurd thought that one of those people had probably stood in this same hallway thinking they were two right introductions away from not having to think about rent for a month. Thinking the night itself might tip the month for them.

Six days on Pike's paper. Twelve hundred and seven dollars and forty cents in the apartment if he counted cash he was already fighting with in his head. Five hundred of it sat in the kitchen drawer in the pawn-shop envelope, already half converted into Duke's money. Under the bed the silver guitar kept its clean little shine. By the chair the wooden one waited like the only honest object left in his life.

Then the real thought arrived.

"The scout's real."

"Yes."

"He listens."

"Yes."

"And Duke can't make a man's ears hear something they don't."

Cass's mouth moved in something harsher than amusement.

"That's your question?"

"That's my question."

He heard how urgent it sounded only after it came out. He hated that she could hear the soft spot now. Hated more that he could not take it back without turning into one more smartass coward in a hallway.

What he felt then was not wounded fairness. Not exactly. He had never been enough of an idiot to think a room like Duke's ran on merit and candlelight. Doors cost what they cost. Men like Duke existed because wanting in was older than shame. Joey had known that from the first afternoon he saw the ledger and the smile attached to it.

What scared him was the deeper thing underneath her sentence.

If the whole arrangement was fake all the way down, then the Landmark stopped being crooked and turned hollow. The last two Thursdays became choreography and permission, nothing more. And one more place where his father might have mattered went dark with it.

Clarence's shelf had proved the city remembered him in at least one real room. Joey could live with manipulation. He did not know what to do with total fraud.

A bar towel on the stack had one corner hanging loose. White once, now grayed by too many washings. Seeing it pulled a memory up so fast it felt borrowed: his mother twisting out a dish towel after men from church had come through the apartment kitchen one August night and turned the place, for forty minutes, into somewhere larger than it was. Coffee rings on the oilcloth. His father laughing low. Somebody harmonizing the wrong line and everybody politely not noticing. Then the last cup rinsed, the table wiped, the towels folded, and the room shrinking obediently back to a kitchen where rent still existed. That was what labor did to music. Turned it back into a room again. The towel in the hallway looked like the same truth after a hundred washings.

"I don't care that the competition's fake," he said.

Cass looked at him as if the mop bucket had just taken a political position.

"You don't care."

"Not the way you want me to."

"That's usually not a promising sentence."

"You told me the scout's real. The room is real enough for a song to land in it. People still hear what they hear, right?"

"Sometimes."

"That's enough."

She pushed off the wall.

"No," she said. "It isn't."

"It is to me."

"Jesus." She looked at him the way some people looked at open flame in dry woods: half disbelief, half unwilling respect. "You really are one of those."

"One of what?"

"People who think the song wins if it's true enough."

The first heat rose in him then, not because she was wrong but because she was standing too close to something he had spent years holding together with bad jokes, inherited repertoire, and the family habit of calling faith practicality if you said it with the right shrug.

"The song doesn't win anything," he said. "It just gets heard or it doesn't."

"And you think that makes the system incidental."

"I think it makes the system secondary."

"That's a luxury opinion."

That landed where she meant it to. Joey almost snapped back and didn't. Luxury was not a word that had spent much time in his life unless it was attached to apartments on the wrong side of Atlanta or menu prices designed to shame him before he ordered. But he knew what she meant. Belief could be class blindness if you used it to ignore who had paid in blood, sleep, cash, knees, pride.

He thought of Pike in the hall outside his apartment, one hand on the frame, giving exact legal days in a voice so bored it circled back into mercy. Luxury. Maybe belief did look like that from outside: a broke man defending the invisible while the visible clock kept moving.

Still.

"He can stack the lineup," Joey said. "He can smile people into paying him for the privilege of losing. He can decide what story he wants the month to tell about itself. He can't force a song into somebody's body once it leaves the speaker."

"No," Cass said. "He just decides which songs get put near the bodies that matter."

The room noise swelled beyond the wall, then thinned again. Someone called for another round. Glass knocked glass. The act onstage hit a line too hard and the microphone ironed it into brightness. It all sounded farther away now, like weather heard from a utility closet.

Cass rubbed one thumb along the seam of her bottle and went on.

"You know what happens to musicians who believe exactly what you're saying?" she asked.

Joey said nothing.

"Some quit before thirty and get jobs they can stand in without hating themselves too loud. Insurance. Dental offices. Whatever." She kept her eyes on the label seam, picking at it with her thumbnail. "Some keep waiting for the room to reward purity and end up old and broke and talking about almosts. Some make a deal in a hallway like this because tiredness gets there before principle does."

She said it flat, without asking pity for it. That did more damage than self-defense would have. Joey knew people who could tell a wound in a way that already contained the request to admire them for having one. This was not that. This was receipts.

"What kind of deal?" he asked.

She looked directly at him.

"The kind where a man sees you at a mic night and knows how hungry you are before you open your mouth," she said. "The kind where he buys you dinner somewhere you wouldn't have picked because the menu doesn't have prices you can laugh off. The kind where he talks about the room like it's church and destiny and your one chance to stop dragging an amp up stairs for eight people, six of them other musicians waiting their turn."

Joey heard Buckhead in that without her saying Buckhead. Heard valet stands and polished brick. Heard long drives back across town with tips folded in your shoe because your bag wouldn't zip right. Heard the particular Atlanta shame of carrying plates or gear into places whose monthly floral budget could have covered your rent.

"And then?" he asked.

"And then he drives you home," she said. "Or to his place. Or to whatever version of home gets described warmly enough that you can pretend you're still choosing cleanly." She took a breath and let it out through her nose. "Nothing he did counted as force. That's part of why it's hard to tell straight. He was kind. He was patient. He had a shower that worked and a bed that wasn't the back seat of a Honda Civic and eggs in the morning and a way of making all of it sound like generosity instead of leverage."

The eggs got him. Not the car. Not the bed. The eggs.

He saw, stupidly vivid, the chrome lip of a diner steam tray, yellow folded squares going dry under heat lamps. Saw his mother cracking two into a chipped bowl on Sunday mornings when there was a little extra money or company to justify it. Saw how little food it could take to make a person feel chosen by a room that wasn't built for them. That was what Duke understood. Not just sex. Not just ambition. Ordinary relief.

Joey stayed still.

He could feel two reactions trying to climb onto his face at once: anger on her behalf and the instinct to protect her from seeing that anger turn into the wrong kind. Men got protective when what they really wanted was permission to hate another man cleanly. Cass did not need that from him. It would have been one more way of turning her sentence into his own virtue.

"You were sleeping in your car," he said.

"Three nights a week, on the bad stretch," she said. "Four if the week had opinions."

Her voice did not change. That was what gave it weight. No tears. No theatrics. Just arithmetic.

"Waiting tables in Buckhead," she went on. "Brunch shifts with twelve-dollar mimosas for women who called me sweetheart and tipped like it offended them to be seen doing better. Open mics where the audience was seven musicians and one bartender wiping the same glass for two hours. You know what that does after a while? It doesn't make you evil. It makes you tired."

A truck hit a pothole outside and sent a small dust-shiver through the back wall. Joey looked down at the yellow lip of the mop bucket, the dirty water line ringed around the inside. That was the thing about tiredness. How untheatrical it was. It did not announce itself as crisis. It just lowered the price of what you would call acceptable until yesterday's line looked decorative.

He nodded once.

"Yeah," he said quietly. "I know that math."

"Then you know what tired does to principle." She shifted again, shoulders against cinderblock now. "It doesn't kill it clean. That's the fantasy. People like to imagine a moment where you either hold the line or sell your soul and then the credits roll. That's not how it works. It's repetition. One more night. One more bill. One more week of telling yourself you can do one ugly thing to avoid ten uglier ones. Then you're living inside the one ugly thing and calling it strategy because the alternative is calling yourself hungry and used."

Joey looked back at the poster. Somebody on it had once thought side bangs were an argument. Another man had his hand around a mic stand like he meant to pull a future closer. The whole flyer had the exhausted optimism of a year when people still printed enough copies to leave them in coffee shops. He wondered if any of those bands had been good. Wondered if good had helped.

"So Duke picked you," he said.

"Duke picked the version of the story where I win," she said. "Because it flatters his taste. Because it makes the month look curated instead of desperate. Because once I'm in, other people pay to stand near the chance of getting in next. He gets my talent. The room gets a night it can brag about. The scout gets a precleaned table of options. And everybody keeps pretending the whole thing happened organically."

"And you get what?"

That made her laugh once, with no happiness in it.

"A stage," she said. "A regular slot. Somebody in the room who might matter. Rent, some months. A shower. A little less panic. A little less living out of the trunk of a car." Then, because apparently honesty had started and was not done with them, she added, "And a bill I still don't know how to total up."

There was no point asking whether she regretted it cleanly. Nothing in it was clean. Regret was another luxury word when the alternative had been not eating or not bathing or sleeping half folded.

He felt his father's principle under all of it, not as words but as pressure: a lousy room did not automatically make the note false.

"Your music's still real," Joey said.

He did not mean clean. Clean was for people who had only ever had to barter with opinion.

Cass's eyes narrowed a fraction.

"Is it."

He did not answer quickly. He let himself hear her set again instead of the idea of her. Not what Duke had arranged around it. What she had done inside it.

When he spoke, his voice came out cleaner than he meant it to, stripped of the usual upholstery.

"Yes," he said. "I heard you. Whatever Duke arranged, that bridge was yours."

She looked at him hard enough that he thought he had overstepped, then kept looking after the overstep had already happened.

"God," she said softly. "You talk about him enough I can hear the shape of him in you."

The line landed low.

He could have joked. Cargo shorts sat right there wanting work. He let it sit. The hallway seemed to lean inward around the sentence. Even the room beyond the wall thinned for a second, as if the building had taken half a breath and held it.

"If I give this up," he said, "then he becomes a fool to me."

Cass said nothing.

So he kept going, because the sentence had already broken skin and stopping halfway would not save anything.

"Not just him. My grandfather too. All of them. Every man who handed a song forward because he thought it still mattered even when the room got it wrong or the money did. If I decide Duke's setup means the hearing isn't real when it happens, then I lose the last decent argument I've got for why any of it was worth what it cost them."

The hallway went still around that.

He heard, absurdly, something drip inside the mop bucket. Heard a stall door bang in the women's room and swing nearly shut again. Heard the next act bleeding through the wall in a key it did not deserve, the singer pushing where the song needed a secret instead.

Cass's face changed, not toward softness exactly. Toward recognition.

"You think belief protects the dead," she said.

"Maybe it does."

"Maybe it just keeps the living standing in bad rooms longer than they should."

"That's possible too."

"And you stay anyway."

"Yeah."

She lowered her eyes to the bottle in her hand. Under the fluorescent light the glass looked medicinal.

"That's the trap," she said. "Not that the room is fake. If it were fake all the way through, people would leave. The trap is that real things still happen in crooked places. A real note lands. A real person hears it. Somebody important looks up for the right reason instead of the arranged one. That's what keeps everybody from running."

That was closer to the answer he had been looking for than anything else she could have said.

Not absolution. Worse. Hope with rot in it.

He felt something in him settle and sour at the same time. Not because the answer comforted him. Because it justified his worst instinct, which was to stay and call it discernment.

"So the hearing exists," he said.

"Sometimes." She looked up. "Sometimes enough to ruin your life around it."

He almost smiled at that and didn't. Too near true.

A burst of applause came through the wall then, heavier than the joke-laughs from earlier. Joey listened before he could stop himself. Two quick claps from the front tables, a delayed broader wave from the back, silverware pausing, then bar noise resuming too soon. Polite enough. Not revelation. His mind took inventory automatically now. The room had been training itself into him.

"You can hear that through a wall?" Cass asked.

He looked at her.

"The difference?"

"You listened."

"Yeah."

"What was it."

He could have shrugged. Instead he said, "They liked the finish better than the song."

One corner of her mouth moved.

"You're insufferable."

"Only on musical topics."

"No." The corner nearly became a smile and thought better of it. "I'm pretty sure it's broader."

He let that sit. The smallness of it helped. It kept the conversation from tipping all the way into sermon or wound display. Two people could sometimes stay honest longer if one of them kept handing the other something with a handle on it.

"You should be careful with Duke," she said.

"You already told me he's selling something."

"I'm not talking about the sell." Cass shifted the bottle from one hand to the other. "I'm talking about the warmth. He makes you feel chosen before he makes you feel charged for it. Makes you feel seen, or fathered, or folded in. By the time you realize every kindness had a receipt attached, you can't tell which parts you owe for anymore."

Thursday came back in the shape of Duke sliding the bourbon across, low light in the glass, the Woodsman name laid on him like a hand he had not asked for. He hated that she had sharpened that memory instead of replacing it. Hated more that his body had liked the warmth before his brain started counting.

"Yeah," he said.

"Yeah."

They stood there for a breath with the truth of him hearing her and her hearing him and neither of them being rescued by it.

Then Joey asked the wrong question because he was still Joey and still had to touch the exposed wire one more time.

"How'd you get your slot, exactly?"

Cass held his gaze.

Her expression did not harden. It simplified. No stage face. No special injury. Just a door closing where he had not earned the key.

She did not answer.

She did not need to.

The answer was already in the hall with them: bed, shower, breakfast, regular stage time, a man who believed in the room enough to weaponize access to it. Agency still counted. So did coercion wearing manners.

Joey nodded once.

"Right."

He pushed off the wall. Nothing left in the hallway was going to come out cleaner if they kept rubbing at it.

Then he stopped and looked back at her.

"Keep the minor third on the bridge," he said. "It's the best thing in your set."

Something moved in her face and checked itself before it could turn visible enough to name.

Maybe gratitude. Maybe anger that he had heard it. Maybe just relief at being spoken to about the music again instead of the terms under it. He did not know. He only knew the sentence had pulled them back to the one part of the room Duke did not fully own.

"That's the only part Duke didn't arrange," Joey said.

He left first because one of them had to.

The room hit him in layers when he stepped back out. Glass. Laughter. Fryer hiss. A voice onstage trying too hard to sound like it had never tried at all. The air out front felt warmer after the bleached corridor, rich with beer breath, citrus peel, and sweat dried once then damp again. Every time the front door opened, a sheet of street heat came in with exhaust, rain-not-yet, and cigarette smoke from under the awning.

He crossed to the back ledge and found his beer where he'd left it, warmer now, flatter, tasting like it had spent ten minutes learning disappointment from the air.

He drank anyway, then took it to the bar.

Onstage, the song was doing all the pleading Cass had warned him about. Big chorus. Clean ache. Too much shine on the line that should have carried splinters. The front tables gave it the correct amount of attention. The back bar kept working. Applause came in obedient bursts, not mean, not moved enough. Joey leaned one elbow on the bar and let the room reassemble itself around him now that he knew more about its wiring than was probably good for him.

Across the room, Cass came out of the hallway a few beats later, alone.

She did not look at him.

She did not need to.

Duke stood near the front, half turned toward a table of men in rolled sleeves and expensive watches, smile broad enough to pass for hospitality from the cheap seats. Then his eyes moved. First to Joey at the bar. Then to Cass coming back in from the hall. Then back again.

The smile stayed warm.

The eyes changed jobs.

Joey had seen that look on him before with the ledger open, with the Thursday list clipped near the register, with the buy-in spoken in a voice meant to sound like principle instead of math. It was not jealousy. Not small suspicion either.

It was management.

Duke had just noticed a new angle in the room.
