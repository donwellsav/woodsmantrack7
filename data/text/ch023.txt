The Arrangement

Duke had told the showcase acts to get to the Landmark early, and Joey came early in the special mean way a man did when he had paid to be included.

By then the week had already laid hands on him in all the usual Atlanta places. Heat lifting off the sidewalks after sundown as if the concrete could not let an insult go. Busking hours that had sawed his shoulders raw under the strap. Ones and fives gone soft with strangers' sweat. Pike silent since filing day. The second paper shoved under his apartment door still sat on the kitchen table on top of the red notice, one more clean square of threat with no breath on it. The notebook with its crossed-out starts. The silver guitar back at the apartment, waiting under the bed to be the wrong answer again. He came up Moreland with the wooden guitar on his back and the dirty certainty the room was about to tell him something true enough to make him sick.

Before he saw the Landmark, he heard it.

Ice clattering into metal. A milk crate of bottles set down too hard behind the bar. Somebody in the kitchen shoving a rack where a rack did not want to slide. The hallway A/C losing to August. Then three dry guitar notes from the stage and a man's voice saying, "Check. Check." The notes skimmed the empty room and came back changed. A bottle on a back shelf answered with a glass tick. Half a breath later a fork struck a plate in the kitchen, as if the building kept its own lag time even without people in it.

Joey always heard a room before he saw one. When he was tired enough, it felt like a defect. Tonight it felt like a warning.

Inside, the corridor beside the stage smelled of bleach poured over stale beer, fryer grease breathed out from the kitchen, and cold air too weak to do anything but move the stink around. The fluorescents made everybody's face look doughy. The cinder-block wall still held the day in it; when his case brushed past, heat pressed through the canvas and tapped his elbow.

Duke's office door stood open a crack.

Light cut through the gap in a hard yellow strip. Joey might have kept walking if he had heard Duke laughing with the bar, or haggling with a distributor, or doing any of the hundred ordinary jobs that kept the Landmark from collapsing under its own age. Instead he heard Cass.

She was not loud. She was not putting anything on. Just low and tired in a way that stopped him before he had decided to stop.

"Randall's flying in for showcase night," Duke said. "That's the point of showcase night. That's the architecture."

Cass answered from deeper in the office. Joey could not see her yet, but he could hear how carefully she was spending breath.

"The folk kid paid."

Joey locked up so fast the guitar case kissed the door frame with a soft thud.

"They all pay," Duke said. His voice had that maddening warmth that could pass for kindness, warning, salesmanship, or faith, depending on what the room needed from him. "That's what the buy-in is. It buys in. It buys a shot at the room, a slot in the system, a night that happens in front of a man like Randall instead of dying in rehearsal. It does not buy me forgetting how to build a bill."

A small pause. In it Joey heard the office around them: the wall unit rattling, the ice machine coughing once down the hall, a soft chord from the stage where somebody was checking a cable.

Cass said, "Was it ever supposed to be a real competition?"

Duke let the question hang.

That pause told Joey more than the answer would have.

When Duke finally spoke, he made his voice gentler, and on him gentle was never innocent. It was a hand closing more softly.

"Cass. Come on."

Not yes. Not no. Just the question turned away from the light.

"You told him he had a chance," Cass said.

"He does."

"At what."

"At being heard. That's more than most people get."

"That's not what people hear when you say showcase."

Duke breathed out through his nose. Joey could see the smile without seeing his face. The one that made disagreement feel childish.

"What people hear," Duke said, "is usually their own fantasy in better lighting."

Cass did not bite. "Don't put me in the middle of this and call it destiny."

"No one is calling it destiny."

"Good," she said. "I work too hard for destiny."

That one landed clean. Joey could hear it in the quiet after. Duke heard it too; he let it sit a beat, maybe because he knew better than to swat at a line that good.

Then he said, softer, "You know what a room needs. Randall isn't flying down here for worthy effort and a handmade tragedy. He's coming because a night has been shaped in front of him. He's coming because you can take a room in your teeth and hold it there."

Cass said, "And part of that shape is me standing where you put me and making the rest of it look natural. Smile, sing, shake hands, act like my feet don't hurt and nobody's asking me to cash somebody else's theory with my body."

"Part of it," Duke said. "Yes. Because you can do it. That isn't an insult to you. It's the job."

The kid.

Joey pushed the door open.

The office was small enough that the three of them had to register one another all at once.

Desk. Ledger. A paper cup with cold coffee filmed over at the lip. A compressor estimate curled under a liquor invoice, both pinned by the coffee cup like Duke meant to shame them into staying paid. Flyers pasted over older flyers until the wall corners had their own sediment. A filing cabinet with one drawer stuck open the width of a thumb, like it objected to authority on principle. Photos everywhere. Black-and-white glossies. Curling color snapshots. Famous faces, local faces, faces Joey knew from records and from old men in shops saying names with reverence. The room held history the way a coat closet held smoke.

Cass's black stage bag sat open by her chair, a compact, throat lozenges, a gas receipt, a drugstore receipt, and a rolled fist of ones showing in the bottom. Then she looked at him and went pale.

Not guilty. Not busted. Just ambushed-tired. The face of somebody who had already survived the bad version of a conversation and now had to survive the worse version with a witness.

Duke's smile arrived half a beat late.

On somebody else it might not have meant anything. On Duke it was a siren.

"Joey," he said. "Come in."

Joey stepped in. He left the door open behind him. He wanted the fact of an exit in the room, even if none of them were dumb enough to confuse seeing it with having it.

The wooden guitar stayed on his shoulder. Sweat had darkened the strap where it crossed his shirt. His collar was stuck to the back of his neck. He could smell bus exhaust in the cotton and feel the coppery ache still humming in his fingertips. He skipped hello.

"I sold a gold guitar for that money."

Cass shut her eyes for a second. Duke did not blink.

"I know you did," Duke said.

"And you still sat here telling her my money bought me the privilege of not mattering."

"That is not what I said."

Cass said, "Near enough. Some of us still have to go out there after the speech and make it look inevitable."

Duke glanced at her. The look was almost tender, which only made it meaner.

"You see?" he said. "This is what happens when everybody insists on hearing insult where there is only structure."

"People get touchy," Cass said, "when the structure charges tuition."

A sound floated through the wall then: a singer onstage touching one note and holding it just long enough to see where it landed. The room took the note, planed it flat, and returned it smaller. Joey had felt that happen from the stage. Hearing it from Duke's office made the whole building seem mechanical, like a thing built to swallow offerings and burp back verdicts.

He looked at Duke.

"You said showcase. You said room. You said heard."

"And you will be heard."

"That's not the same thing as what you were selling."

Duke folded his arms. Not defensive. Patient. Patient the way a man got when he thought the answer was already in the drawer and everybody else was just wasting time before opening it.

"No," he said. "It's the same thing as what I sold. It is not the same thing as what you wanted to buy."

The sentence was so clean Joey almost laughed. That was Duke's gift. He could take something ugly, square its corners, and make it audition as wisdom.

Cass had turned her face toward the photo wall as if she could not stand one more second of men sharpening nouns at each other.

Duke came around the desk. The office shrank when he moved. Not because he was big. Because he moved like the room had already agreed to arrange itself around him.

"Let me ask you something," he said. "You walk into a restaurant and get mad because the chef wrote the menu before you sat down?"

Joey said nothing.

"The kitchen is still real," Duke said. "Somebody deciding sequence before you arrived doesn't make the meal fake."

"Music isn't dinner."

"No," Duke said. "It's harder. Pace matters. Order matters. Who follows who matters. If you think that's rigging, fine. I call it the labor of keeping a room alive long enough for a real song to reach somebody."

"Nice word for rigging."

"Precise word for survival."

Cass gave a short laugh with no joy in it.

"There it is," she said. "The sermon."

Duke looked at her. Not annoyed. Hurt, almost. That was part of how he got away with so much. He did not have to fake his own injuries. He felt his version of them for real.

"You think I enjoy this part?" he asked.

"I think you enjoy being the man who gets to call himself necessary."

Cass kept her voice flat. That was the tiredest thing about her. She was not performing defiance. She was too worn down to decorate the truth.

Duke took the hit and let it sit. A slower man would have lunged at his defense. Duke waited long enough for the room itself to seem like it might defend him.

Then he turned back to Joey.

"You think merit runs rooms?" he said. "It doesn't. Merit might run songs. Rooms are rent, liquor orders, attention span, and the couple by the bar already on their second fight before the first act tunes up. You want a meritocracy because it would keep your hands clean. The truth is dirtier and more expensive than that."

Joey wanted to call bullshit. Some part of him did. Another part had already spent too many Thursdays watching a room tip away or come alive one body at a time. He had seen attention drift. He had felt it pull tight. He knew by now that a stage did not turn honest just because the person on it meant well.

That was what made Duke so hard to hate cleanly. If he had been simpler, disgust would have sat easier in the body.

Duke kept going.

"You've seen it," he said. "Somebody catches one right silence in the right slot and the whole place remembers what music is for. That isn't magic. That's placement."

A memory moved through Joey before he could stop it. Second Thursday. A waitress slowing mid-crossing at the bridge of a song. One front table going quiet person by person. The strange bodily intelligence of a room deciding, for reasons it would never explain, to listen. He hated that the memory came up now, here, to argue with him from inside his own chest.

He looked away from Duke because even partial agreement felt like disloyalty to something he had not named.

That was when he saw the photograph.

It was smaller than the others and half hidden by two nicer frames. Cheap dark wood around it, too much like the five-dollar frame above his own turntable at home. Dust furred along the top edge. A man on a low stage under bad light, mouth open in the middle of a line, eyes gone inward in the exact way Joey had spent half his life worshipping and resenting.

His father.

The office gave under him.

Not dizziness. Not some movie jolt. Lower than that. Structural. Like stepping on a board that dropped a quarter inch and taught you, too late, how much weight it had been carrying.

He knew that face in every private dialect grief had to offer. The crooked photo on his apartment wall. The hospital light that had made his father look smaller and farther away at the same time. Then the office snapped back around him.

Only this was not kitchen memory.

This was wall memory. Public memory.

Not relic. Evidence.

Duke followed his gaze.

Something in Duke's voice changed, small but real. Less velvet in it. More spine.

"Gladys," he said, touching a frame above it. "Before anybody outside Georgia understood what they were looking at."

Another tap, one frame over. "Johnny Jenkins. Had the thing. Wrong room, wrong year, wrong math."

Then his finger moved to the photo Joey could not stop staring at.

"Your daddy."

Nobody said anything.

The wall unit rattled in the window. Out front, a truck hit a pothole and the bottles answered in the bar. Onstage somebody raked a chord hard enough to make the office door breathe in its frame. The Landmark gave the chord back warm and worn, the way it always did when sound found the right wood and the wrong paint.

Joey could not make himself look anywhere else.

He had wanted the Landmark to stay present tense. Duke's crooked present. Cass's compromise. His own mistake. Something separate from the older, cleaner altar he had built around his father. Seeing that photograph on Duke's wall took a hammer to that partition.

Inheritance went public all at once.

Not just the songs. The rooms. The doorways. The bargains nobody explained to sons.

"Was there a buy-in for him too?" Joey asked.

Cass turned toward Duke before Duke turned toward either of them. The question landed so clean there was nothing to do with it but leave it on the floor.

Duke's silence lasted longer than any speech he had given all night.

It was the most honest sound in the office.

When he finally answered, the polish stayed off for one whole sentence.

"I don't know what Zeke charged," he said. "Or what he called it."

Then he looked at Joey and added, "I know rooms always cost somebody something."

It would have been easier if he had hustled back into charm right away. Instead he stayed in the harder place for one more breath.

Duke touched the compressor estimate with two fingers, then the liquor invoice under the coffee cup.

"Twelve years I've kept this place open," he said. "Twelve years of rent on a building every developer on this block would rather flatten into condos. I spent noon arguing with a repairman over that compressor and four o'clock begging a keg delivery not to leave because the card machine hiccuped. Roof leaks. Wiring older than half my singers. Bartenders who want paychecks in money. A kitchen that burns through oil, bleach, and patience whether anybody has a transcendent evening or not. The checks clear or they don't. The city inspects or it doesn't. None of that takes applause instead of cash."

He opened one hand to the office, the hall, the stage beyond it, as if the whole worn body of the Landmark were laid out on his desk like a patient who still owed him money.

"So yes," he said. "I choose. I steer. I build nights. I put heat where it needs to be and relief where it earns itself. Not because I'm holy. Because if I stop, this place becomes memory and somebody else's floor plan."

Cass's jaw worked once. Joey could see she had heard some version of this speech before, maybe enough times to know where every sentence bent. What hurt in her face was not surprise. It was recognition without any release in it.

"You always make it sound like mercy," she said.

"No," Duke said quietly. "I make it sound expensive."

The line hit Joey because it ran too close to the truth.

He looked back at his father's photograph.

It was a bad photo. Too dark at the edges. Grainy in the wrong places. Cheap print stock. Maybe even somebody's finger shadow trapped in one corner from when it got developed. No glamour in it. No myth. Which made it more dangerous than the framed one in Joey's apartment. That one asked to be kept. This one asked to be admitted.

If his father had played here, then his father had played under somebody's order. Somebody's bill. Somebody's idea of what should come before and after him. Maybe a buy-in. Maybe a favor. Maybe a tab with a softer name. Maybe a room crooked enough to stay open and honest enough to matter anyway.

The clean split Joey had been living inside came apart in him.

Not because Duke was innocent.

Because Duke was not the whole contamination.

That was worse.

"I thought," Joey said, and the sentence broke on him because it came out rougher than he meant it to. He swallowed and tried again. "I thought if this place was dirty enough, I could say it didn't count."

Duke did not interrupt. For once he seemed to know talk would cheapen it.

Joey kept his eyes on the photo.

"Not the songs," he said. "The room. I could keep that separate."

"You can't," Cass said.

Her voice had flattened further. Not cruel. Just tired clear through.

Joey looked at her then. Really looked. The careful makeup already losing its fight with the day. The set of her shoulders saying she knew exactly what it cost to stand in this office and then go out and do the job anyway. She did not look like Duke's accomplice. She looked like somebody doing long division with bad numbers and no room left for fantasy.

"This is what I meant in the hallway," she said. "About belief keeping the living in bad rooms longer."

He nodded once. There was no comfort in being understood by somebody whose understanding had plainly cost too much.

Duke went back behind the desk, but he still did not sit. "You want the room pure because then disappointment would excuse you," he said. "You could walk away and call yourself intact. But the hearing can still be real inside all that. You've heard it."

Again the room outside made its own case. This time a singer caught not just a note but a phrase. The words were too blurred to make out, but the shape of them lifted the air. The hallway went quiet on instinct. A plate in the kitchen waited a beat before landing. Then it was over and the building resumed its usual racket. Maybe that was all a room miracle ever was. Not purity. A few seconds of obedience.

Joey hated Duke for being able to point at a true thing from the wrong side of it.

He shifted the guitar on his shoulder. The old strap gave a dry creak. Without looking, his hand found the worn spot by the case handle where the leather had split and been wrapped years ago in black tape. His father had done that repair at the kitchen table too, tongue against his teeth, unwilling to buy new while patching still existed. That taped seam, stupid and small, dropped Joey back into the apartment for half a second: screen door banging, bacon grease in the pan, his father working silence into usefulness. Then the office closed around him again.

He had inherited objects from a man who had also, apparently, inherited bad rooms.

Nothing stayed clean very long in this city. Not wood. Not buildings. Not songs.

"I thought if you were crooked enough," Joey said to Duke, "I could call the whole place false and be done."

"And?" Duke asked.

Joey looked once more at the wall. Famous people. Almost-famous people. Dead people. Living people who had probably hoped to be remembered some other way. His father among them, proof that public history and private grief had been sharing walls all along without asking him.

"And apparently that would've been convenient."

For the first time all evening, Duke did not answer with a line meant to win.

He only nodded.

That irritated Joey more than another speech would have.

He turned and left before the office could shrink any further.

The corridor hit him with bleach, stale beer, and the cool hiss of overworked air. Out onstage somebody laughed into a microphone, then apologized to the empty room for testing it. The room took the apology and gave back indifference. Joey passed the crack in the curtain and caught one slice of the front: naked tabletops, the bar lights coming up, the stage waiting for somebody to tell it what kind of night it was. It looked less holy than it had an hour earlier and more binding.

By the time he reached the street, the heat had settled in the brick and was already climbing back out of it. Moreland traffic kept bad time with itself. A MARTA bus knelt at the stop and hissed back upright. Two tourists argued about parking under smoke-shop neon. Somewhere nearby someone had lit incense and lost immediately to fryer grease. Atlanta went on being Atlanta without the slightest interest in his revelation.

He stood for a second with the guitar still on him and understood only this: he had not inherited songs from outside the system. The bargain was older than Duke.

Back in the office, Duke stayed where he was until Joey's footsteps had gone from the hallway and then from the front room too.

Cass had already left by the other door without saying goodbye. Duke did not look surprised. He braced one hand on the desk and rubbed the bridge of his nose with the other, as if sincerity itself had weight and he was tired of wearing it in his face.

Then he sat.

He opened the ledger, but not to the money page. That arithmetic was done. Five hundred dollars bought in. Cass accounted for. Beer, whiskey, kitchen inventory, all the smaller hungers that kept a room from going dark.

He turned to the running order.

Names. Timing marks. Arrows only Duke fully understood. A note about where the room would need relief. A star beside Cass. Another act placed high enough to fail quickly and usefully. Near the bottom, written cleaner than the rest because it had joined the page late, was WOODSMAN.

From the stage came another dry chord, then two more. Somebody out there was feeling for the room's bones before doors.

Duke looked at the name a long moment.

Then he picked up the pen.

Not to add it. That part was done.

He wrote two short timing marks beside the line and underlined the slot once.

Bottom slot. Last position.

Not kindness. Not exactly punishment either.

The kind of place a man gave when he wanted a room to remember somebody and wanted that memory to arrive under conditions he still controlled.

He capped the pen. Closed the ledger. Sat one more second in the bad fluorescent light while the whole building hummed around him, waiting for the night to put its face on.

WOODSMAN was already official.

Duke was only deciding what kind of trouble to make out of that fact.
