Third Thursday

By the time Thursday came back around, improvement felt less like comfort than evidence, which was the problem.

If he had still been bad in the easy, public, humiliating ways, the whole business would have stayed mercifully simple. He could have blamed the room, Duke, the front table, nerves, the bad monitor, the contaminated economics of a church turned venue turned gate. He could have gone on treating technique as the missing country and himself as a man stuck at the border with the right papers in the wrong line.

But Thursday had the nerve to come back after Moreland.

After Mercury.

After Veritas.

After both of them had walked off and left him with less camouflage than he'd started the week with.

The apartment sounded wrong for writing before he even cracked the notebook.

It had its usual noises. Fridge hum. Somebody's television bleeding canned laughter through the wall in bright ugly bursts. A car alarm two blocks over trying to turn petty theft into opera. Pipes knocking once in the building as if the whole place objected to being asked for one more function after dark. The dead fan still hanging in the window like a resigned witness. A MARTA bus exhaling at the stop on Moreland, doors folding open and shut with the same tired breath Atlanta used for everything: transit, labor, compromise, one more night.

None of that was new.

What was new was Joey's inability to pretend the louder room wasn't inside him.

The silver guitar stood in the corner by the record player. Clarence's sleeve still slouched against the turntable behind it, side B waiting there in pencil.

He had dragged the thing halfway out from under the bed two nights earlier looking for loose pages and never shoved it back. Now it leaned there with the case open beside it, all wrong clean lines and unearned shine. In the apartment's weak lamp light the tuning pegs caught hard little stars and threw them back at him. Better finish. Better neck. Better hardware. No weather on it.

He hated how often his eyes went to it.

The wooden guitar lay across his lap instead, lower bout tacky where the old repaired crack always got sticky in heat. That bad patch did him the kindness of feeling true every time his thumb found it. He rubbed the seam once and let it remind him of nothing except itself. Cheap chair. Crooked blinds. Notebook on the seat beside him like a witness who had shown up early.

He opened it.

Blank page on top.

Below it, the pressure scars from the old failures. Lines pressed hard enough through earlier sheets to groove the paper underneath, as if every bad attempt had wanted its own gravestone.

He played the fragment once.

Not the whole thing. It never arrived as the whole thing. It came in through a side door the way it always did, his fingers finding the progression before his mouth could decide whether to admit it was in the room. Three measures. Maybe four. A turn in the middle that belonged to nobody dead enough to protect him.

Then his own hand swerved.

Back into older ground. Safer ground. A chord shape he could wear without being seen in it. Left hand moving before he consciously ordered it, choosing inherited vocabulary over exposure with the speed of an old survival reflex.

He stopped.

Set the guitar flat across his thighs.

"You unbelievable coward," he said to the room.

The room, being an apartment, declined commentary.

He wrote one line.

Crossed it out before the ink had time to gloss over.

Wrote another, and this one got as far as bus brakes before daylight before he realized he was finally writing this city instead of some borrowed elsewhere. That frightened him worse than the failure had. He slashed only the back half of the sentence and left the first words bleeding through the ink.

Wrote another, safer only because it was vaguer.

Killed that one too.

He tried a chord cue instead of a sentence, as if harmony could smuggle honesty through customs if language kept getting flagged. That died on the page faster than the first two lines had.

Private failure's insult was not that nobody saw it. It was that nobody needed to. The page witnessed well enough on its own. Every half-line sounded like somebody who had studied the tradition hard enough to counterfeit feeling without ever putting his own body underneath it. He could hear the polish in it now. The tastefulness. The little evasions dressed up as restraint.

He looked at the silver guitar again.

For one weak second he imagined picking it up. Just to hear what his hands did on something cleaner. Just to pull family out of the mechanics and see if that changed the sentence any. Just to know whether his blockage was really grief and inheritance and the museum of his father, or whether he could fail in a more contemporary key and at least get the experiment over with.

But he knew before touching it that all it would do was make the avoidance prettier.

Still, he reached.

He picked it up once and let the neck settle into his palm.

It felt excellent.

That was the problem.

No sticky seam. No place rubbed pale by years of the same right hand. No cheap repair asking to be forgiven every summer. The thing felt like a future you bought retail. A life with no fingerprints already on it.

He played one chord.

The sound came out clear and cold and almost offensively balanced. It filled the apartment too beautifully, hanging above the fridge hum and the neighbor's television as if the room had suddenly become more expensive than the life inside it.

Beautiful.

Unusable.

He set it back in the corner hard enough for the strings to complain.

"Not you," he muttered.

Then, after a beat, "Also not me, apparently."

He took up the wooden guitar again and tried for another twenty minutes because not trying at all would have required a cleaner surrender than he was prepared to stage before sunset.

The attempt got uglier as it went. One line too stiff. One too naked. One sounding so much like a borrowed ghost of his father that he crossed it out hard enough to tear the paper at the margin. Sweat worked down his back even with his shirt hanging open. The pen kept slipping in his fingers. Outside, thunder moved somewhere over the city without yet bothering to become rain.

By the end of it the notebook held five aborted lines, two scratched-over chord cues, a top sheet he had ripped free with unnecessary violence and dropped to the floor by the chair, and one half-line about bus brakes before daylight that he had not managed to kill clean.

The page underneath was somehow worse for having been revealed.

He looked at the clock.

Pike could file now.

Thursday did not care whether a man had anything true to bring it. Thursday cared about arrival time.

So he capped the pen, set the wooden guitar back in its case, left the silver one glaring quietly from the corner, and went to the Landmark with failure still warm in his hands.

Night on Moreland had thickened by then into that late-August pressure that felt like rain rehearsing somewhere else in town. Traffic kept moving. Headlights made quick churches of every windshield and then passed on. The block around the Landmark smelled like old fryer oil, bus exhaust, cigarettes, hot brick, and the medicinal sweetness from the smoke shop two doors down losing another round to bar grease. Somewhere farther off a siren rose and flattened again. Atlanta never shut up. It only changed registers.

Inside, Thursday was already underway.

Not full. Not showcase crowded. But alive in its usual practical ways. Bar stools occupied. Fries moving. A pair of women near the front sharing a basket and a grievance. A man in a Braves cap telling a story to no one who had fully agreed to hear it. A server balancing a tray with the face of somebody who had long ago stopped confusing hospitality with devotion. The front table changed people week to week, but the posture stayed the same: paid, comfortable, certain the room ought to work around them until the room earned better.

Joey went to the bar first because ritual had become a technology.

"Cheapest beer."

The bartender popped the cap without looking up from the register.

"They get more expensive every time you improve."

"Then I need to start failing publicly again."

"No, you don't." He slid the bottle across. "It's boring to watch twice."

Joey handed over four dollars and took the beer, one more small subtraction from what the gold guitar had become.

The bartender gave him one glance then, longer than the transaction required. Eyes to the case. Up to Joey's face. Back to the stage list clipped near the register.

"You look worse," he said.

"Emotionally or architecturally."

"Both."

Joey drank before answering.

"Helpful."

"Aim to serve."

He left it there because the bartender was the sort of man who only offered concern if he could disguise it as bad service.

The Thursday list ran in the same crooked order it always did, but Joey heard it differently now. He could tell where the room was loose and where it might tighten. Could hear which singer onstage was pushing for importance before earning proximity. Could hear which guitarist had tuned for brightness because brightness traveled faster than weight. Could hear fear choosing tempo over shape. That knowledge had cost him something, and part of him was still proud of it in spite of everything. Pride, he was learning, did not turn false just because it was insufficient.

When Duke glanced up from the corner table and gave him the smallest nod toward the stage list, Joey felt his body answer before his feelings got a vote.

Go time.

He set the beer down half-finished. Took the guitar from its case. Checked tuning with the efficiency of a man who had stopped confusing ritual with panic. Around him the room went on being itself. Ice. Laughter. Fry hiss. Somebody asking for ranch from too far away. Cash drawer. Chair scrape. All of it the sound of a place that had promised nobody transcendence and might still accidentally permit some.

Then he stepped onto the stage.

The mic stand leaned slightly left. Same as always. The wedge monitor hummed low at his boots. The first table on the right was already in mid-conversation. Somebody near the bar had a phone on the table lighting up every thirty seconds with one fresh problem after another. None of it startled him now. That was the point.

He did not open with force. Force was first-Thursday behavior, a frightened man's way of trying to bully weather.

He opened with something older and low-lit, a song his father used to call patient enough for mean rooms. Not slow exactly. Just unwilling to hurry itself on behalf of strangers.

The first verse went out over cutlery and half-attention.

He let it.

Second verse, he lowered rather than raised. Drew the line in tighter. Made the room come the extra foot if it wanted the rest.

One table did.

Then another.

At the bar, a glass got set down quieter than it needed to.

A waitress carrying wings across the back slowed without meaning to and finished crossing on the chord instead of through the lyric.

By the bridge, the woman with the grievance basket near the front had stopped talking long enough to hear what she was stopping.

Joey felt the shift and kept his shoulders loose. Fear wanted him to pounce and call that control. He let the room walk the extra foot on its own.

He moved into the second song with barely any patter at all. A year ago that would have scared him. Joey used to treat talking as a kind of percussion. Now he understood that too much explanation was just another way of apologizing in advance.

Still, he felt the old impulse flare when the room quieted. The reflex to give them county lines and hymn routes and some halfway funny scrap about where a tune had probably first learned to sit up straight in Georgia. His father had known how to talk around a song without draining it. Joey, when he got scared, usually talked to keep the song from getting to him first.

He left the words in his mouth and played instead.

Hands steady. Voice steadier. The set shaping itself not through inspiration but through earned room craft. He left silence where silence belonged. Let a phrase hang a beat past where his nerves liked it. Let the front table discover it was already listening before its mouths got the memo.

The room answered in pieces.

Not miraculous pieces. Human ones.

A laugh from the side booth died early and stayed dead.

The man in the Braves cap turned his stool a quarter turn toward the stage without meaning to make a statement out of it.

The woman at the bar with the lit-up phone put it face down and forgot to pick it back up.

Even the kitchen seemed to work around him for half a minute, fryer hiss and plate clatter landing between lines instead of across them, as if the building itself had learned enough manners to wait.

And because the body loved irony, he felt good.

Not redeemed. Not transformed. But good in the old blunt physical sense. This works. That chord landed. That line made the room inhale together. They are here with you now.

It happened on the third song.

A small open place in the middle. Three chords of air he had built into the arrangement over the last couple of weeks because the bartender had been right about giving songs somewhere to sit. The room was with him. The table noise had fallen to manageable weather. Even the man in the Braves cap had turned enough that his own story now sounded rude to him.

Joey's fingers reached the opening.

And there it was.

Not the whole song. Never the whole song. Just that live little turn with his own address on it.

For one clean second he could have stepped into it.

He didn't.

His left hand shifted like it had rehearsed the dodge.

A tasteful old variation dropped into the gap instead, housebroken and gorgeous. Nobody in the room could have called the move cowardice.

The transition was seamless.

The room rewarded it.

He heard a pleased sound from the front, the kind people made when they thought they had witnessed taste. Two heads lifted at the bar. Attention held and deepened because what he had done was good. Precisely good. Good enough to hide inside.

That was what made him want to be sick.

Because he knew exactly what he had covered and how fast he had done it. He had not frozen. Had not failed in public. Had done something worse. He had executed the avoidance well.

He finished the song. Then another. Then walked them all the way to the end of the set with enough control now that the applause came up unified instead of accidental.

Not showcase applause. Not destiny. Thursday applause. Honest enough to matter. Thin enough to stay believable. More of the room in it than there had been on second Thursday. Enough that Joey had to admit what he had done even while hating the category.

He stepped offstage with the guitar still warm against him and knew, in the same instant, two things that did not improve each other.

He could hold the room now.

He had just used that skill to dodge himself more elegantly than ever.

The bartender had his beer waiting where Joey left it.

"Dog came over and sat down," he said.

Joey took the bottle. "For a while."

"Long enough."

That should have felt good.

Instead it landed like a doctor's report on a knee that worked perfectly while the rest of the body got worse.

Joey took a drink.

From the corner table, Duke lifted two fingers once in acknowledgment and then went right back to the ledger. No scene. No bourbon this time. Just the manager's version of witness. You did the job. Next item.

A man Joey didn't know stopped on his way back from the bathroom and tapped the lip of the stage with two knuckles.

"That third one," he said. "Good stuff."

"Thanks."

The man nodded and kept moving.

That was all.

Enough to keep a person from leaving. Not enough to save him.

Joey sat with the beer and listened to the next act set up badly. The room had already begun forgetting him in the efficient way rooms had to if they meant to stay open all night. Plate dropped in the kitchen. Laughter at the bar. Somebody ordering another round. Attention redistributed like cash after tax. The song he had just played still moved faintly in his hands, but out in the room the space had already been rented to the next need.

He should have felt proud.

Instead he kept seeing that open place in the third song. The exact second where he could have let the unfinished thing show itself. Not all the way. Just enough to admit it was alive. And the smoother, cleaner, more technically admirable detour he had chosen instead.

Competence, he thought, was turning out to be another species of hiding.

He finished the beer, packed the wooden guitar away, and left before anybody could turn his set into a conversation he would have to survive politely.

Outside, the city had gone from one kind of heat to another. Pavement still breathed the day back up through his shoes. A couple leaned against the wall outside the bar down the block and fought softly enough to suggest practice. Somebody burned incense in one of the storefronts and lost immediately to fryer grease. The applause still buzzed faintly in his palms like a ghost signal from the strings. By the time he reached the apartment, the feeling had narrowed into something harder and lonelier.

Inside, the silver guitar was still in the corner where he had left it.

He hooked one foot under the case and shoved it back beneath the bed until only the clean edge caught streetlight.

The apartment's first gift was always honesty. No room tone. No audience. No illusion that a good set had changed the furniture.

The torn page still lay on the floor by the chair.

The notebook waited open beside it, exposing the next wrong attempt like a wound you kept lifting the bandage from just to make sure it had not healed on its own.

Joey set the case down by the wall. Took off his shirt. Sat. The room smelled like old paper, sweat dried into cotton, and the faint sour trace of whatever somebody in the building had overcooked an hour earlier. He stared at the page long enough for the fridge hum to become personal criticism.

Then he picked up the pen.

What came out was worse for having survived the Landmark first.

Because now he could hear exactly what was missing.

Not polish. Not phrasing. Not room sense. He had those. Enough anyway.

What the page wanted was the one thing he still approached like an armed doorway: a sentence nobody dead had authorized and nobody alive had yet rejected.

He wrote six words.

Crossed out four.

Added three more that made the line safer and therefore deader.

Crossed the whole thing out.

Started again below it with only a chord cue this time, as if maybe the guitar could drag language behind it by force.

It didn't.

He tore that page free too.

Paper hit the floor beside the earlier one.

The sound was absurdly light for how final it felt.

He looked over at the silver guitar.

Still perfect.

Still wrong.

He looked down at the wooden one in its case.

Still wrong too, in the useful way. Sticky seam. Bad repair. Sweat-dark strap. History in bad materials.

The honest instrument could not do the work for him either. The right tool did not remove the right risk.

He sat there a long time with the pen in one hand and nothing in the other, the applause from the Landmark draining out of his body by slow degrees while the room around him resumed its smaller civic duties. Pipes. Fridge. Distant traffic. Somebody laughing in the building with no obligation to make art out of it. The silver guitar under the bed catching one thin stripe of streetlight and looking, from a distance, almost reasonable.

At last he put the pen down.

Not in surrender.

Only because he was tired of pretending motion and progress were the same thing.

Onstage, he thought, he could do it now.

He could make strangers hush.

He could pace a room.

He could leave silence where it needed to be and trust people to walk the extra foot toward it.

Here, with the page still wrong and the silver guitar shoved under the bed and the wooden guitar waiting honest as trouble, he still could not say the one thing that would belong only to him.

The room, he understood then, had become postponement with better acoustics.
