The Room in Daylight

He heard the building before he trusted what he was looking at.

Mop water first. Bleach and wet wood trying to make peace. Then chair legs scraping as somebody stacked them upside down on tables inside, a refrigerator humming under that, the blunt tap of a bucket against baseboard, a man's cough swallowed fast by brick.

By the time Joey reached the Landmark, the day had gone from hot to punitive.

He had gone back to the apartment first because he was not carrying Clarence's record into a bar and pretending that counted as caution. The sleeve leaned beside the turntable stand now, out of reach of the fan. He had set it there with two fingers on the edges and heard his father at once, not as a full memory, just a hand-sized one: not the middle, son, the edges, oils go where the music is going. The thought flashed and was gone before it fully became memory, but it stayed in his fingers all the way down the hall. So had the other thing, the unsettled seam on side B Clarence had left breathing when Joey walked out. He had almost turned back to the turntable twice. He had not.

Under the bed, the silver case had caught a strip of late-afternoon light and thrown it back too cleanly. It still looked impossible in a room with crooked blinds and a refrigerator that complained in its sleep. The wooden guitar had stayed in the chair by the window where it belonged. The gold guitar was gone. The record was by the turntable. The pawn-shop envelope had come with him.

It rode now in the inside pocket of his shirt, flat to his ribs. Twelve hundred from the gold guitar, plus the recovered twenty-five and the forty cents that had somehow survived everything else. Enough money to make a bad decision feel organized. Enough to turn Duke's door from fantasy into arithmetic. Not enough to change the fact that Pike could still file in fourteen days.

That was the meaner part. Broke was simple. Broke let a man want cleanly. Almost-paying was where the rot started.

He crossed Moreland with the walk sign already flashing down, cars breathing heat at his knees. The block smelled of old brick, gasoline, fryer grease, incense from the shop two doors down, and the soft sourness of garbage beginning to take itself seriously. A MARTA bus knelt at the curb with a hydraulic sigh. Two kids in black jeans came out of the tattoo shop laughing too hard for the weather. Somewhere up the block, a man was arguing into a phone with the concentrated misery of somebody trying to keep an electric bill from turning into a worldview.

Atlanta in August did not hand out atmosphere. It handed out conditions.

By the time Joey stopped in front of the Landmark, sweat had gone tacky under his shirt, and the envelope against his chest felt less like money than like something he was trying to smuggle past himself.

The building from the street looked less like destiny than it had on the flyer.

That should have helped.

Old brick. Narrow front. A faded vertical sign with half the bulbs dead and the other half trying to remember enthusiasm. The windows reflected the street harder than they revealed the room. You could still see what the building had first been built for, and not in the polished adaptive-reuse way rich people liked to praise after they had made a neighborhood unrecognizable. In the bones. The front arch rose too high for a bar. The proportions asked for witness, not cocktails. The brick still held an earlier intention the business inside it had not quite scrubbed away.

Joey put one hand over the envelope through the shirt. Not because he thought it would vanish. Because touch was the quickest way to make a number feel real, and he needed the number real before he stepped through a door that had already started trying to tell his life back to him.

Then he pushed inside.

The Landmark in daylight was not a club pretending to be holy. It was a church that had taken a long, crooked route through rent, liquor licenses, bad wiring, and human appetite and wound up serving drinks without giving up the architecture that had first taught it how to carry sound.

The ceiling rose higher than it had any business rising over a neighborhood room. Old beams crossed above the bar lights and speakers and the dark curtain over the stage. The stage sat where an altar might once have been, if the building had belonged to the sort of faith that liked its emphasis dead center. Chairs were upside down on tables, legs in the air like disciplined animals waiting for the room to bare its teeth again. A man with a mop worked the floor with the steady indifference of somebody whose paycheck did not improve because another young musician had shown up full of need. The air was cooler than outside, but only by degrees. Mostly the room held the memory of cooler hours in its corners: stale-cool, dim, bleach cut with old beer, the sweetness of old wood waking under damp.

Without bodies in it, the stage looked smaller.

That did not comfort him. Some rooms only lied once they were full. Empty, they showed you the size of the job.

At a corner table near the side wall sat a man big enough to make furniture look temporary.

Ledger open in front of him. Calculator. Pen. A cup of coffee that had long ago given up the idea of heat. Pale shirt with the sleeves rolled exactly. Cuff links catching what little light the room allowed and holding it. Gold in the watch. Gold in the smile when he looked up and found Joey in the doorway.

Duke had the sort of face that read fatherly from across a room if you were the kind of person who went looking for fathers in strangers. Up close, the effect complicated itself. The warmth was real. That was the danger. Men who lied badly could be fenced. Men who believed in the thing they were selling were harder.

The mop man did not look up.

Duke did immediately.

This was his room. Joey felt that from the speed of the attention alone.

"You Duke?" Joey said.

Duke took off his glasses first.

That, apparently, was how he judged people. Not with a squint. With ceremony. The glasses came off, got folded, got laid across the open ledger with the kind of care that suggested even interruptions were entered into the system.

"Depends," he said. "You city?"

"No."

"Fire marshal?"

"No."

"Health inspector?"

"No."

"Church?"

"No."

"Then we're improving already." Duke leaned back. The chair accepted him like it had been built for negotiation. "What are you?"

"I play guitar."

"There it is." The smile widened. Warm, amused, practiced without tipping fake. "A much better class of problem. Come in. Shut the door. Terrence."

The mop man stopped mopping.

Duke did not raise his voice to do it.

"Get this man coffee. The good bag. Not the bar coffee. The bag from my office." He looked back at Joey. "Sugar?"

Joey had not thought that far.

"Sure."

"Two." Duke nodded once, as if this had confirmed something. His eyes dropped briefly to Joey's empty hands, then to the callus along the side of his index finger, then back to his face. "Name."

"Joey Woodsman."

The name landed.

Not with revelation. With interest.

Duke repeated it once under his breath like a line he might use later if it proved worth the trouble. "Joey Woodsman. That's a name with timber in it. That's a name somebody ought to announce over a microphone while the room does the respectful thing and looks up."

Joey, because nerves always made him drier than he meant to be, said, "Mostly pigeons, right now."

Duke laughed once, genuine enough to unsettle him.

"Good. You know you're in trouble. That helps." He gestured to the chair opposite him. "Sit down, Joey Woodsman. A man walks into my building in the middle of the afternoon, no instrument in his hands but every sign of one in his life, looking like the weather has been personally insulting him for hours, I assume he's here to ask for something. Saves time if we skip to the noun."

Joey sat.

The chair under him had one back rung polished smoother than the others where years of hands had used the same leverage point to stand. Whoever sat here regularly had opinions about posture.

The room sounded different from the chair than it had from the door. Closer. The refrigerators under the bar worked a little too hard. Terrence's mop moved over the boards with a wet whisper. Street noise came in softened, as if the brick had opinions about what counted as indoor information. Somewhere above the back hall, something metal clicked, then settled. The place was between uses and knew it.

Terrence set down a mug of coffee without looking at Joey's face.

Real coffee. Not diner burner coffee. Not bar coffee left to blacken into punishment. Clean smell. Slightly expensive. Beans that had known intention somewhere along the line.

Duke watched Joey take the first sip.

"Worth fourteen dollars a bag," he said. "Roaster in Decatur. I buy three a week for this office because cheap coffee produces cheap decisions."

Joey swallowed. "That's a strong management theory."

"Most correct theories sound arrogant until they save you." Duke drank from his own cold coffee and gave no sign he considered temperature a category with legal standing. "All right. Ask."

Joey set the mug down carefully.

He had rehearsed versions of this on the walk over. Some direct. Some casual. A couple that tried to make it sound as if he were doing Duke the favor of clarity. All of them collapsed under the room.

"I want a slot in the showcase."

Duke did not answer right away.

He smiled again, slower now, not because the request surprised him but because Joey had apparently asked in a format he respected.

"Well," he said. "Walked in off the street and said the sentence out loud. Nobody does that anymore. They email. They have a bartender's friend send me a link I will not click. They say they are just trying to connect, which is a phrase invented by cowards and consultants. Nobody comes in sweating through a floral shirt and asks for the slot with his own mouth. That takes a kind of stupidity that borders on bravery."

"I can't tell if that's an insult."

"It's a sorting tool." Duke folded his hands over the ledger. "What kind of music?"

"Folk, mostly."

"Mostly is where the interesting things live."

"Folk headed somewhere else," Joey said. "I don't know what to call it yet."

Duke's eyes sharpened, not with suspicion exactly. With appetite.

"Good answer. Anybody who names his sound too early is usually trying to cover for the fact that there isn't one yet." He glanced up toward the beams, then back at Joey. "You know what this place used to be."

"Church."

"A.M.E. Eighteen ninety-two if you trust the records. Earlier if you trust the women in this neighborhood, who usually know more than the records anyway." He lifted one broad hand toward the ceiling. "Pews where those tables are. Altar where the stage is. Acoustics built for the human voice under full commitment. Not tasteful singing. Not somebody tasteful's nephew doing a tasteful solo while everybody admires how cleanly he has been raised. Testimony. A church is built to carry people willing to risk being heard."

He let that sit.

Joey looked up.

The beams did something unpleasantly persuasive to him. They made him think about sound as a physical fact. About where a held note would go once it left a mouth or a string. About how his father's voice had sounded in Clarence's shop not an hour earlier, plain and labor-made, and how a room like this might either bless that plainness or expose it. Even empty, the Landmark felt like it had a chest.

That irritated him. He did not like being impressible in public.

Duke saw the look and smiled as if he had watched men fight the room before.

"Exactly," he said softly, though Joey had not spoken.

Terrence moved the mop bucket to another section. The water sloshed and thudded against plastic with a church-basement sound.

Duke settled back.

"You know the Ray Charles story."

"I've heard versions."

"Everybody has. Most of them are lazy." Duke pointed toward Joey's chair. "Nineteen seventy-one. Ray comes in through the back, not the front. Through the alley, through the kitchen, scares the dishwasher half to death, sits right where you are sitting. Not at the stage. Not at the bar. Right there. Somebody gets him a ham sandwich. I cannot verify the sandwich with the rigor I prefer, and that bothers me, because details are how history avoids becoming decoration. But I know about the chair."

Joey glanced down at the worn rung under his hand.

The wood did not look holy. Just used. Which, he had learned by now, was usually the more convincing condition.

"He sits there forty-five minutes," Duke said. "Not talking much. The waitress asks if he needs anything else. People always botch this and say he asked for water. He did ask for water. But first he asked what the room sounded like empty."

Duke lifted his hand and clapped once.

The sound went out clean and came back changed.

Not thunder. Not fake magic. Just one hard report that hit the back wall, found the ceiling, ran the beams, and returned with a little more body than it had left with. A glass behind the bar answered faintly. The curtain by the stage breathed once. Joey felt the pressure of it brush one cheek before the room finally let it die.

Without thinking, he heard a bar of his father's record under the clap, not literally, just the shape of how it might travel in here: first chord opening, voice coming in plain, the room deciding whether to carry it or let it drop. Under that ran the same rougher pressure from side B, unnamed and waiting. For half a second the place seemed to tilt toward the imagined sound, and then it was only a room again and Duke was still watching him.

"That's what he wanted," Duke said. "One clap. Empty room. Honest bones. Then he got up and played three hours, and according to Zeke Carter there were people in the street with their ears against the brick because the room was giving them as much Ray as the walls would let out."

He let the story rest exactly long enough to do its work, then made the turn with the ease of a man used to walking myth into policy.

"Maybe all of that is true. Maybe half. Zeke lied recreationally. But he never lied about sound."

Joey wrapped both hands around the coffee mug and felt the heat.

Clarence's shop had made his father public. Duke was trying to make that publicness feel like welcome. Joey knew better. Wanting it anyway was the problem.

Duke studied him over the rim of his cold coffee.

"Woodsman," he said after a moment. "That's a neighborhood name."

The sentence hit Joey lower in the chest than it had any right to.

"Is it?"

"If you keep your ears open." Duke shrugged. "Old songs. Stubborn men. The sort who think devotion excuses bad business. Clarence mentioned you. Mae too. Little Five Points isn't as big as people like to imagine when they're trying to preserve the romance of bumping into one another."

That was less than Joey wanted and more than he should have trusted.

Maybe Duke knew his father. Maybe he knew the surname only as gossip and residue. Maybe he knew exactly how much to imply to a young man sitting across from a ledger with hope all over his face. Joey hated that he could not tell. Hated more that the uncertainty did not weaken the hook.

"I'm not here because of my father," he said.

The sentence came out wrong the instant it left him. Too fast. Too smooth. Not true enough to sound true.

Duke did not smile.

"No?" he said mildly.

Joey looked at the stage instead of at him.

"I'm here because I want the slot."

"Better." Duke nodded once. "Dead men make excellent weather and terrible strategy."

Cruel enough to sound useful.

He turned the ledger a little, not enough for Joey to read the names, just enough for him to see the architecture: columns, dates, amounts, the margins where extra information waited to become fate. The sight of the ruled lines gave Joey a fast, unpleasant glimpse of Pike's notices on the apartment door, the rent ledger in the office downstairs, the hospital bill that had arrived two weeks after the funeral and used the word balance as if grief had been a clerical delay. Paper liked a straight line. It never cared what had happened to get there.

"Here," Duke said, tapping the page once with his pen, "is where romance receives an invoice."

He said it lightly, but the sentence landed with the force of procedure.

"Showcase has a buy-in. Production. Sound. Lights. Staff. Scout airfare from Nashville, hotel, dinner, car service, all the little invisible costs people prefer to imagine are generated by fairy dust because the stage looks pretty once they sit down and have feelings."

He paused.

"Five hundred dollars."

The number hit the room and changed its use.

Before that, the Landmark had been old beams, stale cool, the Ray chair, testimony, seduction. After that it was math.

Joey did the subtraction immediately because broke people always did.

Twelve hundred from the gold guitar. Twenty-five recovered. Forty cents that still counted because forty cents always counted. Minus five hundred left seven hundred twenty-five dollars and forty cents. Enough to pay the number. Not enough to put five hundred into Duke's hand and forget Pike could still file in fourteen days. Not enough to turn food and transit and whatever emergency came next into solved categories. Enough, though.

Enough was the dangerous quantity.

He looked down into the coffee as if the answer might be waiting on the surface.

The smell of the pawn shop came back on him for one ugly second. Glass counter. Old metal. The owner's hand turning the gold neck under bad fluorescent light. That impossible instrument becoming inventory while Joey stood there telling himself he was only translating miracle into survival. Five hundred dollars made the translation cruder. Less survival. More tribute.

Duke saw the arithmetic happen. Of course he did.

"You can almost hear a man counting from here," he said.

Joey gave him a look. "You watch everybody's face that closely?"

"In this room? That's my main qualification."

The shame was not being broke. It was being close enough to choose badly on purpose. The pawn-shop envelope pressed harder against his ribs, asking whether he meant it.

Duke, because he was not stupid, did not crowd the moment.

He let Joey sit in it.

Then he said, "I am not putting you in the showcase today."

The disappointment flared in Joey before he could get a hand on it. Quick, bright, humiliating.

Duke saw that too.

"That wasn't a no," he said. "That was structure."

He nodded toward the stage.

"Thursday's where the room finds out whether it wants to keep your name in its mouth. Different crowd by ten, especially if they're waiting on Cass, but that's the lesson. The room doesn't lie. Not for long. Come in. Put your name on the list. Let the bones hear what you are when nobody from Nashville is in town and nobody's pretending this is the moment everything in their life becomes justified. Let me hear what you are when the room owes you nothing. That's closer to the truth anyway."

It sounded fair.

That was part of the machine too.

"And if I bring you five hundred," Joey said.

"Then we keep talking."

Duke flipped a page, then another, until he found the one he wanted. Joey caught names, clipped notes, stars, numbers, the whole thing kept in an order so neat it almost felt intimate. This was not merely accounting. This was architecture. Who got close. Who stayed in the margin. Who had paid enough to be taken seriously, and who still needed to want harder.

"This place is not a charity," Duke said. "And it is not a church anymore either, no matter what the ceiling thinks. But I am willing to be interested."

He looked up.

"You understand the difference?"

Joey wanted to say yes.

What came out was, "I think so."

"Good enough for today."

He uncapped the pen.

Joey watched the tip hover over the page.

For one sharp second he thought Duke was about to write him on an actual line with the collected names and totals. The official place. The real circle. The world where attention and commitment had already been exchanged.

Duke did not do that.

He wrote WOODSMAN in the margin.

Off to the side.

Not crossed out. Not confirmed. Just there in the white edge of the paper where possibilities and delays waited either to harden or vanish.

Joey felt the hope anyway.

That was the humiliating part.

Duke blew once on the ink, though it barely needed it.

"There," he said. "Now you've got something to live up to."

He stood and came around the table with both hands extended, full warmth returning, the whole Duke apparatus turned back down to human scale.

"Welcome to the Landmark, Joey Woodsman."

Joey stood too, because staying seated would have looked childish and because part of him wanted, against his better sense, to meet the gesture where it was offered.

Duke's hands were large and dry and closed around his with the odd authority of paperwork finalized through skin.

"Thanks," Joey said, and heard too much of himself in it.

Duke either noticed or had heard the same note in a hundred men before.

"Don't thank me," he said. "Go play. That's how you thank me."

Joey nodded once.

The stage sat empty behind Duke's shoulder. Chairs still up on tables. Terrence still mopping. Daylight still cutting through the front windows in hard slants that made every dust line in the air visible if you let your eyes go soft enough.

For all Duke's talk, the room had not chosen him.

Close enough to matter.

Off to the side enough to show the terms.

Joey stepped back from the table, coffee heat still in one hand and the image of his own name in the ledger margin already fixing itself somewhere meaner than memory.

He walked back out into the Atlanta afternoon with the number in his head, the pawn-shop envelope against his chest, and WOODSMAN written in a margin like a blessing that had learned bookkeeping.
