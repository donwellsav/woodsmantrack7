Pike's Clock

He heard the hallway before he smelled it.

A television somewhere behind one of the doors was laughing too hard at something canned. A baby one apartment over cried with the exhausted regularity of a metronome nobody had agreed to own. Somebody upstairs dragged a chair across the floor in long, punishing bursts that made the cheap plaster tick. Down by the stairwell, the building's busted window unit coughed, rattled, went nearly silent, then came back with the smoker's persistence of an old man too mean to die on schedule. Under all of it ran the city: a MARTA bus breathing out on the avenue, a motorcycle taking Moreland too hard, the thin far siren of somebody else's emergency moving south.

Then the smell got there.

Old grease reheated past dignity. Bleach too weak to win anything important. Wet carpet with a settled opinion about mildew. August in Atlanta had a way of warming every small human failure until it declared itself. Joey stood in the second-floor hall of a building that had probably looked respectable during the Carter administration and had since spent forty years losing one little argument after another with gravity, humidity, landlords, tenants, and whichever handyman had been cheapest that week.

His key never solved the door by itself.

The lock wanted its sequence. Lift, shove, turn. Sometimes shoulder. Sometimes a quiet curse if he had company, a louder one if he didn't. Tonight it needed the whole liturgy. By the time he got inside, his shirt had glued itself to his back in three new places and his temper had risen just enough to meet him there.

The apartment received him without surprise.

It always did. Joey half believed that if he came home one day carrying a horse or a casket or the whole brass section from a failed wedding band, the room would simply absorb the news by making less space for everything else. Nothing in it had enough slack left for astonishment.

The walk back from the park still sat in his body. Strap bite across one shoulder. Handle heat stored in his palm. The afterimage of Veritas beside the bench with that water bottle and that sentence still working under his ribs like it had paid rent in advance. Safe isn't yours. Little Five had thrown its usual evening racket at him the whole way home, fryer grease leaking out of restaurant doors, sweet vape clouds, brakes, bass from somebody's car, too many people walking like hurry itself might cool them down. He had made it home without spending anything, which ought to have felt disciplined and instead felt like the kind of postponement broke men learned to call virtue because they needed a cleaner word.

The first thing the place looked like, always, was sheet music.

Sheet music on the kitchen table in slanted stacks that had once been categories and were now mostly weather. Sheet music on the counter under an unopened gas bill, two clean forks, and a flyer he'd picked up because it said live music and then turned face down before he had to know where. Sheet music on the floor beside the milk-crate record player and the milk crates full of records, those at least still obeying a system only Joey could read. Gospel here. Folk there. Country with the thin blue thread of his father's preferences running through every crate no matter how Joey tried to sort around it. One paper sleeve leaned halfway out of a stack with his father's blocky pencil on the corner: capo 2, hold the last line.

Every flat surface had been conscripted. The room no longer held belongings so much as evidence. Not ruin. Ruin would've implied an event. This was slower than that. Paper. Cups. Sleeves. Picks. Envelopes. The long patient sediment of domestic things not getting better.

The sink held dishes old enough to feel permanent. A plate with dried yolk crusted at the rim. Two mugs. One fork welded by neglect to the bottom of a bowl. The fridge held hot sauce, two beers, half a loaf of bread hard enough to count as memory, and a plastic container of something that had crossed over from leftovers to warning.

The microwave blinked 12:00.

It had been blinking 12:00 since the power cut out three weeks ago in a storm that lasted eleven minutes and somehow managed to knock his whole block thirty years deeper into itself. Joey had meant to reset it that first night. Then the next morning it had reminded him of his mother after outages, going room to room and correcting every clock in the house like she could drag time back into line by hand if Georgia Power was too sorry to do it. He could still see her pressing buttons with the side of one thumb, muttering at the appliances as if they had personally chosen inconvenience. Since she wasn't here to fix this one, he had let it keep lying in public.

He set the guitar case by the kitchen wall and opened the outside pocket long enough to check the twenty.

Still there. Folded once. Separate from the rest of the money, exactly where he had left it in the park. The rent envelope sat inside too, red stamp and all, as if PAST DUE had ambitions beyond the mailbox and meant to follow him from room to room until it found itself a seat at the table.

He pulled the notice out and set it beside the notebook, where it instantly made the whole table look less like his and more like an office where bad news was processed.

Twenty-five dollars.

He didn't count it again. Counting belonged to the bench, where money still arrived with motion around it and hope could briefly mistake itself for arithmetic. Here the cash only sat and accused.

He set Veritas's water bottle on the counter beside the sink. It looked too sleek for the apartment, all clean steel and deliberate cold against old dishes and bad habits, like it had wandered into the wrong book. For a second it called up jelly jars of ice water on his mother's table in August, sweating rings into the oilcloth while his father tuned in the next room and pretended not to be listening for whether dinner was cheap enough to keep the week alive. The memory flashed and shut. Objects did that. They handed you a room from twenty years ago and took it back before you could sit down in it.

On the wall above the record player hung the photograph.

It was not a good photograph in any technical sense. Cheap print. Cheap frame. Ceiling too low in the room where it had been taken. Microphone probably feeding back. Audience too dark to read as anything but witness. But the man in the picture had his eyes shut and his mouth open and his guitar in his hands, and his hands knew where to go. That was the part Joey never got past. Not the resemblance. The resemblance was old news. Strangers still sometimes told him he had his father's face with the solemn satisfaction of people who thought likeness counted as comfort. No, it was the hands. The photograph had caught his father somewhere inside the work, somewhere music had taken him without asking permission, and the cruel thing about the picture was that it had done so permanently.

The frame itself came from a drugstore near the hospital. Joey had sliced his thumb on one of the metal tabs the night he snapped the backing shut. He remembered the clean bright sting of it, how angry it had made him that a frame could draw blood after the week had already taken more than its share. The cut had healed. The picture stayed.

Sometimes he could look at the photograph and think only of lineage, which was manageable. A craft handed down. A family habit. Something older than money and, for a minute, nobler than it. Other nights the picture caught him lower. Then it wasn't lineage. It was comparison. Proof on the wall that another Woodsman had stood in bad light with very little money and still managed to look claimed by his own hands. Joey never knew which version of the photograph he was coming home to until it was too late.

The frame hung crooked.

It had always hung crooked. Joey had straightened it once, about a month after the funeral, and by the next morning it had leaned again as if the wall itself objected to correction. After that he let it be. A man could spend his whole life trying to make one thing in a room sit right and still wake up to the same angle.

Tonight he stepped toward it and lifted two fingers toward the frame.

He got within an inch.

Then he let his hand drop.

He could have told himself he was too tired. He could have told himself it would only lean again by morning and he didn't feel like losing that tiny argument twice in one day. Both were probably true. Neither was the reason.

Touching the frame always risked turning the picture back into an event.

He turned away before it could.

The chair waited in its corner by the table. It had been in the apartment before Joey moved in and would still be there after he left because no force on earth was getting that chair down the stairs. The upholstery had once aimed at mustard and eventually arrived at nicotine. He sat in it the way a man sat in inherited trouble: because it was there and because replacing it sounded expensive.

He picked up the wooden guitar.

This hour was supposed to belong to honesty. The park took performance. The apartment was supposed to take whatever was left after the smiling, the history, and the safe repertoire got peeled off. That was the theory, anyway. Public hours for the old songs, private hours for the thing underneath them. Joey had been telling himself versions of that for so long the lie had acquired furniture.

He started "Wayfaring Stranger."

The room answered the old song immediately. The cheap frame gave a tiny sympathetic tick on the wall. One spoon in the sink trembled against a mug and settled into a faint bright rattle on certain notes. The window unit in the hall coughed again right off the downbeat as if even the building knew this arrangement by now. The apartment had heard this song enough to take it in without argument. It knew where the tune lived. It knew how to let Joey disappear inside it.

That was the comfort and the indictment.

The first verse went clean. So did the second. Somewhere under the third, the other thing pressed up through his fingers like a drunk trying to cut the line outside a bar. Not a full melody. Not something he could write down and defend in a room full of men who called themselves songwriters. Just the same stubborn turn he kept killing, the same pressure in his hand that felt less invented than uncovered.

The apartment reacted differently to that one.

Maybe only because he did. But the air seemed to draw up around the fragment. The old room noises stepped back half a pace. Even the spoon in the sink quit buzzing. For half a second the place felt newly awake, as if it had been listening politely all along and had just now heard the thing it was actually waiting on.

He stopped so hard the string snapped a short offended sound against the fret.

His own hands lay there on the guitar as if they'd been caught with somebody else's wallet.

"Really," he said to them.

No answer from the hands. No answer from the room either unless the microwave counted. 12:00. 12:00. 12:00.

The notebook lay open on the table where he'd left it the night before, or the night before that, or six failures ago. The page showed four starts and four careful sets of strike-throughs. Joey didn't slash lines out. He crossed them out neatly, line by line, like a man trying to make it look as if no violence had happened.

The lines embarrassed him in different ways.

One had tried for beauty too early and wound up sounding like somebody with central air and opinions about vinyl. One reached so hard for his father's plain authority it came off like costume, a boy in a dead man's jacket tugging the sleeves short. One had managed half a sentence of honesty before self-consciousness arrived and dressed it in language pretty enough to kill it. Another wasn't really words at all, just a knot of half-rhymes huddled together like men waiting outside a shelter before the door opened.

He could feel Veritas in his head against his will. Not her face. Worse. Her accuracy.

Maybe that's your problem.

Easy line in a park. Harder in bad light with a spiral notebook and a rent notice. As if the song were already at the threshold and he was the one holding the latch shut.

He set the guitar across his lap and stared at the notice.

PAST DUE.

The phrase had gone beyond meaning and become design. It sat on the page in red block letters next to the office address and the exact amount he didn't have. Joey's whole adult life had begun to feel like a series of envelopes informing him that time had moved on without asking whether he was coming.

His stomach gave one mean little twist at the sight of it, as if body and paper were in active correspondence. He tried to remember the last full meal he'd eaten without counting first and came up humiliatingly blank. Eggs two mornings ago, maybe three. Toast if bread that stale still counted. Coffee enough to make his face look fed from a distance. He thought of the half loaf in the fridge and was suddenly back in his mother's kitchen again, watching her cut the heel off bread gone hard and soak it in milk because wasting food felt like blasphemy in a house where every dollar had already been assigned. The memory lasted exactly as long as it took him to look away.

The twenty in the outside pocket of the case seemed to glow through the vinyl in his imagination.

He thought about spending it on groceries just to strip it of whatever meaning Veritas had hung on it. Eggs. Ground beef. Gas station aspirin. Bread that was still interested in the future. Make the money common. Turn judgment into lunch.

Instead he sat there with the guitar across his thighs and listened to the building make itself around him.

The fragment moved again at the edge of his left hand.

He reached for the notebook before he could talk himself out of it and wrote six words.

By the seventh, the sentence had gone false.

He crossed the whole thing out.

Another try. Worse. This one started plain and then stiffened up halfway through, like a man remembering too late he was being watched. He crossed that out too. By the third attempt the page had become an argument. Inherited songs entered already absolved. Anything new had to meet his father, the rent, Veritas, and his own bad faith all at once.

He shoved the notebook away hard enough to make it rasp across the table and send a loose page of sheet music drifting to the floor.

That was when the pounding started.

Not a knock. Not even a series of knocks. The flat sound of a palm against cheap wood with administrative fury behind it.

"Woodsman. Open the door."

Joey froze without dignity. His body knew the voice before his mind supplied the name.

He didn't move.

The pounding came again.

"I can hear you breathing in there, Woodsman. The walls are thin. Everything in this building is thin. Open the goddamn door."

Joey closed his eyes once, set the guitar carefully against the wall, and stood.

The door needed its sequence again. Lift, shove, turn. Tonight the kick landed at the end of it with more feeling than was strictly required.

Gerald Pike filled the doorway like a grievance that had learned to collect rent.

Late sixties, maybe older. Built out of old disappointments, rental income, and the conviction that a clipboard in the hand made any conversation official. Joey would've mocked the clipboard if the clipboard had not, in fact, done exactly that.

Pike's shirt was tucked too hard. His belt rode high and punitive. His face held the permanent pinched look of a man who had spent thirty years discovering new forms of tenant logic and taking each one as a private insult. The hall light behind him made the scalp at his temples show through the thin white hair.

"You know what today is," Pike said.

Joey leaned against the frame because standing up straight suggested reserves he did not have. "Tuesday."

Pike didn't blink.

"It's the fifteenth. Rent was due on the first. You know what happens between the first and the fifteenth in the state of Georgia when rent isn't paid?"

Joey said nothing. He had learned that if you let Pike ask his own rhetorical questions, Pike would eventually answer them for the pleasure of precision.

Pike lifted the clipboard half an inch, exactly on cue.

"A clock starts," he said. "A legal clock. A clock I did not build and do not control but am legally entitled to wind. That clock is ticking, Woodsman. It has been ticking fourteen days. In another sixteen days, I can file. You know what filing means?"

Joey looked past him down the hallway to where the broken window unit was still rattling out its death speech by the stairs.

"Eviction."

"Eviction." Pike nodded once, almost pleased. "The word itself. You said it. I didn't have to."

Then Pike looked past Joey into the apartment.

That was always the worst part. Not the demand. The inventory.

His eyes moved over the sheet music, the dishes, the guitar against the wall with its scuffed shoulder and old glue line catching the hall light, the mug holding dead pens and loose picks, the blinking microwave, the bottle on the counter too nice for the room. The look paused at the photograph and stayed there a beat too long.

"Is that your dad?"

Joey hated the softness trying to hide inside the question. Hated that Pike noticed the picture at all.

"Yeah."

"He a musician too?"

"He was."

Pike kept looking at the photograph.

"Uh-huh," he said. "And did he pay his rent?"

There were half a dozen answers available, all of them bad. My father is dead. My father could fill a room you couldn't afford to enter. My father left me a guitar instead of a savings account, so congratulations to all concerned. What came out was nothing.

Pike finally looked back at him.

"I'm gonna ask you a question," he said, "and I want you to hear it the way I mean it, which is as a man with a financial interest in you staying alive. Are you eating?"

The sheer wrongness of it made Joey laugh once under his breath.

"I'm fine."

"That's not what I asked."

Pike shifted the clipboard to his other hand. Joey noticed then that the old man's fingernails were clean. Meticulously clean. It seemed like exactly the kind of detail Pike would consider evidence in his own favor. The question hung there between them, uglier than the rent talk because it required more from Joey than a number.

"I asked if you're eating," Pike said. "You look like you lost ten pounds since June and you didn't have ten pounds to lose."

The hallway behind him carried up a thin ribbon of cigarette smoke from the stairwell. Joey became aware of the apartment from Pike's side of the threshold. The stale coffee. The bad food situation. The sheet music everywhere trying to pass for purpose. The photograph on the wall like a witness statement. One thing to live in a room arranged that way. Another to be observed in it.

"I said I'm fine."

"No," Pike said. "You said words in that order."

If Pike had sounded tender, Joey might have had to throw him down the stairs on principle. But the old man sounded merely exact, and exactness was harder to fight because it stood too close to truth.

"I'm not a monster, Woodsman," Pike said. "I'm a man who owns a building. Those are different things, even if they don't always feel different."

He tapped the clipboard once against his thigh.

"End of the month," he said. "The clock is ticking. I can't stop it even if I wanted to."

Then, after the smallest beat:

"And I'm not sure I want to."

That was more like it. Whatever concern Pike possessed only made it across the threshold after ownership had translated it.

Pike turned and started down the hall.

His footsteps were heavy and even. No rush in them. Men who believed in clocks didn't hurry. Time did their work for them.

Joey stood in the doorway and watched until Pike turned the corner by the stairs and disappeared.

The television behind the wall laughed again. Somebody flushed. The building resumed being a building.

He shut the door.

This time the lock caught on the second shove.

Joey leaned his forehead against the cheap wood for a second, then slid down until he was sitting on the floor with his back to it. The apartment felt hotter from down there. Or smaller. Hard to tell which pressures belonged to architecture and which ones belonged to panic.

The guitar was two feet away against the wall.

He didn't reach for it.

He looked at the rent notice on the table. Then the photograph. Then his own hands in his lap.

They looked ordinary enough. Long-fingered. Dry at the knuckles. Thin gloss of callus at the tips. The hands of a man whose one dependable skill was a skill the world had not figured out how to pay for unless it could put a register near it and sell drinks in the dark. The hands in the photograph had probably looked ordinary too. That was part of the trouble. Joey had spent years turning his father into a category because categories were easier to inherit than people.

Across the room, the case leaned where he'd left it, the outside pocket hiding the folded twenty like a secret he didn't respect. Twenty-five dollars total. Sixteen days until Pike could file. A refrigerator containing almost nothing that wanted to become dinner. The math was grade-school simple. That was one of the ugliest things about poverty. People liked to act as if the problem were complexity. Most days it was arithmetic plain enough to slap you.

Pike's question stayed after the man himself was gone.

Are you eating?

Joey hated that it had landed. Hated that Pike had noticed the sharpened wrists, the fallen-in face, the way coffee had been doing work food was supposed to do. Hated even more that part of him had heard something almost like concern in the question and had not known whether to trust it or resent it. Probably both. Both was a crowded neighborhood in Joey.

His eyes went back to the photograph.

Not as music this time. As a man. As a body in a cheap frame who had somehow managed to look fully elsewhere while still standing in a room full of people. Joey had spent years calling that devotion. Tonight it looked too close to escape.

The notebook sat where he'd shoved it, page scarred with new strike-throughs over old ones.

The microwave kept blinking 12:00, false noon over a room where Pike's sixteen days had already started keeping the truer time.

Outside, a siren moved somewhere south and then got smaller. A car stereo washed briefly through the window with too much bass and a hook Joey almost recognized before it passed. The city kept going. Buying cigarettes. Missing trains. Making it to night with whatever story people had on hand.

After a long time, Joey pushed himself back to his feet.

He took the guitar up carefully, as if picking up something alive but tired, and carried it back to the chair. He did not reopen the notebook. Did not touch the rent notice. Did not straighten the frame.

He put his fingers where they had gone a thousand times before. The neck fit his hand. The old glue ridge found his thumb. Across the room the rent notice waited on the table, and under everything Pike's numbers kept ticking.

Then he started "Wayfaring Stranger" from the beginning.

Because beginning again was the only move he still trusted against a clock already running.
