Buy-In

The Landmark sounded unfinished when Joey came back through the door.

Not quiet. Never that. More like caught between faces. Somebody onstage tightened a snare, loosened it, tightened it again. Ice crashed into a metal bin behind the bar. A stool leg dragged too far, and the floor answered with a dry complaint like an old church member denied the right to leave early. Under the low work lights, a guitar struck the same bright chord three times. Each time the room took a little lacquer off it and sent it back smaller. Before customers, the place told the truth in a different accent. Less testimony. More labor.

He stayed just inside the threshold long enough for the cold air to find the sweat at the back of his neck.

Moreland still burned behind him. Brick, bus exhaust, rain-smell without rain. Inside was bleach under old beer, fryer grease not awake yet, the hum in the walls, and Duke's air-conditioning fighting August every time the door opened.

The paper bag warmed one hand through the grease. The coffee warmed the other through thin cardboard. The envelope lay flat inside his shirt, left of center, body-warm by now, like he'd tucked a bad spare heart under his ribs and it had picked a meaner rhythm. All week it had kept changing shape against him. Pawn-shop counter. Kitchen drawer. Pike's notice on the table. The wooden guitar by the chair, useless from across the room. The silver guitar's tuning pegs catching a pale patient glint from the bedroom doorway whenever he bent to count the money and make sure it had not walked off on its own. His body kept putting every paper rectangle in one family and calling it danger.

Veritas's voice had not improved with distance.

Now you're deciding what you'll pay for permission.

He hated that sentence more indoors than he had at the cart.

Probably because the room gave it furniture.

Duke sat at his corner table with the ledger open, glasses low on his nose, calculator by one elbow, cold coffee by the other. He looked less like a villain than a man balancing books for a church he still half believed in. A yellow lamp lit one half of the table. The other half sat in church-dark. Even from here Joey could smell the old leather of the ledger, oil and paper and other people's hands.

He saw Joey and smiled at once.

"There he is. You look like a man carrying supper and a felony."

Joey went to the bar first. Set the bag and coffee down. Peeled back the lid and finished the last swallow because walking into the real part with both hands full would have made it easier to pretend he still had not decided. The coffee had gone from hot to useful. He folded the bag down once and left it by his elbow.

Onstage, the same guitar tried a little run this time. Too many notes. Too much wrist. No weight behind any of it. The room flattened the whole thing into a bright harmless sentence.

Then he turned.

Duke watched him come over with the patient interest of a man who liked seeing decisions walk toward him.

"You got a minute?" Joey asked.

"For you? Depends what it costs."

"Apparently five hundred."

Duke liked that on a sentence level. Joey could see it, and hated himself for feeding it.

Duke leaned back and tipped his chin at the empty chair across from him. "Sit down, Woodsman."

Joey stayed standing.

If he sat, he would start explaining. Men like Duke always did better when you helped narrate your own compromise. Joey had already paid four dollars for dinner and a full week of indigestion. He was not about to hand over the softer parts for free.

"I'm here about the showcase," he said.

Duke's face changed by a hair. Not surprise. A finer edge. The host slid away and the room manager took his place without even pretending it was a different man.

"I figured that might be where your feet were headed."

Joey nodded. Slipped his fingers inside his shirt. Felt the paper there, warm from his skin. He drew out the envelope and laid it on the table between the ledger and the cold coffee.

Small move.

It still landed like he had thrown something.

Duke looked down at the envelope, then back up at Joey.

Around them the room kept piecing itself together. Glass clinked behind the bar. Somebody laughed too hard by the kitchen door. The fryer gave one dry cough, not ready to commit to grease yet. Onstage the guitar player stopped, tried one lower chord, and the room let that one hang a fraction longer, like it respected restraint more than talent.

Joey kept his hand on the envelope.

"That's the buy-in," he said.

Duke didn't touch it.

"You sure?"

He asked it gently enough that if Joey backed out Duke could call himself decent, and if Joey went through with it Duke could call the whole thing consent.

"No," Joey said. "I'm not sure about any of it. That's not the same thing."

Something like approval crossed Duke's face and got turned to management before it could settle.

"Fair answer."

Joey slid the envelope an inch closer and opened the flap himself.

The bills inside were still the same dead hundreds the pawn man had counted out under fluorescent tubes. For a second Joey saw the whole place again: the old man's nicotine thumb, the rag moving in slow possessive circles over the gold body, the finish looking stupid and holy under that bad light while the man did resale math in real time. A thing that should have stayed in the category of fable had turned into counted paper in under ten minutes. Atlanta was efficient that way. Put a counter between a miracle and a man with rent due, and the city knew exactly what shape the miracle would take by closing time.

He took out five bills.

Set them down one at a time.

The sound was barely sound at all. Paper on wood. Still, each one hit him like a bolt going home.

One hundred.

Another.

Another.

Another.

Another.

Five hundred dollars from a guitar that should never have existed, laid down on a table where Duke had once written WOODSMAN in the margin like possibility and delay were two line items in the same book.

Each bill still carried a life in it. Groceries. Bus fare. Three more days before Pike could file. Strings for the wooden guitar waiting by the chair at home. Three or four mornings of opening the fridge without having to bargain with himself first. All of it got pressed flat into one fee.

Joey looked at the money, not Duke.

"There."

Duke took off his glasses.

Not slowly. Not for effect. He just seemed to want nothing between his face and what Joey had set in front of him.

"Where'd this come from?"

Joey let out one short breath through his nose.

It was not a funny question. It was just one of the few left.

"You want the long version or the true one."

"Dealer's choice."

Joey raised his eyes.

"I had a gold guitar."

Saying it out loud in this room made it sound less like a miracle than a symptom.

Duke went very still. On most men stillness meant absence. On Duke it meant everything had narrowed to one point.

"You had," he said carefully, "a gold guitar."

"I had a gold guitar."

Onstage, somebody pushed a chorus line into the mic and leaned too hard on feeling. The room sent it back thin and nasal, kept the consonants, dropped the ache.

"And you sold it."

"I sold it."

Duke's eyes dropped to the five hundreds on the table, then rose again.

"To buy into my showcase."

Joey could have spread the blame around there. Rent. Food. Bus fare. The accumulated stupidity of wanting badly enough to make yourself available to manipulation. He didn't.

"To buy into your showcase," he said.

The line sat between them.

Recognition crossed Duke's face then. Not guilt. Joey had never seen Duke show any sign of carrying guilt as a native instrument. This was something meaner and closer than that. The look of a man realizing somebody had finally committed for real. Not romantically. Not rhetorically. Materially. A kid had turned miracle into cash, cash into tribute, and brought it to Duke's table with both eyes open.

For one bad second Joey saw himself from above. Twenty-six years old. Shirt damp between his shoulder blades. Sandwich cooling at the bar. Five hundred dollars on another man's table so a room might look his way later under lights. Not for a guarantee. Just for entry. Just for the right to stand where the room could decide if it cared.

It was a humiliating angle.

It was the true one.

"Well," Duke said softly.

He put the glasses back on, reached out, and touched the edge of the nearest bill with one finger, as if checking whether it still held heat.

"That," he said, "is one way to answer a room."

Joey almost said Something in me hates you enough to walk out right now.

What he said instead was, "I didn't bring it for your poetry."

"No." Duke smiled a little. "You brought it for my paperwork."

He gathered the five bills into a stack and tapped them once on the tabletop to square the edges. Then he opened the ledger wider to the showcase page.

The old leather smell rose stronger. With it came one quick useless image of Clarence's record sleeve slouched by the turntable at home, side B waiting in it like a question written in pencil, and his father's milk crate in the closet behind it, sleeves worn white at the corners, church programs and browned flyers shoved between albums because paper proof was still proof after the night itself was gone. A room. A date. A name in print. Enough to make absence look organized.

Joey knew that page before he really saw it. He'd been seeing it since Duke first wrote his name off to the side, though in memory it mostly lived as his last name taking up provisional space. When Duke turned the book a little and ran a finger down the column, Joey caught enough to feel the shape of what he was entering. Names. Amounts. Little notes in Duke's hand. All the clerical ways a dream got turned into something a room could invoice.

One line still sat open.

That mattered more than it had any right to.

Duke found WOODSMAN in the margin.

Joey felt his own body go alert at the sight of it, like the name wasn't his but belonged to somebody he had been sent to identify in a morgue.

Duke uncapped his pen.

For a second he held there.

The pause was short. Real. Joey saw that. Duke wasn't milking suspense. He was looking at the name in the margin, the empty line below, the five hundred dollars from the gold guitar, and making whatever private peace he made with the difference between tending a room and owning one.

Behind the bar, a case of bottles hit the counter hard. Onstage, the guitar player finally found a progression with less glitter in it, and the room gave the notes a little more body. Even half built, the Landmark was rude about honesty.

Then the pen moved.

Not in the margin.

On the line.

The scratch of it sounded louder than it had any right to.

Duke wrote carefully, the way some men signed deeds, some men wrote baptismal names, some men addressed invoices they planned to collect by hand if they had to. When he finished, WOODSMAN no longer lived off to the side in all that provisional white space. It sat in the last slot.

Official enough to stain.

Joey felt something in his chest tighten and settle at the same time.

The door costs what it costs.

That did not make it clean.

Duke looked up.

"You're in, Joey."

No flourish. That made it hit harder.

"You're in the showcase."

Joey heard it and felt the whole ugly week close around the words: Mae warning him about the wrong door, Cass insisting real things still happened in crooked places, Veritas refusing to confuse truth with virtue. None of that changed the line his name sat on now.

He looked at it anyway and didn't step back from what he'd done.

"I know."

Duke studied him for a beat. Maybe expecting gratitude. Maybe relief. Maybe one of those young-man expressions that made gatekeepers feel fatherly instead of merely central.

Joey gave him none of those.

Not because he'd suddenly turned noble. The money had just burned the extra theater out of him.

"You understand," Duke said, "that once I write you there, we're not playing at maybe anymore."

"Seems like that stopped a while ago."

Duke huffed a laugh.

"Fair enough."

He slid the bills back into the envelope, slower than Joey liked, and tucked the envelope between the ledger pages before folding the leather shut over it. Then he laid one broad hand on top of the book.

At first glance the gesture read like blessing.

Look at it a second longer and it read like possession.

"Call time next Friday," Duke said. "Be here early. Earlier than you think. Don't drink stupid. Don't disappear. Don't bring me a crisis that wants special handling. If you panic, panic quietly."

Joey almost smiled in spite of himself. "That's the orientation packet."

"No." Duke leaned back. "That's me being kind for free. Orientation costs extra."

The line was polished from use. Still, Duke had earned that one.

Joey glanced toward the stage. Empty now except for cables, mic stands, and the low pre-show shape of work. Somebody had switched from the bright guitar to a warmer one, or maybe the same player had finally stopped trying to impress the air. A few chords rolled out, and the room held them instead of spitting them back. Not applause. Not approval. Just wood and air agreeing for a second. That was what kept wrecking every clean argument Joey tried to build against the place. Crooked or not, it still answered.

He hated how little the truth divided anymore.

"You still believe he listens," Joey said.

Duke did not ask who.

"Randall?" He lifted one shoulder. "The man's got ears. That's why it matters when he shows up."

"And the rest of it."

"The rest of it," Duke said, "is how a room survives long enough to put the right ears in the right seat on the right night."

There it was again. The thing Duke did better than almost anybody Joey knew. He could tell the truth and the hustle in the same breath and make separating them feel childish.

Joey looked at the ledger under Duke's hand.

"You ever get tired of hearing yourself justify this."

"No." Duke didn't hesitate. "I get tired of people wanting the room and pretending the room shouldn't cost them."

He never raised his voice. He didn't need to. Men like Duke almost never shouted their philosophy. They stated it like weather and expected everybody else to dress for it.

Joey thought of the five bills inside the ledger. Thought of the gold guitar becoming a pawn ticket, then an envelope, then a line item in Duke's book. Thought of how fast Atlanta could turn wonder into paperwork if there was a counter nearby and a man in front of it willing to stay practical. Thought of what his father might have said about rooms like this, about men who sold witness at a premium, about the way a city could love music and still ask for cover first. He did not yet know how much he didn't know. He only knew the space his father left kept putting pressure behind other people's sentences.

He dragged his eyes off the ledger.

"If this turns out to be bullshit," he said, "I want the dignity of having predicted it correctly."

Duke smiled. Warm as ever. Still as stone.

"That'll be yours free of charge."

Joey nodded. The movement felt smaller than commitment ought to feel. Maybe that was right. Most commitments happened by hand, not by trumpet.

He turned from the table, went back to the bar, and picked up the folded bag. The sandwich had gone lukewarm. The coffee was almost cold. He took both anyway, because leaving food behind after paying for it offended him more cleanly than paying Duke did, which was useful self-knowledge if not flattering.

At the door he looked back once.

Duke already had the glasses back on. Already had his eyes down on the page. Already looked welded back into the machinery.

Joey stepped out into the Atlanta night with the room at his back and his name on the line.

The air outside had not cooled so much as changed its argument. Cars hissed along Moreland. A MARTA bus sighed at the stop and pulled off. The storm smell had thickened without giving anybody relief. Across the street somebody laughed too loud outside one of the bars. Somebody else cursed at an engine that didn't want to turn over. Heat stayed in the sidewalk like a grudge.

He stood there with the paper bag in one hand and the nearly dead coffee in the other and felt not cleaner, just tied in. He had paid a man he did not fully trust with money that should not have existed so a room that was partly honest and partly crooked might one night turn its head his way. He knew exactly what sentence that made. He had walked into it anyway.

He started walking.

Somewhere between the first block and the second, the fragment came up. Not played. Not hummed. Just a shape in his mouth, the turn of it testing the corner of a word that had no author yet. For two steps he let it sit there. Then the old reflex got to his throat and the shape went back where it came from, and by the third step he was walking to the rhythm of a song his father had taught him instead.

Behind him, in the half-lit room, Duke waited until the door shut and Joey's footsteps were no longer part of the building's weather.

Then he opened the ledger again.

The envelope sat between the pages where he had left it. He took it out, thumbed the edge of the bills, and counted the five hundreds with the same practiced economy he brought to every other ugly necessity. The paper still held a little of Joey's body heat. Most things in Duke's reach seemed to pick up body heat whether they belonged there or not.

He slid the money back inside, turned to the showcase page, and looked at the names.

WOODSMAN sat on the last line now, no longer floating in the margin where a hard choice could still pretend it was provisional. Higher on the page, Cass's name already carried one of Duke's private marks, the kind nobody out front ever got to argue with because they never knew it existed. He drew one neat box around her intro cue and set the pen down.

The room kept building itself around him. Ice. Glass. Wiring hum. A chair leg scraping, then correcting itself. Somewhere onstage the warmer guitar found three notes in a row that belonged together, and the room let them stay.

Duke sat with his hand on the ledger a moment longer, on money a twenty-six-year-old kid had made by selling a gold guitar so he could step onto a stage whose architecture had been leaning one direction long before he reached the door.

Then he lifted the cold coffee and finished it without changing expression.
