The Twenty

The sentence lay between them long enough to stop sounding like an insult and start sounding like a diagnosis.

Joey looked at Veritas as if some late-arriving sign might appear on her face and explain what kind of woman she was. Joking. Hustling. Flirting badly. Selling religion. Crazy in some civic, nameable way a man could step around. He would have taken any of those. Categories were merciful. Categories had rules. A woman in a park who told him the safe song was not his, then stood there as if the rest of the thought belonged to him to finish, did not come with rules. She came with pressure.

She only stood beside the bench with the water bottle in one hand and that old-young face turned toward him in patient refusal, like she had said nothing provocative at all. Like she had only mentioned the weather.

The park kept going. A phone rang somewhere with one of those cheerful marimba tones that made every emergency sound vaguely sponsored. A skateboard snapped over a crack in the walk. The man who had earlier been arguing with a pigeon had evidently lost and was now trying diplomacy in the tone of a wounded statesman. Heat came off the path in a new wave and licked the inside of Joey's elbows like the day checking whether he was done yet.

He looked away from Veritas because there were only so many seconds a man could stand being seen too accurately before self-preservation started dragging furniture in front of the door.

That was when he saw the twenty.

It sat half under two tired dollar bills and a small fan of coins in the open guitar case below the curve of the wooden guitar on his lap, too crisp for the rest of the money, too green, too clean. Harmony Park cash usually came softened by pocket sweat, laundromat heat, bar change, and indecision. This bill looked as if it had never been folded in anger. It had the bright, alarming look of intention.

His right forearm still rested on the guitar's warm lower bout. The old glue ridge pressed lightly into the heel of his hand. Wood under one arm. Crisp paper in the case. Cold metal bottle in hers. The morning had started sorting itself into categories he did not trust.

He bent and picked up the bill.

"This yours?"

Veritas glanced at it, then back at him. "No."

"See, that's exactly the problem."

He held it by one corner as if it might contaminate the rest of the money by contact. Once Joey started talking to avoid something, words bred words. The trick was to sound entertaining enough that people mistook the smoke for weather instead of defense.

"A twenty doesn't just show up out here," he said. "A dollar shows up. That's the standard public rate for guilt in a park. Two if somebody's feeling expansive. Five if I actually got them. Ten is unusual enough that I spend the next half hour wondering if they confused me with somebody doing useful work."

The two office women under the crape myrtle were still lingering just enough to hear while pretending not to. Joey lowered his voice a little, not enough to turn private.

"But a twenty?" He gave the bill a small disbelieving shake. "On a weekday morning. In August. In this park. That's not a tip. That's a statement. That's a note slid under the door."

Veritas took a sip from the bottle and swallowed as if nothing in the exchange had risen above ordinary traffic.

"Or," she said, "somebody heard something worth twenty dollars."

He laughed. It came out thinner than he meant it to.

"Ain't nobody in Harmony Park at ten in the morning worth twenty dollars."

"Maybe that's your problem."

The answer came so flat and fast it nearly knocked the humor clean out of him. He crouched and slid the bill into the outside pocket of the case, the pocket where he kept things he had not yet agreed to trust. Receipts. Loose picks. The occasional business card from somebody who promised to call and never did. The emotional quarantine zone.

"I'm not mixing that in yet," he said. "That would be irresponsible bookkeeping."

Before he could close the zipper, Veritas bent, plucked the bill out with two fingers, and dropped it back into the main pile.

Joey straightened. "Hey."

"Take the money, Joey."

It would have irritated him less if she had sounded charitable. There was no charity in her voice. No benefactor's glow. She said it the way she had said ninety-four, the way a person named a fact that stayed true whether anybody liked it or not.

He heard his own name a beat late.

"How do you know my name?"

That wrong-old smile touched her mouth again. Not wider. Just older.

"It's on your case," she said. "Under the sticker."

He looked.

Of course it was.

JOEY WOODSMAN, written in Sharpie years ago, the black now gone tea-brown and thin from weather. Still there if anyone actually bothered to look. The HARMONY OR BUST sticker above it had nearly bleached itself into a ghost, but the name kept hanging on with embarrassing determination.

He saw the afternoon he had written it then, quick and mean as a paper cut. Kitchen floor. Case between his knees. August light in the apartment, yellow and punishing. Fresh marker in his hand. Twenty years old and not yet fully educated by rent. He had written the name slowly, trying on seriousness the way some boys tried on jackets. A named case had felt like the sort of thing a person owned when he was headed somewhere. Green rooms. Vans. Load-ins. Maybe not glory exactly, but paperwork moving in that direction.

He had forgotten the arrogance of it. Or maybe not arrogance. Hope in block letters.

He had spent two years sitting in public with his own name at his feet and almost nobody had used it.

That landed in two places at once. Relief, because anonymity kept a man from having to explain himself beyond the tune in progress. Shame, because anonymity was only the polite word for invisibility when nobody had asked whether he wanted it.

Veritas had stepped around that whole arrangement like she had seen the chair in the dark everybody else kept banging a shin on.

"I was standing right there," she said. "I heard the set."

Her eyes moved from the case to his hands to his face and back again, not quick enough to be polite, not slow enough to feel possessive. Exact was the only word for it. Exactness tired him out.

"And yeah," she said. "You were worth twenty dollars. Now take the damn money."

What bothered him was not really the amount. Twenty dollars was useful in the small, humiliating way money often was. Twenty dollars was eggs, rice, coffee, and whatever canned thing was on sale if he quit pretending nutrition had a moral dimension. Twenty dollars was two days of not having to choose between lunch and the dignity of acting like he wasn't thinking about lunch. Twenty dollars was the kind of amount that could change a Tuesday without improving a life.

He understood money on that scale. Scale, not fantasy.

What he did not understand was being evaluated with conviction.

People tipped for reasons that barely involved him. They tipped because the weather was merciful or cruel, because they had just fought with somebody, because old songs made them miss a grandfather they had not called enough while he was alive, because a park created the illusion that generosity counted extra in daylight. That was the contract. Joey gave them something familiar enough to move them and distant enough not to obligate them. They gave him enough cash to keep both parties from having to name what the exchange actually was.

This felt different.

This felt like being found.

And finding implied inventory.

"You make it sound very official," he said. "Did you bring a scorecard? Tone, phrasing, unauthorized evidence of personhood?"

"You'd hate the scorecard."

"I hate most scorecards. That's why I never went to grad school."

She let that pass as if it had not met the minimum size requirement for a real thought.

"You're acting like the money's the strange part," she said.

Joey looked at her. "Lady, you came out of nowhere with a bag that can't hold what keeps coming out of it and told me I play my dead father's songs like a shelter. I am triaging the available weirdness as best I can."

That almost got a laugh out of her. Almost. It crossed her face as a brief loosening rather than amusement, as if some older and more patient person inside her had acknowledged that he was, at least, trying.

"The money isn't strange," she said. "You are."

"Now that's rude."

"No. That's accurate."

He crouched again mostly to give his hands a task. Counting money was concrete. Counting money never asked a man what he meant. One single. Two. Three. Four. Five, if he counted the twenty, which he did not want to do and did anyway.

Twenty-five dollars.

His body noticed before his pride corrected it. Relief moved through him quick and humiliating as a shiver. Twenty-five dollars meant eggs, rice, bread that had not already started going sweet around the edges, maybe aspirin. It did not mean rent. It did not even mean the red stamp in the envelope at his feet would have to lower its voice. But it was enough to make the day briefly look negotiable.

Veritas saw that pass through him too. Of course she did.

"There," she said, quiet now. "Now it looks like the truth."

"No," Joey said. "Now it looks like luck."

"You like luck better."

"Luck doesn't ask follow-up questions."

That got the wrong-old smile in full.

Wrong because it did not belong to a face that young. Wrong because it looked more patient than the morning, more patient than Atlanta, more patient than one broke musician trying not to get spiritually audited before lunch.

The attraction in him sharpened then, which annoyed him on principle. There should have been a civic penalty for wanting a woman who talked like a deposition.

He reached for the water bottle mostly to break eye contact. The cold struck his palm hard enough to feel suspicious. Not cool. Not maybe-it-came-from-ice-a-while-ago. Cold. Fresh cold. Atlanta in August heated every object into local citizenship eventually. This bottle felt as if it had crossed a border illegally.

"You trying to poison me?" he asked.

"If I was going to poison you, I wouldn't choose water. You'd suspect the health benefits."

He snorted before he could stop himself. The cap cracked loose. He drank.

The water was so cold it almost hurt. It ran down his throat and into his chest like a clean lie. For one second he was in his mother's kitchen as a boy, only because the cold struck the same place. A jelly jar of tap water after mowing somebody else's yard for cash. Condensation on his fingers. His mother saying, Slow down, you'll make yourself sick, and his father from the other room saying, Let him drink, the boy's part mule. Then the memory blinked out. Objects did that. They flared and shut. If you wanted a whole past, you had to build it yourself, and Joey distrusted any memory that arrived too complete.

When he lowered the bottle, the guitar across his thighs felt warmer than before, old wood and old glue pushing back against the bottle's clean metal cold.

Veritas watched him drink with the same expression she had watched him play. Not pleased. Not possessive. Merely attentive enough to make the act feel public.

"That's the other part that bothers you," she said.

He lowered the bottle. "What other part."

"That I heard it and didn't ask permission first."

He let the silence answer for him a second. A bus sighed at the curb out on Moreland. Somebody dropped coins in his case and kept walking without interrupting their call. The city did not pause to offer privacy just because a conversation had become inconvenient.

"People don't usually do that," he said finally.

"Listen?"

"No." The word came out cleaner than most things he had said to her. "Hear."

He wished at once he had wrapped it in a joke. Too late.

She did not soften. He was beginning to understand that her refusal to soften was not the same thing as cruelty. That made her harder to dismiss.

"Most people hear what they're paying for," Joey said. "Or what lets them feel decent for thirty seconds on the way somewhere else. They hear the church-basement part. The old-song part. The dead-daddy part if they're built that way. They don't usually hear..." He gestured once with the bottle and stopped. Naming the shape felt too close to naming the thing. "Whatever that was."

"Because you don't leave it out long enough."

"Maybe because it isn't done."

"Nothing's done when it first asks."

He rubbed a thumb over the ridged plastic cap. Out on the avenue a motorcycle went by loud enough to shake a few leaves loose from a tired branch. The park around them had resumed its weekday breathing. A man in a Braves cap dropped two ones into the case without looking down. A dog sniffed one of Joey's boots, found no story there, and moved on.

All that ordinary motion made the moment stranger. If the sky had opened or the fountain had started speaking Greek, he could have filed the whole encounter under episode and been done. But the world kept committing to groceries, exhaust, pigeon politics, and sunburn. Only Veritas refused the scale.

"You talk like you know something about making songs," he said.

"I know what it sounds like when somebody sends one back."

"That's not an answer."

"Didn't say it was."

He almost asked what she was. The question rose all the way to his teeth and stopped there. He had the strong, unreasonable sense that asking directly would cost him a kind of cover he still needed. Better to stay in sarcasm, where at least he knew the street names.

"So what then," he said. "You patrol parks for underemployed men committing acts of suppressed originality?"

"No."

"Good. Because the city's already saturated with consultants."

"I was walking through the park."

"And happened to find me."

"No." She nodded once toward his hands. "I found what you pushed down."

He looked at his fingers, then at the bottle, then away. The glue ridge on the guitar's lower bout sat under his thumb where he had set the instrument back across his lap. His father had once slapped that same hand away from a lazy chord and said, Hands know before you do. Joey had hated the line then because it sounded mystical and smug. He hated remembering it now because memory liked putting old sentences back into circulation at the worst possible time.

He bent, took the twenty out again, and folded it once. Careful. Unwilling. He slid it back into the outside pocket, where exceptional things went to wait for a later verdict.

"There," he said. "Happy? I accepted the bribe."

"It's not a bribe."

"Then it's hush money."

"That doesn't even make sense."

"Most of my better theories don't."

He patted the pocket once as if settling a restless animal.

Quarantined again.

Veritas glanced at the pocket but did not reach for the bill this time. That unsettled him too. It meant the point had never been where the money sat. The point had only been whether he would keep insisting it had arrived by mistake.

She tipped her chin toward the guitar.

"Play."

It sounded just enough like an instruction for his first answer to arrive on reflex.

"You don't work here."

"Neither do you, technically."

"That's hurtful and also legally interesting."

The wrong-old smile came and went.

Then she stepped back from the bench, not far, only enough to return him to the arrangement the park recognized: Joey on the bench, guitar in lap, case open, heat climbing, city moving around him in impatient loops.

"Drink the water," she said.

She turned and walked across the grass toward the path. No flourish. No smoke. No theatrics designed to help him file the event. She just moved with that same wrong-angle certainty she had arrived with, and then bodies crossing the sidewalk cut her from his sight for half a second, and when the view opened again the geometry no longer added up.

Gone.

Too clean for a normal exit. Too ordinary for a miracle he could admit to.

He stood before he meant to, craning for another glimpse. He caught maybe a flash of light in her hair by the curb, or maybe only sun off a windshield. The runner in the faded Braves visor crossed his sightline, and by the time the space opened again the park had resumed pretending nothing unusual had happened there.

He sat back down.

The bench was hotter now. Shade had shifted meanly. Sweat ran between his shoulder blades in a line fine as thread. He finished half the bottle in four gulps and set it beside him like evidence.

Around him the park resumed claiming it had never paused. Two teenagers cut across the grass litigating an ex none of their listeners had met. The man with the pigeon had moved on to giving a trash can the speech the pigeon apparently had not deserved. Somewhere beyond the trees a delivery truck backed up with that long electronic beep engineered by people who hated calm.

Joey counted the money again because counting was something he could do without confessing anything.

Twenty-five dollars.

He let himself feel the almost-smile this time. Small. Mean. A little ashamed. Twenty-five dollars was groceries. Twenty-five dollars was maybe two days of not getting lightheaded in the heat. It was not rent. It was not even enough to make the past-due envelope look embarrassed.

He touched the outside pocket once more to confirm the twenty was still separate. Safe from the rest of the money. Safe from him. The distinction mattered even if he could not have explained why without sounding insane.

Then he picked up the guitar and started "Wayfaring Stranger" from the top.

The tune came easy. Of course it did. The old tracks in him were cut deep enough to guide his hands even while his head ran elsewhere. First phrase, second. Thumb steady. Wrist loose. The park recognized the song and settled around it again in the same half-ashamed way it always did. A man with a paper sack of takeout slowed. Somebody clapped once in the wrong place and stopped. The dog walker with three leashes paused just outside the shade line and listened without meaning to.

Midway through the first pass, the fragment pressed up through his fingers.

Not loud. Not triumphant. Just stubborn. A green thing working at concrete because nobody had bothered explaining zoning to it.

Joey killed it.

He shifted the chord and steered back into the old road. His left hand found the old positions faster than thought, the neck familiar, the wood warm, the glue ridge under his thumb like a scar he trusted more than any promise.

The twenty sat quarantined in the outside pocket. The water bottle sweated beside him. His name stayed written on the case where anybody might read it if they ever bothered.

He let his hands choose for him.

They chose the safe one. The one that already knew how to hide him.
