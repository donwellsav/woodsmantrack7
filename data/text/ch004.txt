August in Harmony Park

He heard the park before he bothered looking at it.

Dog tags first. Then the kneeling hiss of a MARTA bus on Moreland, dropping on its shocks like an old man easing himself into prayer. Then the thin metal gripe of a shopping cart with one bad wheel. Then a pigeon making that insulted throat-noise pigeons made when they had already claimed squatters' rights on a public bench and found a tenant where they meant to stand. Harmony Park at nine in the morning, or close enough. Atlanta had not yet climbed all the way into the day's heat, but it was up, damp at the temples, already thinking mean thoughts.

Joey opened his eyes to that stack of noise and sat up on the bench he used six days a week, which was about as close to clocking in as his life got.

The slats were already warm through his shirt. By noon they would quit feeling warm and start feeling personal. By two they would feel like the city had left a skillet out for him on purpose. He swung his backpack down, opened the guitar case at the practiced angle that looked accidental and wasn't, and dropped three seeded coins into the lining with two fingers. People hated being first at generosity. They wanted proof somebody else had gone first and lived.

By now the whole arrangement looked less like setup than confession. The folded red flannel blanket beside him even in August, because some mornings still came off the concrete clammy and mean before the heat finished its work. The plastic coffee cup with yesterday's coffee reheated into fresh bitterness. The manila rent envelope stamped PAST DUE in a red that wanted to be blood and had to settle for office supply. He tucked that inside the case too, angled just enough for the red to show whenever he reached down for change. In the apartment it could still pretend to be tomorrow's problem. In the case it became company.

If you sat out long enough, you learned the park. Which dog walkers stopped because the dog stopped. Which commuters kept moving because they were late and which because they were ashamed they had no cash. Which regulars wanted a hello and which wanted the mercy of being treated like weather. On a bench you had to look public without looking beat, talented without looking like an accusation, hungry without looking desperate. Office jobs had simpler dress codes.

He was wearing the same ugly floral shirt he had bought at a thrift store two summers ago because it had amused him then and, through loyal overuse, had aged into a more serious category of ugly he respected on principle. Sweat was already slicking under his arms and down his spine. Breakfast had been coffee. Before that, older coffee. Before that, the ordinary stubborn belief that reheating old coffee did not count as the kind of decision a man could be judged by.

He reached for the guitar.

The wood was warm from the case and faintly tacky where the old glue ridge crossed the lower bout. His grandfather had done that repair years ago at a kitchen table with a clamp, a prayer, and the kind of confidence older men sometimes wore when the point was less to fix a thing than to refuse to be seen giving up on it. The crack had never really healed. It had only scarred over and become part of the guitar's weather. In damp months Joey's thumb still found it without asking, and every time it did he got one quick, inconvenient picture of his grandfather under yellow kitchen light, jaw set, forearms roped with veins, trying to hold together something that already knew it was split.

The guitar was not beautiful. That mattered to Joey more than he liked admitting. It wasn't a trophy instrument, not some honey-colored heirloom made for glass cases and family myths. It was work wood. Dings along the rim. Clouded finish. A neck his father had played slick in some positions and rough in others. When Joey held it, he never got a full movie of his father. He got pieces. Beard scratch when the man leaned over to move Joey's hand off a wrong fret. Tobacco and coffee in the cloth of his shirt. A thumb tapping time on Joey's wrist with brisk impatience while he said, not unkindly, Again. Like the song had been there before either of them and would wait, but only up to a point.

The case still carried the sticker Joey had slapped on it at nineteen, back when naming a future had felt unreasonably close to earning one. HARMONY OR BUST, it said in letters now chalked nearly white by sun and weather. Under it his own name, once Sharpied dark and certain, had gone thin and brown, as if even the marker had reconsidered him.

He set the guitar across his lap and gave the park one more second before he played.

That second had become part of the ritual. Fake weather check. A way of pretending the park was a room and he was reading it before he opened his mouth. Two men who had slept there were rising slow and territorial under separate scraps of shade. A runner in a faded Braves visor bent at the fountain to stretch, one white earbud still in, her face pinched at her own knees. Somewhere behind Joey a dog sneezed and got congratulated for it like it had solved something. Little Five was waking in layers around him: the freight-rattle of a gate rolling up, incense from some shop already losing a fistfight with fryer grease, traffic on Moreland shoving hot air ahead of itself.

Four notes lifted under his fingers.

He killed them.

Not with a flinch. A flinch would have admitted there was something there to kill. He only rolled his shoulder, shifted his hand, made it look like the guitar had gotten ahead of him in some harmless warm-up way. The fragment had been doing that more lately. Four notes some days. Six on others. Never enough to become a song a man could be held responsible for. Just enough to sound like the lip of one. Just enough to make him feel watched by his own hand.

That was the part he couldn't stand.

It sounded like him.

If it had sounded borrowed, he could have lived with it. If it had arrived fully formed out of some old river of anonymous songs his father trusted, he could have carried it the way he carried everything else. But the little turn in it came from nowhere he trusted. It felt personal, which in Joey's experience was usually another word for undercooked.

He gave the neck a mild shake as if the instrument, not he, had suggested it.

"Not today," he muttered.

Then he went into "Wayfaring Stranger."

His father had taught him that song before Joey's hands were big enough to cover what the song asked of them. Not the lyrics first. Never the lyrics first. The walk of it. The shape. The clean old frame under all the different people who had sung it as though it belonged especially to them. His father loved songs nobody could bill for. Songs with the ownership rubbed off by too many hands. If a tune lasted that long, he used to say, it had already been argued with enough. Your job wasn't to improve it. Your job was to carry it without dropping it.

Joey had built more of his life on that sentence than was probably healthy.

The first pass through the tune put him where it always put him, down in that narrow useful tunnel where memory and skill covered the same ground. His right hand settled. His left stopped making separate decisions and became a piece of older machinery. The park did what it often did when he hit that song clean. It rearranged itself and pretended it hadn't.

A dog walker let three leashes go slack at once. The runner in the faded Braves visor lost half a step and looked irritated with herself for it. A little boy with a dinosaur backpack went still beside the path, his mouth hanging open around a question. One of the men under the trees stopped rubbing sleep out of his eyes and listened instead. Coins landed in the case not on the strongest moments but a little to either side of them, because people liked to pay while acting like their hand had just happened to fall that way.

Harmony Park never became a crowd exactly. It gathered the way humidity gathered, by accumulation and stubbornness. Two cyclists coasted slower. A delivery driver stayed astride his cooler and listened with the helmet still on his head. Two women in office clothes drifted under a crepe myrtle and pretended to study one phone between them while they watched him over the top of it. The runner in the faded Braves visor edged closer without performing the fact that she had edged closer. Public listening had rules. Joey knew all of them.

He could still make a room listen, provided the room was a park and the song was old enough nobody had to ask what it revealed about him.

That had become its own hiding place.

People thought hiding looked like silence. Joey knew better. Hiding could sound competent as hell. It could sound practiced enough to make strangers slow down and feel, briefly, grateful to be alive. He knew how to make a patch of city feel held for three minutes. He knew how to make grief sound public and therefore noble. He knew how to disappear inside a song so thoroughly the vanishing passed for generosity.

And because the park answered him, a little, he got to tell himself this counted as a life instead of an interval before one.

The song ended. The not-crowd gave him the small, embarrassed applause public places specialized in. A few more coins arrived. One woman shot him a thumbs-up that felt maternal in a way he resented on principle. The dog walkers drifted on toward whatever better park economics came next.

He started another song before dead air had time to thicken. Dead air on a bench was expensive. It gave people too much time to remember errands, appointments, and the modern convenience of pretending not to notice a man with a guitar.

Halfway through the next tune he heard the subtraction.

Not silence. Harmony Park didn't hand out silence. But a particular removal from the noise. A footstep that had stopped being a footstep and turned into attention. The little pressure shift that happened when somebody walked into the edge of his music and did not keep moving through it. Joey looked up.

She was across the clearing on the path that cut toward Moreland.

Maybe he had seen her before. Hard to say. Atlanta specialized in faces that felt familiar because you'd once sweated beside them at a crosswalk and your brain mistook shared survival for intimacy. But this woman carried herself with a kind of settled certainty that made familiarity feel like the wrong category. Hair catching gold where the light found it. A face that might have passed for thirty if you asked politely and did not look too long at the eyes.

She had stopped mid-stride, one foot slightly ahead of the other, as if somebody outside the frame had called hold.

Joey finished the song because stopping would have looked like surrender.

When the last chord died she started moving again at once, already aimed at him. No little apology smile. No checking whether he seemed approachable. No preamble about bothering him. She moved like a person arriving for an appointment he hadn't known he had made.

By the time she reached the bench she had a water bottle in her hand that Joey would have bet cash had not been there a second earlier.

He looked at the bag on her shoulder. Too small. Not by opinion. By arithmetic. The bottle belonged to a different set of rules than the bag.

He decided not to think about it.

"You've been out here since nine," she said.

Her voice had none of the usual public approach in it. No flattery, no apology, no protective distance. It sounded like she was resuming something.

Joey shifted the guitar higher on his thigh. "Eight forty-five, technically. I like to set up before the dog walkers thin out. Dog walkers are my best audience. Not on merit. They may be deeply discerning people. I don't know their private lives. But the dogs stop when I play, and then the owners have to stop, and then everybody's just standing there pretending standing there is free. That's usually worth a dollar or two in canine enthusiasm."

She glanced toward a beagle towing a man across the path. "You've thought about this."

"I've been doing this six days a week for two years." He lifted one shoulder. "You sit in Atlanta heat and play the same songs for people trying to get somewhere else, you either study the ecosystem or you go crazy from repetition. Amateur sociology was the cheaper option."

"It's ninety-four already."

"Ninety-four is still manners. By two this bench'll be hot enough to grill testimony off a man. August here isn't weather. It's a landlord. It comes in without knocking and sits down with its shoes on."

That got the faintest movement at the corner of her mouth. Not a smile exactly. More the possibility of one.

"What were you playing?" she asked.

"Just now or historically."

"Just now."

"Wayfaring Stranger." He nodded at the guitar. "Old folk song. Old old. Older than the paperwork. Usually when nobody knows who wrote something, it means somebody poor did and history let somebody tidier keep the pen. My daddy taught it to me. His daddy taught him. Before that, some other tired person sang it for somebody who needed help carrying a thing. That's how the good ones worked for him. Useful songs. Not precious songs. Useful like beans. Useful like a towel. For the people who need it, he'd say. Somebody was sick, somebody was leaving, somebody had to keep working anyway, and the song gave the feeling somewhere to go without making a spectacle of it."

He heard himself doing what he always did when something sharper was coming. Building a museum display out of other people's dead music so nobody had to ask why it suited him.

"Your father told you that," she said.

"Something close. He had a whole theology about old songs." Joey kept talking because stopping would have left him exposed halfway through a defense. "His view was if a tune lasted two hundred years, the argument was over. Your job wasn't to improve it. Your job was to carry it right. Which sounds humble until you realize it also lets a man avoid being responsible for anything with his own fingerprints on it."

He had not meant to say that last part aloud.

She heard the slip. Of course she did.

"And the other thing," she said.

He frowned. "What other thing."

"Before that. Four notes. Then a turn downward you didn't trust. Then you shut it down."

He went still in a deeper way than standing still. The kind that started under the ribs.

"I don't know what you're talking about."

"Yes you do."

He smiled because smiling cost less than telling the truth. "Sometimes the hand wanders. Occupational hazard. Musical equivalent of talking in your sleep."

"That wasn't wandering."

"Maybe the guitar was stretching."

"No."

She said it without force. That made it firmer.

He tried levity again because levity had gotten him through childhood, grief, overdue notices, and at least three dates he should have canceled. "Well, if we're diagnosing from the sidewalk, let's call it artistic acid reflux. Random little notes rising at inappropriate times."

"It sounded like something trying to come through."

"Lady, you've heard about seven seconds of my morning."

"That was enough."

The water bottle was still in her hand. Condensation ran over her knuckles and should have looked ordinary. It didn't. Joey became aware all at once of the coffee souring the back of his tongue, the salt drying at his collar, the damp hem of his shirt pasted to the base of his spine, the weight of the rent envelope in the case at his feet. The whole park seemed to sharpen around her instead of change. He would almost have preferred change. Change announced itself. This was only reality carrying one extra degree of accuracy.

"Can I tell you something?" she asked.

"That has not, so far, stopped you."

"You have a song in you."

Traffic moved on Moreland. Somewhere downtown a siren took one long breath and held it. The fountain gave a weak cough and started up again. Nothing in the park altered its public behavior to mark the line. That made it feel worse, not better.

Joey looked down at the guitar.

The glue ridge caught slightly under his thumb. At once he got his father again, not as a noble ghost but as a warm, irritated body behind him, moving Joey's wrist, saying, Don't baby it. Then the memory went out again. That was how objects treated him. One clean second, then the door shut.

"It's not a song," he said. "It's a habit. Nerve static. A thing my fingers do when I'm not paying attention."

"Your fingers know something."

"My fingers know old songs."

"Safe songs."

He felt himself stiffen. "They're good songs."

"I didn't say they weren't."

"My daddy played them. His daddy played them."

"Yes."

"They're not safe because they're old. They're old because they kept being useful."

"Useful to who?"

There was no mockery in the question. That made it harder. Mockery he could fight. Honest inquiry wanted something from him.

Joey heard himself sliding into explanation again. "To people with jobs and funerals and kitchens. People who needed somewhere to set what they couldn't carry alone."

"And you like that."

"I like not lying."

She held his gaze. "No. You like not risking a different lie."

He laughed once, short and dry. "Lady, you hear one old song in a park and decide to perform surgery on a stranger? Is that a hobby? Park diagnostics?"

"Did your father teach you to disappear too?"

That got through him before he could turn it into a joke.

Heat had come fully into the morning now. The smell of cut grass, dog fur, brick, exhaust, and old fryer grease pressed together until each breath felt already used by somebody else. A truck shifted gears out on Moreland with a sound like a throat clearing before bad news. The rent envelope flashed again at the edge of his vision, that red stamp loud as accusation. Joey wanted, all at once, to play, to leave, to say something cruel, to say something honest, and could not decide which would cost least.

"You don't know anything about my father," he said.

"I know the way you use him."

He looked up at her so fast the motion hurt.

She had not moved. The water bottle rested against her palm. The too-small bag hung from her shoulder as if ordinary space had always obeyed it. Her face carried no triumph. That almost angered him more. Triumph he would have known how to despise. This was only attention with the kindness burned off.

"His songs are safe," she said. "Safe feels respectful to you. Safe lets you disappear inside lineage and call it humility."

The words landed one after another, not dramatic, not raised, simply set where he could not avoid stepping on them.

Joey opened his mouth with three answers ready, all of them clever, all of them false.

None got there in time.

"I get why," she said. "But safe isn't yours, Joey."
