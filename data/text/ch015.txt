Second Thursday

The week between Thursdays did not make him brave. It made him coachable, which felt worse.

Courage could still pretend it had chosen something. Coaching meant somebody else had listened to him fail, named the failure plain, and been right.

He heard the bartender every time his hands tried to sprint through one of the old songs in Harmony Park.

Don't chase the room. Slower than your hands want. Quieter than your nerves like. Let them hear themselves stepping on it.

The park let him try that advice without making a ceremony of it. August had settled into the kind of heat that stopped announcing itself — just a flat pressure behind the eyes and sweat arriving before effort did. Nobody there had bought a table. Nobody thought a beer or a basket of fries bought them the right to ignore him. People either kept moving or didn't, and that was clean enough to work with.

The first time he left a strip of air between two lines, panic hit him so fast it felt mechanical, like the skipped drop in a staircase when your foot expects another step and gets nothing. His right hand almost rushed in to cover the gap. His mouth almost started doing what it always did when he got scared, which was talk. Some sideways story about how the song had drifted down from North Carolina, or how one version of it had lived in Georgia red clay long enough to lose half its Presbyterian manners. Anything to get language back in the air before silence could start making claims on him.

He held off.

A woman with two Kroger bags stopped long enough to hear what came after. A kid on a scooter rolled halfway past the bench, dragged one foot, circled back, and stayed for the chorus. An old man by the fountain looked down at the quarters in his hand as if he had forgotten why he was counting them, then looked up instead.

The ugly part was that it worked.

In the park, anyway. Which was not the same thing as making a paid room stop spending itself on fries and office gossip for long enough to hear him.

The uglier part was what the extra air let creep toward him.

The more space Joey left inside the old songs, the harder the hidden fragment leaned at him from the dark edge of things. It came as a pressure in his left hand before it ever arrived as notes. A shape under the chord change. A live wrongness crouched inside the familiar change his father had handed him. Sometimes it felt less like a melody than like somebody in the next room trying the knob and finding it locked. He kept turning away from it. What he wanted from the week was control, not revelation.

He learned something else he liked even less. The less he talked between songs, the less he could hide in the history of the songs. When he cut the little explanations, the county lines and church basements and migration routes and half-true stories about where a tune had first found him, the music had to stand up by itself. So did he. The park got quieter around him in a different way then, as if even the city wanted to see whether he meant what he had just refused to explain.

Useful in daylight. Unproven under amber lights.

By the next Thursday the silver guitar was still under the bed where it had stayed since the day the gold one became cash. Even in the apartment's bad evening light the latches held a mean little shine. The thing looked pressed and mannered, with no line of hands behind it. Joey crouched once to look at it and immediately hated himself for crouching.

By the chair, the wooden guitar waited with one string a little flat and the case handle dark where years of hands had taught the leather what sweat was for. No shine to it. No innocence either. It fit the apartment the way a scar fit skin.

Beside the turntable Clarence's record sleeve leaned where he had left it after one late-night listen. Side B was still in there with its rougher pressure and its unfinished feeling, the whole thing waiting on a nerve he did not have for it yet. The fan rattled hard enough to make the paper tick once against the stand. Joey looked at the sleeve, then away.

Joey took the wooden guitar.

He went to the dish beside the sink and counted out four singles and a five, feeling the humiliating lightness of that as carefully as he had once felt the obscene thickness of the pawn-shop envelope. Pike's notice was still on the table with seven days left before he could file. The number made the nine dollars in Joey's hand feel stupid. The kitchen drawer below the dish stayed shut. He could feel the money in there without seeing it. Thick fold of bills. Fluorescent dust. The dry, practical thumb of the pawn-shop owner turning miracle into inventory while Joey pretended this was the same thing as choosing. He shut the thought down before it could get ambitious and start doing future math.

Late August had moved from simple heat into something meaner. All week the sky had looked as if rain were considering Atlanta from a personal distance and finding the city not quite persuasive enough. By sunset the air had not cooled so much as curdled. On Moreland the buses groaned like old men turning over in bed. The traffic light at Euclid clicked through its changes with bureaucratic patience. Somewhere down the block fryer grease was already climbing into the evening ahead of the bars, and incense from the storefront two doors over kept losing the fight in shifts.

He heard the Landmark before he saw the sign.

Ice dumped into metal.

Fryer hiss.

A microphone taking one dry thump and then another.

Somebody laughing with enough liquor in it to make the laugh taller than the body it came from.

The room had a way of announcing itself by labor before spectacle. You heard the work first. The carrying, chilling, wiping, stacking, pouring. The night getting built.

Inside, it smelled exactly like last week and not at all like the same place. Old wood. Beer gone warm at the edges. Cold grease from the kitchen trying to pass for fresh. Mop water sitting under all of it like paperwork nobody argued with. The church bones were still in the rafters if a man bothered to lift his eyes, but Thursday night trained everybody's attention lower than faith did. Table height. Bar height. Billfold height.

Two women at the bar in office clothes were already asking the bartender if Cass was on.

"Tomorrow," he said, drying a glass without looking up.

One of them made the face people made when they had come for the expensive item and been offered the cheaper set menu. The other looked toward the stage, judged the room's odds against her disappointment, and said, "We'll see, I guess."

They stayed.

The name moved through Joey harder than he wanted it to. There were people who came into this room already prepared to ask for her. People who would spend money on the chance that a person might appear. He had to keep himself from looking at the stage the way a broke man looked into a bakery window.

He signed the yellow legal pad clipped near the register. Same ritual, same paper, different names. The legal pad was cheap enough that the pressure from old signatures ghosted through the sheet under the fresh one. He wrote Joey Woodsman and watched the name settle among the others in the flat sameness of ballpoint ink. No voltage in it. No inheritance. Just another body asking to be called.

The bartender glanced from the line to Joey's face.

"Back already."

"A week counts as already?"

"It does if you learned slow."

The line sat there between them with the shape of a joke and the weight of an exam score. Joey slid the four dollars across the bar.

"Cheapest beer."

The bartender took the bills, set the bottle in front of him, and said, "Then don't rush this one either."

Duke was at the corner table with the ledger open and a pen moving through names, numbers, and private conclusions. Nighttime Duke still had warmth in him, but it wore work clothes. Daylight Duke could afford to sound like a historian. Nighttime Duke was a mechanic keeping the machine from embarrassing itself. He looked up once, gave Joey a nod so brief it could have passed for a muscle correction, then went back to the page.

The front table had new people and the same problem.

Four of them. After-work age. A man in scrubs with his badge turned backward into his pocket. A woman with the pale line at her throat where a school lanyard had been all day. Another woman with a laptop bag hooked around one ankle like she did not trust the chair not to lose it. A broad, handsome man in a golf shirt eating fries one at a time with the studied patience of somebody who had been talked at since eight in the morning and now meant to enjoy chewing in peace.

They were not rude in any way he could work against. They were simply living inside the rights they thought they had purchased. Food. Drinks. Their own conversation. Relief. The music, if it wanted a seat, had to take one without asking those rights to shrink.

Joey understood that better now, which did not make it any kinder.

A singer before him tried to solve the table with force. Good voice too. Too much of it. Every chorus came in like an argument already halfway lost. The room did what rooms did when a person overreached at them: it kept its own shape. The front table barely changed pitch. Joey watched the singer push harder and felt last week rise in him so exactly his neck went hot.

The bartender kept polishing a glass.

He did not look smug. He did not look pitying either. He looked like a man who had once told somebody the engine was throwing a rod and now intended to let the engine finish being correct.

When he touched two fingers to the legal pad and pointed stageward, Joey was ready in the narrow, ugly way a man was ready for dental work. Not calm. Just stripped of any claim to surprise.

He took the stool this time.

That alone changed the geometry. Less preacher. Less man trying to dominate a room from above it. More witness. More kitchen-table distance. He lowered the microphone until he did not have to reach for it, settled the guitar against his thigh, and let his hands find the first chord without hurrying them.

The old tempo was already in his wrist, twitching to be useful. He could feel last week's version of himself waiting to launch out of muscle memory and make the same public case with a little more conviction, as if conviction had been the missing variable.

He chose the same opening song on purpose.

If the fix only worked on easier material, it was not a fix. It was superstition.

The first chord went out and stayed out.

Last week he had shoved the song into the room like a late apology. Tonight he let the wood answer the room before his voice tried to explain anything. He came in a shade lower than his park voice, close enough to the microphone that he did not have to throw the notes toward the rafters. Not smaller. Nearer. Like he was talking to one person across a kitchen table and the rest of the room could overhear if they wanted.

The first line made almost no visible difference.

That was fine. He had finally understood that a room was not a crowd. A crowd turned in chunks. A room showed it was listening by what it quit doing.

He finished the verse and left space after it wide enough to scare himself.

Everything in him wanted to jump in and save it. Joke. Thank you kindly. Next line. Louder chord. A quick county-history detour. Anything but that hanging strip of air where other people's attention could either arrive or reveal itself by staying away.

He held still.

What changed first was not faces turning. It was sound leaving.

A laugh from the side booth died before it got all the way out.

Somebody at the bar lifted a bottle and set it down quieter than they had picked it up.

The woman with the laptop bag at the front table said, "No, because he sent it at six-thirty and then -" and stopped, not because she had decided to be respectful, but because Joey's last note was still in the space where her sentence meant to go. She looked up with a quick, irritated surprise, as if sound itself had taken up too much room near her chair.

Joey did not rescue her from it.

He put the next line exactly where the room had just gone waiting.

Something in his chest flinched when that worked. Not relief. Shock. The good kind and the bad kind occupying the same inch under his breastbone.

He played the second verse the same way. Slower than his hands liked. The tune had more bone in it at that tempo. More place for age. More room for the grief inside it to show itself without getting theatrical. He kept his mouth close to the mic. Kept the phrasing low and human. Did not reach upward for authority he had not earned.

At Duke's table the pen stopped moving.

Tap.

Once against the ledger margin.

Then Duke looked up fully.

The hidden fragment moved in Joey's left hand under the next chord change, stirred by the very space he was learning to trust. For one crooked instant he felt it trying to come through in the gap, not as a full line, not yet, but as pressure with a pulse. His stomach tightened around it. Not here, he thought, with the same reflexive meanness he might have used on a person who arrived at his door uninvited and too familiar. Not under amber lights in front of people debating condiments.

He kept to the old song.

The front table did not go silent all at once. That would have felt fake even if he had wanted it. The man in the golf shirt kept chewing through half the chorus. The woman with the school-mark at her throat leaned toward him to say something and did not finish it. The man in scrubs stared down at the fry basket as if listening were an administrative error he meant to correct in a second. By the time Joey reached the turn at the end of the song, all four of them were listening in pieces.

Sometimes pieces were enough.

The applause came from nearer the stage than it had last week. Not large. Not unanimous. Real. One of the women at the front table was already clapping before her face had fully agreed to it.

Joey nodded once.

No story. No filler.

Old Joey would have spent the fresh attention before it cooled off. Thanked everybody too much. Told them where his father had first heard the song. Made himself useful with context before the room could remember it had every right to go back to itself. His mouth even opened on habit, some half-formed line about county roads and hymn meters and why songs like this survived because poor people knew how to carry what the world refused to store for them.

He shut it.

The room did not need another man explaining what it should do next.

The second song was lower and sadder and older in a way that usually made him nervous indoors. On the bench, old material could wear dignity like a coat and not have to prove anything else. In a room, the same material could turn into antique furniture if a man touched it wrong.

He started anyway.

The first two lines went out so quietly he almost thought he had underplayed them. Then the room answered by not answering. No bar laugh rose to meet the pause. No burst of front-table talk cut across the phrase ending. A server with a tray stopped half a beat before crossing in front of the stage, waited for the line to finish, then moved on with the private annoyance of someone who had just discovered she was being careful.

That was when silence stopped feeling like threat and turned useful.

Not generous. Useful.

He could work with this, he realized. Not own it. Not dominate it. Work with it. The empty places between notes were not proof that he was losing the room. They were part of the arrangement now, pressure he could shape. Invitation with edges on it.

So he leaned into the spaces again.

What he heard then was the room coming closer table by table.

Not visibly at first. Sonically.

The clink of silverware dropped off near the front.

A chair at the back scraped once and then did not scrape again.

Somebody at the bar finished the rest of a sentence in a whisper instead of a speaking voice.

The man in scrubs finally looked up.

The woman with the laptop bag put both hands around her glass and forgot to drink from it.

Even the handsome man with the fries slowed down enough to listen with his chewing.

Joey felt it all arrive before he trusted what he was seeing. Sound changed texture before faces changed allegiance. He could hear the room making room. Little pockets of self-conscious quiet broke out among people who had not planned to be witnessed by a song and now had to decide whether they were really going to keep talking over it.

His body answered before his mind did. Heat moved up the back of his neck. His ribs felt too small for one full breath. For one ugly second he wanted to cash the whole thing out, stand up from the stool, hit the next line harder, and take credit for what the room was giving him.

That was the old mistake.

He stayed seated. Stayed close. Let the line come in almost plain.

It landed harder that way than force would have let it land.

And because he was not covering the room with volume now, he could hear what the song was actually doing to it. The front table's talk had not disappeared. It had changed register. The little sarcastic edge on their voices was gone. The woman with the school-mark nodded once at something in the lyric or maybe only in the tone, then looked embarrassed that her own face had volunteered information. At the bar, one of the women who had come in asking for Cass was no longer talking at all. She had a hand around the stem of her glass and was staring at Joey with the evaluating calm of somebody revising a purchase. The other had turned half toward the stage and half toward tomorrow, listening without giving up what she'd come here wanting.

At Duke's table the pen lay still beside the ledger.

Duke did not smile. That would have cheapened it. He only leaned forward and rested both forearms on the table, which in a man like him was practically applause.

Joey finished the song on a chord that rang longer than he wanted and shorter than he feared.

The quiet held.

Then the applause came up.

Still not full. Still Thursday. Still a bar with baskets on the tables and clocks in everybody's actual life. But enough of the room had turned toward him that the clapping almost found one body before breaking apart again. The front table was in some of it. The bar was in some of it. Even the bartender, who did not waste motion when motion could be mistaken for endorsement, gave the neck of the bottle in Joey's direction a small lift.

He played one more, because ending exactly there would have required a stage wisdom he had not earned yet. The third song held for half of itself and then the room started remembering its own life again. A fork hit a plate near the back. One of the women at the front table leaned in and finally finished the sentence she had been saving. Joey hurried one entrance, caught himself, and hated that the catch itself felt like progress. Better than last week by a distance. Not mastery. Not safety. Just proof that he had found a way to get the room to turn and no way yet to keep it.

When he came offstage, the guitar felt stranger in his hands than it had after failure.

Failure at least knew its shape. It let him blame the room, the front table, the cheap mic, the fact that nothing sacred survived contact with ranch dressing.

This was worse.

He had felt the Landmark move, then ease some of that weight back onto itself.

Not all of it. Not every table. Not for long enough to mistake for belonging. But enough to make the old excuse go dead in his mouth. The space he had learned to leave inside the inherited songs was not just better phrasing. It was a vacancy. Every time the hidden fragment pushed at him in that space, he had a cleaner place to feel himself dodge it.

He set the guitar back in its case carefully because his hands needed one practical task before his thoughts started using knives.

When he turned, the bartender was already nudging his unopened beer within reach.

"Dog came over?" the bartender asked.

Joey looked down the room once, then back at him. "For a minute."

"Minute's how they start."

Joey let out a breath that almost qualified as a laugh. "You tell that to everybody or just the humiliated?"

"Just the repeat customers."

The line was friendly if a person took the cheaper meaning. It was also a reminder that the room had not adopted him. It had only admitted him for another round. Joey appreciated the accuracy of that more than he appreciated the comfort.

He had barely gotten his hand around the beer neck when Duke came over from the corner table carrying a short glass of bourbon.

The drink was amber, exact. No theatrical raise. No host's voice. No public blessing. Duke set it on the bar in front of Joey with the same neatness he had used all night on the ledger, like he was placing a tool where a tool belonged.

"On the house," he said.

Joey looked at the glass, then at him. "I thought the door cost what it costs."

"It does." Duke rested one hand on the bar. "Drink's different."

He was smiling, but only with part of his face. The rest of him was still on shift, still listening over Joey's shoulder to how many glasses got set down and how fast the room was turning its own attention elsewhere.

"Better tonight," Duke said. "You quit trying to beat the furniture."

Joey took the beer instead of the bourbon because he needed one more second before the old man got inside his chest moving things around. "Felt rude to lose the same way twice."

Duke gave him a short breath of laughter. "Last week you sang at people. Tonight you gave the song somewhere to sit. Folks will come to that if you don't panic and stand up in the middle of it."

If you don't panic.

There it was. The compliment and the leash braided into the same sentence. Joey felt himself respond to both against his will.

Duke nudged the bourbon closer with two fingers.

"There's Woodsman in that," he said.

The sentence hit clean and low.

Joey looked up too fast. "You knew my father?"

Duke's face barely changed. "Knew of him. Heard enough. Heard him once or twice around town. Man knew better than to hurry a room. Most men think they got to grab everybody at once. Your daddy would lay a song down and let people come the extra foot themselves."

For one second it felt like nearness.

Not proof. Worse than proof. A clean place for hope to catch.

Then the rest of it arrived.

Duke had watched the room lean, watched Joey feel it, and chosen that exact moment to lay the father between them. Father as praise. Father as invitation. Father as the oldest bait in Joey's body.

"Keep doing that," Duke said softly. "Thursday'll teach you a lot if you let it."

He straightened, tapped the bar twice with the pad of one finger, and went back toward the ledger before Joey could decide whether he wanted more or less from him.

Joey stood there looking down at the bourbon.

The room around him had already resumed being itself. Fries cooling on plates. A server balancing three fresh beers up her arm. The front table talking again, though quieter than before, as if the set had left a dent in the air they were still arranging themselves around. At the bar, one of the women who had asked for Cass was saying, "Tomorrow, then," in the tone of somebody deciding to come back because a standard had been confirmed rather than denied.

Joey picked up the glass.

The bourbon was on the house. The hook was not.
