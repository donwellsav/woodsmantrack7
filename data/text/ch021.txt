Moreland

Moreland sounded louder after he'd paid five hundred dollars for the privilege of compromising himself.

Not louder in any way a machine could prove. Traffic kept doing traffic. Buses sighed and lurched. A motorcycle kept worrying the same stretch of road like it had sworn a private oath against mufflers. Music leaked out of the bars in wet bright scraps every time a door opened and got chopped off when it shut. Behind him, whenever somebody went in or out of the Landmark, the room tossed a snare crack or half a chorus onto the sidewalk, then swallowed it before the line could finish breathing. The city kept its own hours whether or not Joey Woodsman had just bought a place in somebody else's crooked hope.

He heard all of it rawer now.

Maybe the buy-in had skinned the night. Maybe once he'd watched five dead hundreds leave his hand and disappear into Duke's ledger, every sound on Moreland started arriving itemized. Bus brakes. Bottle clink. A laugh from across the street. A tire hissing through gutter water left over from some half-rain too mean to count. Atlanta sounded like it was charging extra for every damn thing tonight.

The coffee, bought by no kindness he felt like naming, sat bitter and cooling in his hand. The sandwich bag had gone slack with grease. His name sat on the line in Duke's ledger now, no longer drifting in the margin where a man could still pretend fate had made a clerical error. The buy-in money was gone. At home Pike's notice still waited on the kitchen table with three days left before filing. Clarence's record sleeve still leaned by the turntable with side B in it like a question written in pencil. The silver guitar still lay under the bed. The wooden one still waited by the chair. Behind him the Landmark kept breathing cold air and old beer and false welcome over the block.

He walked north because south felt too much like retreat, and standing still would have made him think.

The heat had not broken. Night only changed its posture in Atlanta in August. The sun went offstage and the concrete kept arguing anyway. The air along Moreland held fryer grease, cigarettes, hot rubber, storm water still trapped in drains from some earlier almost-rain, and the medicinal sweetness of something blooming after dark for no sensible reason. A MARTA bus huffed at the stop ahead of him, doors folding open with the same tired pneumatics the city used for everything: transit, labor, compromise, one more day.

He took a swallow of the coffee and winced.

Cold enough now to count as disrespect.

He pitched the cup into a public trash can by the bus bench and missed just cleanly enough that the plastic lid popped off and skittered under the bench. He stood there staring at it for half a beat.

The bench had metal slats instead of wood, city issue instead of park charity, but anything sliding under one hit him the same now. His case under the Harmony Park bench. His own hand shoving it deeper into shadow because he wanted one minute to piss, one minute to think, one minute to keep the world from touching what was his. Then waking to the wrong kind of empty. His stomach tightened before the memory finished arriving.

That, he thought, is exactly the scale of symbolism my life can currently afford.

He bent, fished the lid out, dropped it in properly, and straightened with the paper bag still in one hand and the evening still unimproved.

Then he heard metal kiss concrete once behind him.

Not loud. Exact.

Then a laugh that was not.

It rolled across the block so fully that for a beat even the traffic seemed to make room for it. Not because traffic respected anybody. Only because Mercury's amusement arrived as if the city had better acoustics for him than for everyone else.

Joey shut his eyes once.

"Of course," he said.

When he turned, Mercury was halfway up the sidewalk with the jeweled microphone stand slung over one shoulder like a prince carrying a fishing pole. White suit. White boots. Tie loose tonight, as if even divinity had found the weather excessive. Streetlight moved over him in broken stripes, but nothing about him took on the city's dirt correctly. He stayed bright in a way that insulted every ordinary object around him.

"You say that," Mercury said, "like you've spotted mold instead of company."

"You're company the way a subpoena is mail."

Mercury smiled. "That's good. Keep that one."

Joey started walking again because standing still for Mercury always felt like volunteering. Mercury fell in beside him without visible effort.

The stand clicked once against his shoulder every few steps, a metronome for bad news.

For half a block Joey said nothing. The street kept supplying noise whether he wanted it or not. Somebody revved too high at the light. Somebody else in a nearby bar had picked a jukebox song that wanted to be heartbroken and was currently being overheard by people who preferred to shout through it. Behind them, the Landmark door opened again and a woman's voice leaped briefly into the night, trying a bright little climb near the top of her range. The room shaved the shine off it and sent it back smaller.

That did it.

"My father had the thing for a room like that," Joey said.

Mercury glanced over. "I know his music."

"Everybody knows his music." Joey laughed once, with no humor in it. "Clarence knows. Duke knows. You know. The whole neighborhood's got some public-library version of my dead father and I'm apparently the last one to check him out under my own card. He had whatever you keep talking about. The thing. The real thing. What exactly did that buy him."

Mercury didn't answer right away.

That by itself slowed Joey's steps.

Mercury usually stepped on your sentence before it finished cooling. Joke, knife, jeweled insult, bright reframing, something quick enough to distract from where he meant to press. Silence on him meant the question had either interested him or bored him past entertainment.

"It didn't buy him anything," Mercury said at last.

Joey looked over hard.

"No speech."

"Not yet."

"No parable. No Greek office-hours fable about the soul of the room."

"You seem overstocked on parables tonight."

Joey stopped on the sidewalk. Cars blurred past behind them, light sliding over Mercury's white suit in dirty gold stripes. The paper bag had warmed his palm into dampness. Grease had begun to print through in small dark moons.

"Then tell me plain," he said. "My father had it. The thing. Duke heard it. Clarence heard it. I hear it every time I put on that record. What does your whole philosophy do with the fact that a man can have the real thing and still wind up dead, broke, and half remembered on a shelf."

Mercury stopped too.

For the first time since Joey had met him, there was no readiness in his face. No curated wickedness. No delighted contempt. Only attention with most of the costume taken off.

"It doesn't do anything with it," he said.

The answer landed hard enough to feel insulting.

"It doesn't."

"No."

Mercury shifted the stand on his shoulder and looked up Moreland as if the traffic had asked him a question.

"Some real music fails," he said. "Not because the music failed. Because the math around it did."

Joey said nothing.

Mercury went on.

"Wrong room. Wrong night. Half the audience stayed home because it rained. Sound man checked out at six and never truly came back. Owner booked the act after you in the wrong order, so the room spent your set waiting for louder. Scout got stuck on the Connector. Bartender dropped a tray in your quietest line. Woman at table four got dumped twenty minutes earlier and heard every song as proof of her own exquisite importance. Singer told the truth to twelve people and eleven of them were arguing about whether to try the wings before the kitchen closed." He looked back at Joey. "A thousand reasons. None of them the music's fault."

As he said it, the Landmark door opened again behind them. This time a man's voice came out low and sure, three bars of something almost honest, and Joey heard the room meet it with that brief animal hush it gave a note when it found the right grain of wood. Then the door shut. The hush shut with it.

"So it's luck," Joey said.

"No." Mercury's mouth curved. "Luck is the word people use when they want chaos to sound cute."

"Then what is it."

"The world," Mercury said. "Which is uglier."

That landed. Joey hated it for landing.

He started walking again because standing under a truth made it feel too much like weather, and he wanted distance even if the truth could walk.

"That's supposed to help me."

"No."

"Good."

"Relief looks unbecoming on you anyway."

Joey barked out something like a laugh and kept moving.

"So that's it," he said. "You spend all this time pressing people about what's real, and then when I ask what reality owes the real thing, the answer is nothing."

"Reality owes the real thing less than nothing. Usually it sends a bill."

"Amazing. Great. So the song can be true and the room can still not care."

"Of course."

Joey tightened his grip on the bag until grease pressed through the seam. It made him think of Mae's paper-wrapped sandwich sliding across the diner counter, the ordinary mercy of somebody feeding him without turning it into philosophy. Duke could make every transaction sound ceremonial. Mae never bothered. That difference felt larger tonight than it had at noon.

"Then what am I doing."

Mercury looked over at him with something close to pity, which on Mercury read more frightening than mockery.

"You're asking the wrong question in a new tuxedo."

"No, I'm not."

"Yes." Mercury's voice sharpened by a shade. "What you're really asking is whether your father's truth should have protected him from obscurity, mediocrity, sickness, ordinary human neglect, and every petty mechanical failure that wrecks a life one cheap inch at a time. It should have. It didn't."

Joey went white so fast he felt the blood leave.

Mercury saw it happen and did not look away.

"That's the part nobody wants to say," he said. "Not Clarence. Not Duke. Not Veritas. Certainly not you. Your father could have had the thing all the way through and the world could still have shrugged. The music could be real and the world could still fail to care in time."

Traffic roared past. Somewhere behind them a horn got leaned on far too long and accomplished nothing. Somebody shouted from a patio and got answered by laughter too drunk to contain itself. Joey stood in the middle of all that ordinary noise with the paper bag in one hand and his dead father's unfinished public life suddenly standing on the sidewalk between him and Mercury like another person.

"Fuck you," he said quietly.

Mercury nodded once.

"Good."

"Don't you good me."

"No, listen." Mercury stopped walking and turned toward him fully. The whole block seemed to rearrange itself around that attention. "Mad is useful. Scared is not. Scared men become archivists."

Joey stared.

Mercury went on, softer now, which only made it hit harder. "They polish relics. They get very eloquent about lineage. They carry other people's songs with both hands and call it devotion because cowardice sounds cheap in church clothes."

Anger came up hot enough to rescue Joey for a second from humiliation.

"You don't get to call loving your dead father cowardice."

"I didn't." Mercury held his eyes. "I called hiding in him cowardice. Accuracy matters."

Joey looked away first.

At the curb a bike messenger shot through the light against traffic, a blur of red bag and bad judgment. Fryer grease thickened in the air, then thinned again as a restaurant door swung shut. A bus pulled off the stop with that long hydraulic sigh, and for one second Joey caught his reflection in the dark side windows: one man with a paper bag and a face he would not have trusted in a mirror.

"Scared men spend thirty years carrying other people's songs," Mercury said, "because if the real thing in them fails, the failure has their name on it in permanent ink."

The words went straight in.

Joey hated that. Hated how little cushioning they met on the way down.

"I don't want it to be mine," he said, and heard the rawness of it too late to pull it back. "I want it to be his. I want the whole story to have belonged to him while he was alive to know it did. I want the room to have heard him in time. I want those records to mean he wasn't just..." He stopped.

Mercury waited.

Joey looked out at Moreland. Headlights. Steam breathing weakly out of a street grate. The whole city acting like nobody had just said the ugliest possible true thing in public.

"I want it to have been enough," he said.

Mercury's face did not soften.

"It may not have been."

There was no surviving that sentence with dignity intact. Joey knew it. Mercury knew it. The block knew it and kept minding its own business anyway.

Mercury shifted the mic stand again.

"You're afraid that's your future," he said. "Not the broke part. You're already acquainted. The other part. Real music, real thing, and still no rescue. Still no guarantee. Still no trophy big enough to reverse the math."

Joey wanted to deny it. Managed only anger.

"You really know how to brighten an evening."

Mercury laughed then, a real one, open-throated and merciless and alive. It filled the block so completely a woman waiting at the bus stop turned to look, then thought better of whatever question had started forming.

"There he is," Mercury said. "Much better than self-pity."

He started walking again, faster now, already leaving the moment with the confidence of a man who had done his damage and had no intention of invoicing twice.

After three steps he looked back over one shoulder.

"Write your own damn song, Joey."

Then he cut across the next stream of headlights at a diagonal no mortal body ought to have risked and kept going.

Joey stood on the sidewalk shaking harder than the weather justified.

He didn't know how long he stayed there. Long enough for the sandwich to cool all the way through. Long enough for his pulse to stop trying to outrun his skin. Long enough for the corner to turn from stage back into intersection. Long enough for the Landmark door to open twice more behind him and prove, with irritating consistency, that the room could still answer a good line while remaining morally useless as a system.

Then he started walking.

Fast at first, with the useless speed of a man trying to leave a sentence behind on a public sidewalk.

He got half a block before he heard footsteps matching his.

Not hurrying to catch him. Not tentative either. Exact, as if the pace had been there first and he had stumbled into it.

He didn't look over right away.

"If you are him again with a different costume," he said, "I'm going to start crossing traffic on principle."

"That would be wasteful," Veritas said.

He let out a breath through his nose.

"Fantastic."

She was there beside him in the same wrong-angle calm she had worn in the park, at the cart, everywhere. Dark clothes refusing to take a position on fashion. Hair pulled back. Hands empty tonight. No book. No cart. Just Veritas walking Moreland like the city had always meant her to be one of its less advertised utilities.

For a few steps she said nothing.

That almost made him more nervous than if she had opened with a knife.

Then she said, "Your whole set is still your father's shelter."

Joey turned his head. "They are folk songs."

"Some."

"And the arrangements are mine."

"The arrangements are furniture moved around in a house you didn't build."

The line was so clean he almost admired the workmanship.

"I do substitutions."

"Congratulations. You can repaint the walls."

He laughed once despite everything, then hated himself for giving her the rhythm.

"You all really coordinate your hostility."

"No," Veritas said. "You do that yourself."

He kept walking. She kept pace without effort.

The cross streets slid by in hot little increments. Somewhere a delivery truck backed with the steady panicked beeping that made every city sound briefly like a hospital loading dock. A patio behind them erupted for something on television instead of a song. Joey felt the difference in his teeth. Rooms turned together for all kinds of counterfeit reasons. That didn't make Mercury less right. It just made Duke more possible.

"Is what I'm doing enough," he said, before pride could stop him. "Playing them the way I play them. Carrying them right."

"Enough isn't the point."

He kept his eyes straight ahead. "Of course it is."

"The point is why you keep killing it."

That cut cleaner than Mercury had.

Maybe because there was no theater on it. No laugh. No bright suit. Just a sentence put where it belonged and left there.

"I don't know what you're talking about."

"Yes, you do."

"No."

"Every time it starts to come up," she said, "you shut it down yourself. In the park. On the bench. In your apartment. In rooms where nobody knows what you're about to do and would never be fast enough to stop you if you did. Your fingers find the thing that sounds like yours and then you run back to something inherited before it can breathe."

He felt the fragment pulse once in memory, that small live-wire turn. His own thumb flattening the string against the fretboard in the apartment, choking it before it could round into a line. The park bench under him, heat pressing through the slats while he ducked back into old songs as naturally as a body flinched from flame. He hated that her sentence turned all those little motions into one recognizable habit.

"It's not finished."

"Then finish it."

"That isn't advice. That's bumper-sticker cruelty."

"No." Veritas looked ahead at the corner light changing red to green and back to its private indifference. "This is cruelty. You don't keep it unfinished because it's not ready. You keep it unfinished because unfinished things can't fail in public."

Joey stopped walking.

Veritas took one more step, then stopped too.

People moved around them. A couple carrying takeout. Two kids on skateboards. A woman dragging a dog that had found a smell worth litigation. Nobody gave them enough attention to count as witness.

"I'm not hiding," Joey said.

"You are entirely hiding."

"I'm working."

"No. You're refining camouflage."

That one made him angry enough to come clean for a second.

"If I play one of his songs and the room doesn't turn, fine. Bad night. Wrong room. Duke's machine. Talking tables. Scout with a head cold. Bartender drops a tray. Whatever." He heard the edge rising in himself and let it. "There's context. There's history. There's every other reason in the world for why a thing lands or doesn't. If I play mine and it dies, there's nowhere left to put the body except my name."

Veritas nodded once.

"Yes."

He stared at her, furious at how little room the answer left.

Cars rolled past under the streetlights in bright quick bands. Heat came off the pavement in steady invisible speech. Somewhere down the block somebody's laughter broke high and empty over a joke not worth the volume. The paper bag in Joey's hand had gone cooler, the grease setting now. He could smell mustard and bread and meat and, under all of it, the stale cardboard smell of the cup he'd thrown away. It all felt humiliatingly material. A man could be discussing artistic truth on Moreland Avenue and still need to eat supper before it spoiled.

"You keep ranking the wrong danger," Veritas said.

He turned back too fast. "You don't get to talk about broke like it's an abstraction that serves your point."

"I didn't," she said. "I said fear rearranges itself. Yours did."

He wanted to throw the bag away and didn't. Wanted to laugh and couldn't. Wanted to tell her she was reducing a whole life to one clean motive when the whole problem was that lives were never clean. Three days on Pike's paper were real. Duke was real. The buy-in was real. The silver guitar under his bed was real. So was how easy it had become to use all of it as a wall he could stand behind and still call himself honest.

Instead he said, "You think because you heard me hum something one day in the park you understand my whole pathology."

"No," Veritas said. "I think because I watched you cut it off with your own hands."

That shut him up because it was physical and therefore harder to argue with.

He hated that the two of them, coming from opposite directions, kept arriving at the same door.

"This is bullshit," he said.

"Partly."

"Which part."

"The part where you think naming the systems lets you avoid naming yourself."

He exhaled hard.

"You know, for two people who insist on accuracy, you are both unbelievable show-offs."

"Also true."

That almost got him again. Not laughter. Something meaner, closer to the body's urge to surrender because resisting every sentence was starting to feel like manual labor.

They reached the corner.

The crosswalk signal clicked over. White walking man. Then blink.

Veritas looked left. Joey kept his eyes ahead.

"I told you the first day," she said. "You have to stop hiding in his songs."

Same words. Worse now.

Not because they were new.

Because they weren't.

Veritas stepped off the curb and went left without waiting to see what he would do.

Joey stood there one beat longer, Mercury's laugh still somewhere behind his ribs and Veritas's flatter voice laid over it like a second line in a harmony nobody had asked him to learn. The room behind him stayed crooked. The world ahead stayed worse. He was still getting to his own throat first.

He went straight.

Faster than before.

As if he could get home before either truth turned into a page.
