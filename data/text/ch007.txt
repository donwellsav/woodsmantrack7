The Theft

He heard the heat before he properly saw the park.Cicadas were sawing away in the trees so hard they sounded metallic. A MARTA bus knelt on Moreland with a long hydraulic sigh and rose again with the worn dignity of something that had already done too much for one August. The fountain in Harmony Park coughed up its crooked little spray and let it fall back into the basin in tired applause. Somewhere beyond the path a delivery truck backed up with short angry beeps. Somewhere closer, dog tags clicked bright and fast, and a skateboard hit the bad seam in the sidewalk with the flat crack of wood on concrete. The air carried hot leaves, old beer breathing out of a bar door, fryer grease from the avenue, and the sweet-sour rot of trash beginning to understand itself.

The whole afternoon sounded overdone.

Joey came through the gate feeling late to his own life.

He was usually on the bench by eight forty-five, before the dog walkers thinned out and before the office people had time to promise themselves they would catch him on the way back. Now it was well past one. Maybe closer to two. The microwave had still been blinking 12:00 when he left the apartment, which meant the room remained committed to useless timekeeping, but outside there was only one hour anyway. August. Thick, bright, and mean.

He had not slept so much as failed to stay awake in any honorable pattern.

Ninety-one degrees in the apartment by midnight. The fan dead since April. Pike's voice moving around in the dark with its exact little measurements. Fourteen days. Sixteen more. Are you eating. Joey had spent half the night on top of the sheet and the other half in the chair with the notebook open in front of him like a witness he could not afford to dismiss. Around dawn he had slept just long enough to dream the frame over his father's photograph had finally straightened itself. Waking to find it still crooked had felt less like disappointment than policy.

He had nearly stayed home.

That was the part that scared him. Not the hunger doing its neat lawyerly work low in his gut. Not the heat. Not Pike's clock, though Pike's clock had gone right on ticking inside his skull after the old man left. The worst part had been standing in the kitchen looking at the guitar case and thinking, for one ugly second, that maybe the bench could go on without him for a day. Maybe the park would not notice. Maybe nobody would. Maybe he could fail in private and at least keep his dignity there.

That thought had frightened him enough to leave.

Veritas's water bottle was still on the counter by the sink.

He had looked at it while heading out. Clean silver shape against the bad dishes and the dying loaf of bread. For a second it had thrown him into his mother's kitchen years ago, summer power out, jelly jars lined up on the table with ice floating in each one like a luxury none of them could quite afford and yet kept making room for. Her hand sliding the cold glass toward him without asking if he was thirsty. His father's guitar in the next room, not playing yet, only being tuned. The memory came and went inside a breath. Objects did that. They lent you a room and took it back before you could sit down in it.

He left the bottle where it was. Carrying it would have felt too much like bringing yesterday along as a charm.

He had taken the rent notice off the table before leaving. Folded it back along the old hard crease and slid it into the case's outer pocket with the twenty. If the thing meant to follow him room to room, it could at least ride beside the guitar and do its accusing in public.

The bench looked the way work looked when it was not going well for you but had not yet granted you the drama of calling itself tragedy.

Same warped slats. Same patch of shade already losing the argument. Same path curving toward Moreland. The man with the pigeon was back, though today the pigeon had the better posture. A woman in scrubs crossed the far side of the park with the clipped forward lean of somebody coming off shift instead of heading in. Two kids under a tree were sharing a bag of chips and reading each other's insults aloud. A shirtless man on the low wall by the path had his eyes shut to the day as if sun itself were medicinal. The city kept making its little exchanges. Joey could feel the park ready to continue being itself whether he was equal to it or not.

He set the case down and sat carefully, like something in him had shifted loose overnight and might slip worse if startled.

The guitar felt heavier than it should have when he took it out. Not wrong. Not changed. Only more itself than his hands were prepared to manage. The old glued crack along the lower bout caught tacky against his thumb in the damp heat, his grandfather's repair announcing itself the way it always did in August. When the latches popped, the smell from inside the case came up warm and familiar: old felt, old wood, a faint thread of dust and smoke that never fully left no matter how many years passed. For one instant he was twelve again in the fellowship hall at Mount Calvary after a repast, watching his father close the case one-handed while talking to three different people at once, cigarette tucked behind one ear, all of them laughing too hard because the food had been free and the dead had been loved. Then the smell thinned and he was back in Harmony Park with his shirt sticking to him.

His father's guitar. Joey's now mostly because everybody else who had loved it enough to call it family was dead.

He tried not to think about how little separated inheritance from property once money got hungry enough.

The case lay open beside his foot while he got himself arranged. The rent envelope sat in the outer pocket with the red stamp visible through the gap. The folded twenty from Veritas was tucked apart from the small bills and coins like something under quarantine. Twenty-five dollars total. Still twenty-five. He had not spent any of it. That ought to have felt like discipline. What it felt like was superstition. Break the twenty and it became ordinary money. Leave it intact and maybe it could still mean something besides eggs, aspirin, and a smaller version of losing.

The red on the envelope kicked up another quick picture he had not asked for: his mother turning unpaid bills face down before supper, smoothing them with the side of her hand as if keeping bad news flat on the table counted as management. The same motion she used on napkins. On church programs. On the blanket over Joey's knees when he was sick. Practical grace, that was what she had called order when they did not have enough of it.

He put the guitar across his lap and played anyway.

Not well at first. Not because the notes were wrong. The notes themselves were older than his bad day and more durable than his body. But everything in him was half a beat behind. Thumb late. Wrist stiff. The song reaching the air just after he meant it to. He started with "Wayfaring Stranger" because of course he did. The old road. The one his hands could find while the rest of him was still trying to report for duty.

The park received the song without opening for it.

That was the best way he could think to say it. The notes went out and touched things, but nothing yielded. The fountain kept coughing on its own rhythm. The bus brakes on Moreland smeared the ends of his lines. A dog lifted its head, listened with professional doubt, and put its chin back on its paws. The kids with the chips lowered their voices for half a verse and then took them right back. The runner in the faded Braves visor slowed enough to let the chorus catch her and then moved on. The afternoon took the music the way a crowded room took a polite stranger: enough to prove it had manners, not enough to make space.

Joey could usually work with that. He had made a life out of teaching indifferent air to hold still. Today he could feel the song leaving him thinner than it ought to. Each phrase cost more breath than it earned. Each run of notes felt like carrying a box up three flights only to find out it belonged in another building.

He finished one song. Then another.

Between them the fragment lifted once under his fingers, just a small turn of melody he did not remember choosing, and he killed it so fast he nearly flinched at himself. The move had become muscle memory. Cut it off. Put a boot on its neck before it got ideas. Do not let the thing stand up long enough to embarrass you in daylight.

But even that little rise changed the air more than the old song had.

The fountain seemed to fall quieter for half a second. One of the chips kids looked up. The shirtless man on the wall opened one eye. Nothing dramatic, nothing he could have proven to anybody, only the mean small fact that the park heard the buried thing more readily than it heard the repertoire he trusted.

He hated that.

By the middle of the second song, the edges of his vision had gone white-bright with warning.

He stopped.

No applause. No coins. Just the fountain, the bus, the cicadas, his pulse knocking in his temples like a tenant pounding old pipes.

He bent and slid the guitar back into the case.

The motion hurt his shoulders more than it had any right to. Sweat ran down the sides of his face and gathered at his jaw before dropping onto the lid. He wiped it away with the heel of one hand and felt suddenly, stupidly furious at the public nature of bodies. All day long they leaked proof. Hunger had a look. Exhaustion had a smell. Bad sleep rode on a man like cheap cologne and announced itself before he got to object.

He lowered the lid without latching it all the way.

Then he pushed the case under the bench with the pocket facing him.

He did it automatically. Old practical habit. Shade was shade, even mean little shreds of it. Keep the guitar off the hot ground. Keep the money out of the first line of sight. Keep the rent envelope where he could still see it without having to touch it. The case went under with a scrape of worn vinyl and metal on concrete. From where he sat, he could still see the red edge of the notice and the faint bulge of the pocket holding all twenty-five dollars that currently stood between him and whatever came next.

His whole life fit under a public bench.

The thought should have made him more alert. Instead it made him tired in a place deeper than sleep.

He leaned back and let his head touch the slat behind him.

Just for a minute, he told himself.

That was all. One minute with his eyes shut. One minute to let the nausea pass, the heat settle, the pulse come down out of his skull. He was not going to sleep in the park like one of the men who had nowhere else to put the middle of the day. He was not that far gone. He was only resting. There was a difference, and men in trouble lived or died on their definitions.

His hand stayed on the bench slat beside him as if still keeping time.

The wood was hot enough to leave a second feeling inside the first one. The grain pressed into his palm. That texture carried another quick theft of memory: his father at the kitchen table scraping a pick across bare pine while talking through a chord change, Joey small enough then to think the table itself was helping write the song. It lasted no longer than the memory of the jelly jars had. Still long enough to sting.

He heard footsteps on gravel. A child complaining about thirst. Somebody laughing too hard at a phone. The clipped wet spit of the fountain. A siren far off, headed south toward somebody else's emergency. The city kept nudging him from all sides, but each sound seemed to move a little farther away with each breath.

Pike's voice came back first. Are you eating.

Veritas answered it from yesterday, uninvited. Safe isn't yours.

Joey wanted to tell both of them to go to hell and lacked the energy to do it even inside his own head.

He thought of the photograph above the record player. The folded twenty. The red stamp on the envelope. He thought of nothing. The order of those thoughts changed, loosened, stopped mattering.

Sleep came over him in public the way humiliation usually did: gradually, then all at once.

His mouth went slack first.

Then his shoulders.

His fingers kept moving.

Not much. A drift over the bench slat. A small tapping slide where frets would have been if the wood under his hand had known the shape of strings. His lips moved too, sound barely rising, but the fragment was there. The same stubborn turn he kept cutting off when he was awake. In sleep the censor was gone. Whatever fear he used in daylight to press the thing back under had slackened with the rest of him, and the melody leaked out the way water found a crack in bad plaster. Quiet. Persistent. Certain of its own pressure.

He did not know he was making it.

The park altered itself in small involuntary ways. A stroller wheel slowed on the path. The chips bag stopped crackling under the tree. The fountain's slap found a softer measure for a breath or two. Heat lifted off his face as somebody's shadow crossed the bench and stayed there.

Through the warm red blur behind his eyelids, he felt more than saw her.

Not because of magic. Because of exactness. The same still attention as yesterday, only closer. When his lashes dragged half open, Veritas was standing over him as ordinary as a woman waiting for a bus. The too-small bag on her shoulder. The old-young face turned down toward him. Her eyes moved once over the sleeping slack of his mouth, the hand on the slat, the case under the bench, the red mouth of the rent envelope, and back again. Not hurried. Not hungry. The look had the clean, clinical patience of an X-ray.

She crouched.

There was no flourish in it. No furtive glance around to see who was watching. She slid one hand under the bench, found the handle, and drew the case toward her with the calm economy of somebody lifting a child who had fallen asleep in the wrong room. The vinyl scraped softly against the concrete. Joey heard it somewhere inside the dream and failed to rise to it.

His fingers kept tapping the slat.

The fragment kept slipping loose through his mouth.

Veritas paused with the case half out, looked at him once more, then at the envelope, then at his hand moving uselessly against the wood as if it could still keep time for what mattered.

Then she stood up with the whole of his life in one hand and walked away at a pace too unremarkable to trouble anybody.

No one shouted.

No one pointed.

The woman in scrubs kept moving. The shirtless man on the wall shut his eye again. A cyclist rolled through the mouth of the park without looking left. Public loss always wanted witnesses and almost never got them. The park went right on being a park while theft, if calm enough, passed for an errand.

Joey slept another minute. Maybe five. Maybe twenty. Sleep in that kind of heat had no honest ruler in it.

What woke him did not feel like alarm at first.

It felt like coming up light where weight should have been.

Less shade on his face. More sun on his knees. The wrong angle in his neck. Bitter cotton in his mouth. The world coming back one sound at a time: the bus sigh on Moreland, fountain spit, a skateboard cracking over the sidewalk seam, somebody arguing lazily into a phone as if the day had not already done enough damage.

He opened his eyes to the same washed-out afternoon and for a second had no idea how much of it he had missed.

His body answered before his mind did.

He reached down under the bench for the case.

His hand met heat.

He frowned and reached farther, still not awake enough to give the mistake its proper size. His fingers swept concrete, bench leg, the dusty lip of shadow underneath. No vinyl edge. No metal latch. No familiar obstruction waiting where it ought to wait.

He straightened too fast. The park lurched white at the corners.

Then he bent and looked.

Under the bench there was only pavement. Gray, hot, empty pavement striped by the shadow of the slats.

The case was gone.

So was the rent envelope.

So was the twenty.

So was the guitar.

For one long second his mind refused the arrangement. Not denial exactly. More a stunned clerical delay. As if the world had handed him a form in the wrong order and his brain was still trying to find where the first line belonged. He put his hand under the bench again, from a different angle this time, the way you checked a tooth twice after bumping it. Nothing changed. Concrete. Heat. Dust.

His palm flattened on the ground where the case should have been.

The pavement burned.

He did not move it.
