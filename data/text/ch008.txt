Mercury's Test

His hand stayed on the pavement until the burn stopped being surprise and turned into fact.

Then Joey pulled it back.

The empty space under the bench did not fill in because he wanted it to. It stayed what it was: gray concrete, a bottle cap stomped nearly level with the grit, dust gathered at the back leg, a stripe of shade too thin to hide anything. No case. No neck of the guitar jutting where the zipper never quite sat flush. No red edge of the rent envelope showing through the pocket like a warning he had earned. Harmony Park kept moving around the vacancy with the rude competence of a weekday afternoon in Little Five. A skateboard cracked over the bad seam in the sidewalk. The fountain coughed up its thin crooked spray and let it fall back in tired applause. A MARTA bus hissed on Moreland and pulled away sounding put-upon and permanent.

Joey got down on both knees and looked again.

From the left first, because maybe panic had angles.

Then from the right.

Then flat on one forearm, cheek almost to the hot concrete, peering into the little oven-dark under the bench as if the case might have tucked itself farther back out of modesty. His fingers skated through grit hot enough to sting. They came up dirty and empty. Sweat dropped off his chin and darkened the pavement in two quick spots that vanished almost at once.

The case was gone.

Not moved. Not kicked aside by some well-meaning idiot. Not picked up by a parks worker who had mistaken his whole life for unattended property. Gone.

So was the guitar.

So was the rent envelope.

So was the twenty-five dollars that had not been enough for anything and had now, by virtue of no longer existing, become a fortune.

He stood too fast. White burst at the edges of his vision. For one bad second the whole park seemed to tilt away from him, bright and indifferent, and he had the particular humiliating fear that he might black out right here and turn a theft into an event people remembered for the wrong reasons. He caught the back of the bench with one hand and waited for the flash to settle.

Then he started looking with purpose.

Under the next bench. Behind the trash can with the split green liner breathing out hot beer and sweet rot. Along the path toward the fountain. Toward the Moreland side. Toward the patch of shade where the shirtless man had given up on upright living and stretched out like a citizen of a more failed republic. Joey walked ten feet, then twenty, then forty, scanning for the cheap exact outline of his own survival: soft black case, one latch mean, one sticker faded past color, handle wrapped once with electrical tape that had long since stopped improving anything.

Nothing.

He emptied his pockets like a man inventorying a wreck.

Keys first.

Then a quarter from his right front pocket, hot as a coin left on a dashboard. For half a second the feel of it took him back to being eight in the laundry room at the apartments on Boulevard, his mother pressing quarters into his palm two at a time and saying, do not lose these, baby, I mean it, as if money only became real after it had traveled hand to hand with instructions attached. The memory lasted exactly as long as metal against skin and then it was gone.

A dime.

A nickel from his back pocket.

A guitar pick with a chipped edge.

A folded scrap of sheet music, worn so soft at the corners it had started turning clothlike. His father used to carry tunes like that in his shirt pocket when Joey was little, not because he needed the paper on stage but because touching it between songs seemed to calm something in him. Joey looked at the scrap now as if it might count as inheritance in legal terms.

Forty cents total.

Forty cents and no guitar.

The math settled all at once. Twenty-five dollars gone. Rent notice gone. Guitar gone. Forty cents left on his person. Pike's clock still moving with the same patient legal appetite. The apartment still hot. The dead fan still dead. The bread heel on the counter still turning from stale into plan. Joey stood in Harmony Park with coins heating in his palm and felt the ugly clean drop of a life reducing itself to arithmetic.

He tried people because that was what a man was supposed to do before despair got standing.

"Excuse me," he called to a woman reading by the fountain. "You see a guitar case anywhere? Beat-up black case, sticker says HARMONY OR BUST if the light hits it right."

She looked up over her book with the face city people made when a stranger tried to assign them emergency without notice. Not cruel. Just instantly protective of her own afternoon.

"No, sorry."

A guy in a Hawks cap walking a dog got the same question. So did a man with an energy drink and a headset who pointed vaguely toward Moreland as if direction itself might count as aid. A teenager in a tattoo-shop T-shirt shrugged and said, "Nah, man, but people steal everything out here," which had the problem of being both true and useless.

The runner in the faded Braves visor was still at the edge of the clearing, one heel up on the bench, one earbud in, hamstring getting more devotional attention than most prayers. Joey almost asked her too. Then he looked at the severity of her relationship with stretching and decided against it. Some people were clearly not on assignment for anybody else's apocalypse.

He circled back to his own bench because it was still the only fixed object in the day.

Sat down hard.

The wood hit the backs of his thighs through his jeans like reprimand. Heat had soaked into the slats so completely it felt less like sitting than like being branded with municipal indifference. His empty hand kept closing as if it still expected the case handle to find it. There ought to have been a next move. Call the police, maybe. But say what. Somebody stole my forty-dollar guitar out of a public park while I slept like an idiot with my rent money in the pocket. He could already hear the dispatcher's voice going professionally flatter with each sentence.

He looked left, toward the street that led back to the apartment.

Then right, toward Moreland and all the storefront glass and heat shimmer and useless possibility of motion.

Left meant the crooked photograph, Veritas's water bottle still on the counter like a dare, the notebook open where he'd left it, Pike's clock continuing its exact work whether he came home or not. Right meant cigarettes outside the corner bar, fryer grease lifting from a vent, the crosswalk count changing for people with somewhere to be. Neither direction held a guitar.

His father would have known what to do, which was a lie Joey had told himself often enough that it arrived in the dead man's voice.

No, he corrected himself. Not his father. The framed version. The one on the wall with his eyes shut and his mouth open and no uncertainty visible anywhere in the hands. Actual fathers lost things too. Actual fathers got tired enough to make dumb decisions. Actual fathers left bills face down on tables and promised they were going to work something out before they had any respectable clue how. But the sainted version grief built for public use stayed competent forever unless something later broke the spell.

The thought did not comfort him. It only made the whole park feel flatter and hotter and more exposed.

He bent forward and pressed both palms into his eyes until color burst behind them.

"Look," he said, because there was nobody there and because he was too tired to keep up proper theological standards. "I know this is stupid."

The fountain spat. Somewhere south a siren swelled and then got smaller.

"And I know this is not how anybody's religion works. Mine either, on paper. But if there are any gods of music, or bad luck, or poor men with guitars, or dead men with decent timing, or whoever handles this kind of complaint..." He stopped and laughed once without humor. "No. That's too broad. Specifically my guitar. I'd appreciate my guitar. Not a lesson. Not a symbol. Not character development. The guitar would be enough."

Nothing happened.

Of course nothing happened.

The city kept being itself. Bus brakes. Fountain spit. A bike chain ticking under strain. Somebody behind him saying, no, if you leave now you'll hit Ponce traffic. Atlanta had room for grief, theft, prayer, and bullshit, but it rarely paused one thing for another.

He dropped his hands and stared at the dirt by his boots.

He had prayed exactly twice since the funeral. Once in the hospital bathroom while the fluorescent light buzzed overhead and his father's breathing came through the wall in machine rhythm. Once now, in Harmony Park, with baked grass and dog fur and hot concrete lifting around him. Both times he had felt embarrassed before he finished the sentence.

"Please," he added, lower now because humiliation had already done the larger work. "If anybody's listening. Please."

He heard the note before he saw the man.

Not a melody. Just one high clean sound laid into the air like silver wire, impossible to place and even harder to mistake for anything that belonged naturally in the park. Joey looked up.

Mercury stood ten or twelve feet away in a patch of late light as if he had been there the whole afternoon and the rest of reality had only just caught up with the paperwork.

He was exactly who he looked like.

White fitted clothes, theatrical without tipping all the way into costume because the body inside them had enough certainty to make the whole arrangement feel less chosen than inevitable. Mustache sharp enough to count as punctuation. Jewelry bright in ways the light should not have been able to justify. A jeweled microphone stand rested beside him like it had materialized first and then built the man upward around itself out of efficiency.

He stood there with the total self-possession of somebody who had done this before and found the administrative side of miracles mildly dull.

Twenty feet beyond him, the runner switched legs and kept stretching.

Joey stared.

"Holy shit," he said.

Mercury inclined his head just enough to suggest both greeting and stage cue. "Good evening."

"You're..."

"No."

"But you look exactly..."

"No."

Joey pointed because language had given up for a second and finger-based argument was what remained. "The outfit, the mustache, the cheekbones, the mic stand. You cannot stand there looking like that and expect me not to say what I'm about to say."

Mercury lifted one ringed finger, shut his eyes briefly, and nodded as if listening to a familiar overture he had not requested again. "Stop. I know. Everybody says it. Thousands of times. Ten thousand, give or take. I remain flattered. I remain innocent. Not him. I'm Mercury. Just Mercury. One name. Like Cher, like Madonna, like Prince, except I was doing the one-name thing before Prince was an administrative possibility."

Joey blinked. "You were doing it before Prince."

"Long before Prince. Long before his mama. Long before mamas generally. But we are not here to discuss my branding." Mercury looked him over with a level quickness that felt invasive and weirdly professional. "You look like a man who lost something important. Am I wrong?"

"How did you..."

"You called the gods of music." Mercury spread one hand. Rings flashed hard and cold. "Well. I showed up. So either the system works or you're hallucinating. Either way, tell me what happened."

Joey looked around the park once, because if he was speaking to a white-suited apparition beside a microphone stand in Harmony Park at least one other person ought to have had the courtesy to register concern.

The runner kept stretching.

A kid by the path dropped a bag of chips and swore at it.

The city, as usual, refused corroboration.

"My guitar got stolen," Joey said.

Mercury nodded gravely. "Stolen."

"While I was..." Joey nearly lied from reflex and found the lie too exhausting to carry. "Asleep. On that bench."

"You were asleep on a bench."

"Napping," Joey said. "Big difference. Sleeping on a bench is a life event. Napping on a bench is a business decision. I was between sets."

"And while you were making this business decision, somebody walked off with your guitar."

"My dad's guitar." The correction came out harder than he intended. "Before that my granddad's. Before that his daddy's. Four generations in one case with one bad sticker and two worse latches. Worth about forty dollars to the wrong man and worth..." He stopped because the sentence had started angling sentimental and he hated getting caught in it. "Look, it's what I got. And the rent envelope was in there. Twenty-five dollars. Which I know is not an amount that impresses anybody with capital. But my landlord has a clock running and he does not accept family history as currency."

Mercury's expression shifted by less than an inch. It still registered as attention. "I see."

"So." Joey opened his empty hand. The coins clicked against each other meanly in his sweat. "That's the emergency."

Mercury looked at the bench. Looked at Joey. Looked at the late sun slipping through the trees in hot slats of gold. Then he said, simply, "Wait here."

He vanished.

Not in a blaze. Not even in a respectable explosion. More in a brief excess of glitter and a high slipping falsetto sound with no visible source, which meant Joey's nerves felt it before his ears could sort it. Smoke rose where there ought not to have been smoke and lost interest in being smoke almost immediately. The runner touched her earbud and frowned at the trees.

Then Mercury was back.

He held a guitar case Joey had never seen before.

He set it on the bench between them, popped the latches with a flourish that somehow made latches sound aristocratic, and opened it.

Inside lay a guitar made entirely of gold.

Not gold hardware. Not lacquer with delusions. Solid gold, or something so convincingly gold the distinction had stopped mattering. It caught the late light and threw it back in every direction like a mirror arguing with the sun. The thing did not look musical. It looked expensive in the way certain churches looked expensive, all testimony plated over transaction. Joey thought of offering trays moving down pews in childhood, brass bright enough to make generosity feel monitored.

Mercury gestured elegantly.

"Is this your guitar?"

Joey stared at the case. Then at Mercury. Then back at the case because certain offenses deserved a second look.

"My guitar is not made of gold."

"But say it were." Mercury's voice stayed courteous, nearly bored, the way a banker might ask a man to confirm the last four digits of his Social Security number before deciding the rest of the afternoon. "Hypothetically. Say you went to sleep with a wooden guitar and woke up with this. Would this be it?"

Joey laughed once before he could stop himself. "If I had a gold guitar, I would not be sleeping in a park. I'd be sleeping in the Four Seasons."

"Nice lobby there."

"Wouldn't know." Joey kept looking at the guitar because not looking at it had become theater he did not respect. "And that thing's worth more than my whole bloodline on paper. My guitar is wood and glue and family damage. That right there?" He nodded toward the gold body. "That is not my life. That's somebody else's idea of getting out."

"You're sure this isn't yours."

"I'm sure."

Mercury leaned against the microphone stand like a man easing into the portion of the show where the audience accidentally told the truth. "Let's do it one more time. Gold guitar. You say three words, yeah, that's mine, and your afternoon improves. No more bench. No more rent math. No more turning pockets out like a witness. Is this your guitar?"

Joey looked at the gold instrument and felt temptation arrive where it always arrived: not in his ethics but in his body. Air-conditioning that worked. Groceries bought without theater. Pike's face forced into reluctant neutrality when Joey handed over actual money. A night's sleep behind a locked door. The lie would solve things. Not all things. But enough things to sound dangerously similar to mercy.

Then his hands ruined it.

Even from where he sat, he knew what would happen if he picked the thing up. The neck would sit wrong. The weight would feel arrogant. The body, smooth and immaculate and rich with the smell of itself, would not have that one tacky patch near the old repaired crack where his fingers always caught in August.

"No," he said.

Mercury's brows rose half a measure. "No."

"No." Joey shook his head harder now that the answer had surfaced. "My granddad tried to fix the crack with Elmer's glue. The white school kind. Like he was repairing a science-fair volcano. Didn't fix a damn thing. Made it worse. Made it sticky. There's a spot on the lower bout where your fingers catch if the weather's hot, and the weather's always hot enough in this city. I know exactly where that spot is because I've been playing that guitar since I was thirteen and hearing stories about it longer than that." He tapped his own sternum once. "That one?" He pointed at the gold. "Ain't mine."

For a second the tacky glue seam came back to him as vividly as touch. His grandfather on the porch in Lithonia with the guitar across his knees, newspaper spread underneath, cigarette burning down in the ashtray while he swore at the crack in a low respectful tone, as if even damage required manners. Joey had been little enough then to believe glue was a kind of prayer. The memory flashed and was gone.

Mercury regarded him another beat. Then he shut the case.

"Wait here."

Gone again.

This time the return took longer, or maybe Joey's pulse had finally learned to count around impossible events. When Mercury reappeared, he carried a different case.

He opened it.

A silver guitar lay inside. Clean. Shining. Expensive in a colder, meaner key. Not miraculous the way the gold one was. Clinical. It looked like something a man bought after he had already decided polish mattered more than memory.

"This one, then," Mercury said.

Joey looked once, briefly, and felt almost grateful for the easier answer.

"Also not mine."

"It's silver." Mercury sounded mildly persuasive now, as if he were helping Joey settle on a jacket. "Let's say your guitar underwent a partial upgrade while you were out."

"Guitars do not upgrade. That's not a thing guitars do."

"You'd be surprised what happens while people nap."

"Mine's wood, man." Joey could hear himself getting more certain as he spoke, the way the body did when it recognized its own instrument faster than the mind recognized its own argument. "It has a sticker on it and one latch that'll cut you if you're not paying attention. It smells like old felt and smoke and car trunks in August. If you open the case too fast the neck shifts because the padding's worn out on one side. It sounds like four broke men played it for a hundred years, because that is exactly what happened. It ain't gold. It ain't silver. It's wood."

Mercury sighed then, but not theatrically. More like a being recalibrating expectations downward.

He disappeared in a short impossible chord no single human throat ought to have been able to make. Glitter went sideways in a breeze that had not existed one second earlier. Smoke threaded itself into a shape above the bench and then lost nerve before Joey could read it.

The runner jerked a hand to one ear.

Her earbud had slipped loose. She frowned at the trees, at the path, at nothing Joey could name, then looked over at him because he was the only available suspect.

Joey, still running on heat and panic and now apparently divine customer service, heard himself ask, "You seen a guitar around here?"

She blinked once, shoved the earbud back in, and resumed stretching with punishing devotion.

Committed to the bit, Joey thought.

Mercury returned carrying a third case.

The instant Joey saw the sticker on the side, his whole body moved before the rest of him had time to organize permission.

HARMONY OR BUST, faded past color.

Mercury laid it on the bench and opened it.

There it was.

Wood. Old. Beat-up. The crack in the body, badly mended. The warped line of dried glue. The cheap familiar smell rising up from the case like a room reopened after bad weather. In the compartment sat the envelope, thin as ever. Fives. Singles. One ten. The red stamp still shouting from the paper. No miracle added. No interest paid. Pike's clock had not taken the hint.

"Oh my God," Joey said, and the words came out from lower than talk usually lived. "That's it. That's my guitar."

He reached for it too fast, then gentled immediately, ashamed of the snatch. The body hit his chest. The neck settled into his palm. His thumb found the worn place at the back where years of sweat had polished the finish to a softer darker shine. His fingertips found the tacky repair seam without looking.

Home.

Relief went through him so quickly it did not stop to ask dignity for permission. His chest opened all at once like somebody had cut a strap loose. He laughed once, sharp and disbelieving, and then because the body occasionally outran shame when it got something back it had already mourned, he actually jumped. Both feet off the ground. A quick stupid burst of gratitude with no audience worth naming.

Mercury watched with what might have been delight or might only have been exact amusement.

"Play me something," he said.

Joey sat because standing with the guitar felt too much like daring the universe to repossess it. He laid the instrument across his lap and let one hand rest on the sticky repaired place for a second. The tack under his fingertip told him what the miracle had not. Real. Still his. Still wrong in exactly the right way.

He tuned by habit even though the strings were near enough. Tiny corrections. Low E a little flat from whatever impossible transit had just occurred. B a hair sharp. He heard the changes before he trusted them. Sound first, always. The park answered each plucked note differently now that panic had stepped down out of his ears. The fountain's cough fell into the spaces. Traffic on Moreland hissed under the lower strings. Somewhere a bottle hit a bin behind the bar and gave him a clean accidental fifth.

He did not play "Wayfaring Stranger." That one felt too exposed now, too close to the fragment Veritas had heard and the accusation Mercury had already started forming just by standing there. Instead he went older and meaner and less famous, one of those minor-key songs that traveled like a man through familiar dark. The kind his father used to pull out for small rooms where people listened with their shoulders instead of their mouths.

Joey let his hands find it.

At first he only played. No singing. The strings had to answer the panic still climbing down out of him. He could feel the tremor in his right hand during the first measure, hear the over-bright click of a pick striking harder than needed. By the third line through, the old muscle memory took the lead. Wrist loosened. Thumb settled. Breath lined up behind the rhythm instead of tripping over it.

Then he sang.

Not loudly. He was too emptied out for loud. But clean.

The park did what rooms did when something true entered them before anybody had agreed to host it. Not a hush, not a miracle, just a slight involuntary rearranging. The woman by the fountain lowered her book a little. The skeptical dog lifted its head and did not put it back down. Somebody outside the corner bar laughed and then stopped on the edge of the next note as if he had forgotten what the joke was. Even the crooked fountain seemed to find the measure and keep to it for half a verse before resuming its own bad drumming. The place did not fall at Joey's feet. It simply admitted him for the length of the song.

Mercury listened with the total professionalism of somebody who knew exactly what good music cost and had long ago stopped being dazzled by pretty surfaces.

"Why the old stuff?" he asked when Joey let the last chord fade. "I'm curious."

The question hit the part of Joey that always wanted history when honesty felt too naked.

"Because the old stuff is the truth," he said.

Mercury did not interrupt.

Dangerous choice.

"That's what my dad used to say. Old folk songs are people telling the truth in a form the world will let survive. Rivers, crossing over, strangers, Canaan, mountains. You couldn't always say the thing plain and live through it, so you taught it to a melody and let it travel." Joey heard himself getting going and could not stop it. Once deflection found a pulpit, it liked to preach.

Mercury remained still.

Joey pressed on because once he had started he could feel the whole old family argument climbing up through his chest, and he needed somebody impossible to hear that this was not laziness, not cowardice, not merely lack.

"My great-granddad was a sharecropper in Alabama. You know what a sharecropper does for entertainment? He sings. Because singing is free and everything else costs money. So the songs get handed down because handing them down is free and losing them is expensive. Not expensive like cash. Expensive like losing the person who taught it to you. You lose the song, you lose their mouth, their weather, the way they bent one line different from everybody else. Then they're not just dead. They're gone-gone."

The word took him, briefly, to his mother in the kitchen humming under the rattle of the cheap vent fan, not performing, not even thinking of herself as singing, only holding the day together one task at a time. Her hand sliding a skillet off the burner. His father in the next room tuning the guitar with the seriousness some men reserved for prayer. The memory arrived off the smell of old wood and hot air and vanished again before it could soften him.

"And poor people," Joey said, quieter now because he had reached the center of it, "don't get much that stays theirs. Not really. Jobs don't stay. Apartments don't stay. Men don't stay. Records warp, cars die, landlords age into paperwork. But songs can stay if somebody carries them right. So yeah. I sing the old ones. Because they're true, and because they're the last free inheritance some people ever get."

"And you?" Mercury asked. "What do you sing?"

Joey frowned. "I just did."

"No." Mercury's tone stayed so light it became unbearable. "Your people sang. Your father sang. You sang what they brought you. What do you sing?"

The question landed like thrown brickwork. Joey felt it hit in the ribs.

"That's not..." He looked down at the guitar because the guitar was at least on his side about materials. "That's not what we do."

"No?"

"We carry the songs." Joey heard how plain it sounded and hated the need underneath it. "We're carriers."

Mercury's eyes sharpened almost invisibly. Subtle enough that a sane man might have missed it. Joey did not.

"Convenient," Mercury said.

"No. Faithful."

"Mm."

"You say that like those are enemies."

"Sometimes they are."

Joey felt irritation rise because irritation was easier than fear and came dressed in better clothes. "You asked why I sing the old stuff. I told you. You want me to apologize for not being a genius in public?"

"I'm asking a simpler question than that."

"Then ask it simpler."

Mercury obliged.

"Your great-granddad sang them. Granddad sang them. Your father sang them. You sing them. Hundred years of Woodsmans, give or take. In a full century of all that carrying, nobody ever put one new song in the box?"

Silence sat down between them.

The fountain sputtered.

A motorcycle went by on Moreland so loud the engine seemed to peel a strip off the air.

Joey looked at the neck of the guitar. At the half-moon wear where his own thumb sat. At the darkened spots on the spruce where three generations of right hands had marked the same labor. He wanted an answer he could respect. The one he had was smaller.

"That's not what we do," he said again, and heard this time how much it sounded like a fence built after the property line had already failed.

"You know what they call a person," Mercury asked pleasantly, "who carries something that isn't his and never puts anything of his own in the box?"

Joey looked up despite himself. "What."

Mercury smiled with terrible kindness.

"A delivery man. UPS driver with a good voice."

The line hit clean.

Joey sat there with the guitar across his lap and felt, with a particular exhausted hatred, that the insult worked mostly because it had chosen the exact word he did not have an answer for. Delivery man. Carrier. Custodian. Loyal son. Fraud. The categories shuffled against each other so fast he could not keep them separate.

"That's not fair," he said, and even to him it sounded like the kind of sentence people used when fairness was not the argument.

"Neither is rent."

"Neither is a man stealing a family guitar from a public park."

"Correct." Mercury spread his hands. "World's thick with ugly pairings. Stay with me."

He bent, reopened the gold case as casually as another man might open a tackle box, and nudged it toward Joey with the toe of one white boot. Then the silver.

"Keep them."

Joey stared. "What."

"The gold. The silver. Sell them, play them, worship them, use them as doorstops, I do not care. They're yours."

"Why would I..."

"Because I just put a gold guitar in front of a broke man and he said no, that ain't mine. Then I put silver in front of the same man and he said no again. You know how many people do that?" Mercury's mouth twitched. "In my experience, which is longer than you want to hear about, almost nobody. Almost nobody turns down gold. They take it. They smile. They call themselves practical. Then they spend the next ten years wondering why the thing in their hands never once answers back like home."

He tapped Joey's knuckles with one ringed finger. Light touch. Heavy fact.

"Because hands know, Joey. Hands always know. You can bullshit a room. You can bullshit your landlord. You can even bullshit your own story for a while if grief is doing half the work. But you cannot bullshit your hands."

The park had tipped toward evening without asking permission. Light went amber at the edges. The fountain sounded thinner. Traffic on Moreland had changed from daytime hurry to end-of-day irritation, everybody wanting home at once and none of them willing to admit how tired they were. The heat had not broken. It had only sunk lower and gotten meaner.

Joey looked at the gold. Then the silver. Then down at the wooden guitar across his knees, tacky glue and all.

Gold shone like money pretending to be salvation.

Silver sat there cool and exact and unlived, a polished answer to a question nobody in his family had ever asked. It looked like the kind of instrument a man could play flawlessly while never once risking anything true.

Wood. Gold. Silver.

"Sing one with me," Joey said suddenly, because the day had already gone beyond normal requests and he no longer trusted the remaining boundaries. He nodded toward the jeweled stand. "One song. For the park."

Mercury laughed, really laughed, head tipping back just enough to show the line of his throat.

"I can't do that."

"Why not."

"Oh, you do not want that headache." Mercury winced as if remembering paperwork older than paper. "There are legal issues. Contracts. Obligations extending past the mortal plane. Estates are messy. Publishing is a nightmare. There are people with a lot of money and no sense of humor who would make your life very difficult."

Joey glanced at the gold case. "I got a gold guitar now. I can hire a lawyer."

Mercury looked at him with an expression that crossed, for the length of a breath, into something almost like affection before closing the door on both of them.

"No, Joey. You truly cannot."

He swung the microphone stand up over one shoulder.

"Take care of yourself," he said. "Keep playing."

Then he paused, and whatever in him had been performing went very still.

The park noise did not stop, exactly. It only seemed to lose confidence. The next bus sounded farther away than it should have. The fountain's little slap against the basin thinned out. Joey felt the back of his neck go cold in spite of the heat.

"And do me a favor."

Joey tightened his hand on the neck of the wooden guitar. "What."

Mercury's eyes settled on him with the full bright force of somebody who had been joking until exactly this second.

"That thing underneath the old songs. The thing you keep pushing down." He nodded once, not toward the cases, not toward the bench, but toward Joey's chest as if the address had been settled a long time ago. "Stop pushing it down."

The words did not hit like accusation. Worse. They hit like recognition.

Joey thought of the fragment slipping out in sleep. Of how quickly he'd killed it every time it tried daylight. Of Veritas in the park hearing one turn of melody and naming it his before he had even admitted it existed. Of how the park had listened harder to those accidental bars under his hand than it had listened to the songs he trusted. He wanted to say he didn't know what Mercury meant. He wanted to say there was nothing there. He wanted to make a joke and put some furniture back in the room.

He did not get the chance.

Mercury was gone.

Falsetto. Glitter. Smoke. Nothing that counted as explanation.

The park remained.

The fountain still coughed.

The runner trotted off at last, saved from no visible danger and unimpressed by all of it.

Joey sat with the wooden guitar in his lap and two impossible cases beside him and evening pressing close around the bench.

Gold on one side.

Silver on the other.

Wood in his hands.

He looked down at the forty cents still scattered on the bench beside his thigh, then at the recovered envelope in the pocket, the red stamp still shouting, then at the impossible shine of the other two instruments. Relief had arrived, yes, but it had not come dressed as rescue. It had come dressed as a harder question. What belonged to him. What he had only carried. What poverty would let him keep. What it would force him to sell. What his father's hands had passed down besides chord changes and hiding places.

He was still broke enough to count coins.

Still hungry enough to think first about what the gold might weigh at a pawn shop.

Still uneasy enough to know the silver was worse in a quieter way. Gold announced itself. Silver only waited to be mistaken for improvement.

Still exactly the man Mercury had looked at and called a delivery driver with inherited cargo.

He stood and tried to gather all three cases at once.

This turned out to be idiotic.

He got two by the handles, made it four steps, set them down, went back for the third, reconsidered, came back, tried balancing the wooden case against his knee while the silver one slid outward and the gold one nearly cracked him in the shin, swore under his breath at the logistics of miracles, and finally stood in the middle of Harmony Park looking like a musician who had either robbed the world's strangest store or been punished by a fairy tale with carrying labor.

The evening had gone that coppery color Atlanta got just before full dark in August, when the heat still clung to the brick and the sky over Moreland looked smoked at the edges. Cars kept moving. Somebody on Euclid shouted for another round. A door at the bar opened and let out old beer, fryer grease, and one chopped-off line of music from somebody else's set. The city gave no sign that anything had been amended on Joey's behalf.

Above all of it, louder than traffic and meaner than the joke, Mercury's last line stayed where it had landed.

Stop pushing it down.
