4:52 A.M.

The needle kept going after the song quit.

It made the same small dry sound every time around, a hiss with a scratch buried in it where the groove had run out and nobody had lifted the arm. Joey sat through one turn. Then another. Then another. By the fifth he was on the floor without being able to say exactly how he'd gotten there.

His back hit the side of the bed. His knees came up. One heel slid on loose paper and shot a page under the chair. The apartment stayed exactly as poor as it had been ten minutes earlier. Dishes in the sink. Half a loaf in the bread bag going stiff on the counter. The microwave over the stove blinking 12:00 at a room that knew better. The notebook draped over Pike's notice and the newer white sheet beneath it, one red block of print and one clean white corner still showing at the bottom. The chair that had probably outlived three tenants and would likely outlive him. The silver case under the bed with one bright latch catching the weak stove light. The bad screen in the window letting in a narrow stripe of Atlanta night that smelled like warm brick, old grease, and the damp-sweet edge of August trying, finally, to die.

Nothing in the room had changed except the one fact that made all the other facts swing around it.

His father had written a song.

The photograph still lay face-down on the table where Joey had left it, the backing paper dull in the weak light. The blue-ink set list was still burning in his head.

His father had written a song.

Not a fragment. Not a private hum lost over dishwater. A real song. A titled song. Pressed onto a record and carried into a room under his own name.

He scrubbed a hand over his face hard enough to drag the skin.

"You son of a bitch," he said, not loudly.

The apartment gave him back fridge hum, a plumbing knock inside the wall, somebody walking heavy in the hall, and the runout hiss from Clarence's record.

He laughed once, sharp and joyless.

"No, go on," he said. "Stay dead and dignified. That's convenient."

He was not a man who talked to rooms for comfort. He had seen enough lonely men doing that at bus stops, diner counters, outside package stores, on benches after the park emptied, to know the difference between prayer and leaking. This was not prayer. This was leaking.

He pushed himself up far enough to reach the table and turn the photograph back over.

His father in mid-song. Eyes shut. Mouth open. Gone somewhere Joey had spent half his life mistaking for certainty. Joey had built a whole private church out of that face. A church where the old songs were enough. A church where carrying counted as courage. A church where authorship belonged to men with a different appetite and a worse vanity. A church where devotion and fear could wear each other's coats and nobody in the pews was rude enough to notice.

Now the face looked different.

Not false. Worse. Incomplete.

"You couldn't mention it?" Joey asked. "One time. Any time. Hey, by the way, I maybe left a page out."

The record kept turning.

He got up on one knee, reached over, and lifted the needle. The sudden absence of the hiss made the room ring.

That was worse.

He sat back down hard enough to jar the bedframe. Springs complained under the mattress. Somewhere outside, a bus sighed at the stop on Moreland, doors folding open and shut like a man too tired to commit to anger.

"No," he said, and this time he knew what he meant by it. "No, you do not get to do that to me."

He thought of the kitchen table in the old apartment, the one with the burn mark from a pan his mother had set down without looking. His father bent over the wooden guitar while Joey was still small enough for the body of the instrument to look like furniture. Big patient hands. Dry fingertips. Coffee and summer sweat and the woolly smell of old case lining. His father telling him where the tune lived, where the change came, which fingers to trust when his head got crowded.

The old songs first.

Always the old songs first.

The old songs lasted because they were built to carry weight.

The old songs did not belong to one man, and that was part of what made them holy.

Joey had not invented that theology. He had inherited it with the chord shapes.

And all that time there had been Track Seven, one original tucked into the set list like contraband and then sealed back up.

Joey put both hands over his mouth because for one hot second he thought he might throw up. He did not. He only breathed into his own palms and waited for the room to stop pitching.

It didn't.

Anger came cleaner than grief just then. More useful. It let him keep his shape.

"What was I carrying, then?" he asked the table. "A torch? Is that what I was supposed to think?"

His voice came back from the walls smaller than he meant it to.

"Because it feels a lot more like I got handed a box of breakables with your name on it and told not to drop anything."

That landed. He felt it land.

The worst part was not the hidden song. Not even the lie, if lie was the right name for it. The worst part was feeling, all at once, how much of his own life he had arranged around a man who had not been as simple as Joey had needed him to be. He had made a shrine out of a partial truth because a partial truth took less upkeep. Dust it. Obey it. Hand it to yourself as a rule and call the rule love.

Veritas in the park, cool as bad news: safe isn't yours.

Mercury in that ridiculous suit, amused and cruel and not wrong: delivery man.

Joey pushed both voices away so hard his shoulders cramped.

"I know what they said," he muttered. "I don't need the chorus."

He looked back at the photograph.

"Were you scared to tell me?" he asked. "Or was I too young when you still had the chance? Or did you decide one song was enough trouble for one lifetime and the better inheritance was to keep me out of it?"

He waited as if there might actually be an answer hiding somewhere behind the cheap glass.

There wasn't. Only his own mind, mean enough now to try both sides.

Maybe his father had meant to tell him later.

Maybe later got eaten by hospital rooms and money and bad lungs and all the ordinary thefts a life could commit against itself.

Maybe the song had cost more than Joey knew.

Maybe his father had heard exactly what it cost to step one inch out of the museum and say something true in his own name, and maybe he had wanted to spare his son that bill.

Joey hated those maybe's even while they lined up.

"You don't get points for protection you never explained," he said.

He got up too fast, had to brace one hand against the wall, then crossed the room and picked the photograph off the table. The frame was light. Cheap. A five-dollar object made heavy by the life Joey had hung on it. Dust had gathered in the top seam. One corner still wore the old white nick from the move to this apartment. He held it at chest height and looked at the man inside.

"You know what I did with you?" he asked.

The father's face, frozen in song, did not improve under questioning.

"I let you stay perfect because it saved me time. That's what I did. I let you stay dead and right so I could keep doing the one thing I knew how to do and call it devotion."

He swallowed. The next sentence came out before he could dress it.

"You could've said it was allowed."

The word sat there between him and the frame.

Allowed.

As if he were still a boy waiting on the dead to stamp his passport. As if what hurt most was not only the omission but the humiliating proof that even now, at this hour, on this floor, he was still asking permission from the same man he was cussing.

He shut his eyes.

"Listen to me," he said, disgusted with himself. "Thirty-something years old and I'm still asking you for a hall pass."

He set the frame down more carefully than before. Not gentle. Just exact.

Then he pulled the notebook toward him. Its bottom edge snagged Pike's notice and dragged it half an inch before the paper caught. One red corner stayed visible under the cover while he flipped through the pages of crossed-out failures.

Every bad attempt was still there because he had never quite been able to tear them out. Four lines that sounded like hymns after a sedative. Six lines so careful they might as well have been cut into a memorial stone. One chorus built almost entirely out of words his father might have used, which had been exactly the problem. One page with almost nothing on it but a date and a chord progression, as if even failure had to look organized. The paper was pressed through with old indents from nights he had gripped the pen too hard and produced nothing. Under the stove light those grooves looked like the record of a man trying to write without ever letting himself say anything unforgivable.

He read them and felt contempt rise so fast it almost made him grin.

"Jesus," he said. "No wonder."

He tore out three pages in a row. The sound startled him. Paper went softer than he expected once it gave. He tore out two more. Crossed-out ghosts in his lap. He dropped them on the floor. Another page. Another. Soon there was a pale drift of them around his feet, all those failed respectable attempts finally demoted to trash.

The room looked better immediately.

Not cleaner. Truer.

As he stood, the silver case under the bed caught the lamp again. For one second he pictured taking it out. The brighter body. The harder shine. The cleaner lie. He left it where it was.

He went to the chair and took up the wooden guitar.

The familiar weight of it hit his hand, then his forearm, then the middle of his chest when he settled back onto the bed instead of the floor. The neck had that honest shine his father used to talk about, worn smooth where four sets of hands had trusted the same stretch of wood. Joey's thumb found the shallow groove near the third fret and for a second he was back at the kitchen table with his father tapping the side of a cooling mug while thinking through a change. Once for maybe. Twice for no. Three times if the song, or the bills, or the day had moved past language.

The memory went quick. The feel of the groove stayed.

"All right," he said to nobody. "Then let's need something."

He played the fragment.

It showed up the way it always did, sideways, trying to slip in under the guardrail of an old standard. For weeks it had been the melody that shoved itself under "Wayfaring Stranger" or leaned its shoulder into a hymn and tried to make room. Joey had been batting it back like an unpaid bill.

This time he did not stop it.

He let the phrase finish.

Then the next phrase.

Then the turn he always dodged because the turn was where the thing stopped sounding borrowed and started sounding like it had his address on it.

He missed the chord, swore, backed up, played it again.

No audience. No room. No Duke. No bench with strangers drifting past and deciding what part of him they meant to buy or bless or ignore. Just him, the bed sagging under one hip, the stove light, the photograph on the table, and his father's hidden act of treason or courage or both hanging in the air like permission nobody had asked to receive.

The melody held.

Not pretty. Not finished. But held.

He tried words. The first line that came out sounded two hundred years old and false as courthouse wood. He stopped.

"No."

He crossed it out before the ink even dried.

Second try. Too much father in it. Not theft, exactly, but imitation with its church clothes on. He could hear one of his father's cadences perched on the line like a man waiting for his seat to be offered back to him.

Crossed out.

Third try. Better rhythm. Wrong nouns. As soon as he said river, he knew he was hiding. He had not seen a river all day. He had seen cracked sidewalks, the green bench at Harmony Park, a MARTA bus exhaling at the curb, Mae's coffee, Pike's jaw, Duke's ledger, the red digits of the pawn shop sign buzzing in the heat weeks ago when he was still dumb enough to think objects could solve a life.

He was not writing about a river.

Crossed out.

He put the guitar down, stood, paced the length of the room, came back, sat, played the first two lines again, lost them, cursed, found them, wrote a chord over the margin, scratched an arrow, stood again. The room had too much furniture for pacing and too much paper on the floor for not pacing. He did it anyway. Heel, toe, heel, toe, around the chair, past the milk crate, past the sink, back to the bed.

Writing, it turned out, was not dignified work.

He should have known that already. He had watched songs happen in bars, churches, kitchens, green rooms, record shops, funeral repasts, parking lots after midnight. The good moments arrived whole enough to make people lie later and call them gifts. What nobody bragged on was the labor around them. Repetition. Embarrassment. The hour and a half of sounding stupid before one line finally quit lying.

He sat again and played the verse from the top.

The song wanted morning in it. Not his father's morning. That title was taken. Morning as pressure instead. Morning as hour. Morning as the part of the day when the city had not yet decided what kind of story it meant to tell. Joey knew that hour. Delivery trucks backing into alley mouths. The first bus brakes whining. Heat not yet fully awake to its own cruelty. Barbacks smoking outside while bakery people came in. Harmony Park before the children, before the dog walkers, before the joggers who liked his music as long as it looked healthy from a distance. He knew what Atlanta sounded like before it put on its daytime face.

That was the ground he could stand on.

He started there.

The line came leaner this time. Less decoration. A little shame in it. Good. Shame meant he had stopped posing.

He wrote it down.

Next line took twenty minutes and a glass of tap water gone warm in his hand.

He kept trying to sound worthy of the tradition when the tradition was not the point. The point was to tell the truth without snapping the music off its bones. Every time he reached for a phrase that smelled like weathered hymnbook, the line went dead in his mouth. Every time he let the city in, it moved.

Streetlight through bent blinds.

Sweat cooling on the back of his neck after a night that had run too long.

A landlord's notice with one red corner still showing under the notebook.

A landlord's knock still living in the body after the hallway emptied.

A father made human by one rough track on a nearly forgotten record.

The room gathered around those facts.

Not metaphor first. Fact first.

He played the verse again. The second half collapsed. He crossed out half the page. Pencil shavings from the cheap sharpener flecked the table. At some point the eraser tore and started leaving pink smears. He used the edge of his thumb instead and rubbed graphite into the heel of his hand.

Outside, a car came through too fast on Moreland. Bass rumbled under the window, then was gone. Somewhere in the building a toilet flushed. Somebody coughed a long smoker's cough that ended in a silence so complete it made Joey briefly hold still and listen for the next breath.

He realized he was hungry.

He ignored it.

He replayed Track Seven.

Not to steal from it. To hear where his father had left the rough edges. To hear the places where a line almost broke and the song survived anyway. To hear that an original did not have to arrive varnished to count.

This time he heard the fear in it too.

Not cowardice. Exposure.

His father sounded like a man who had opened a door and was singing from the threshold without knowing what waited on the other side.

That did not forgive the hiding. It did not make a virtue out of silence.

But it wrecked the cleaner version of Joey's anger. The voice on the record was too bare for villainy, too human for sainthood. Whatever had happened after he wrote it, whatever decision or exhaustion or grief had sent him back into the standards and shut his mouth around Track Seven, the first act itself had cost him.

"All right," Joey said to the speaker. "Maybe."

It was not absolution.

It was the end of the simpler story.

He took up the guitar again.

The chorus had been the problem all along. Verses he could bully into shape if he kept the images specific. The chorus was where he kept trying to make a statement, and statements were where the dead climbed onto his shoulders and started arranging his face for him.

He played through to the turn and let the melody rise where it wanted instead of forcing it down into something solemn. The note held longer than he expected. Not flashy. Just open. The kind of note that risked plainness because plainness was the only honest register left.

The words that followed were not grand. Thank God.

They were about surviving the hour before daylight. About what a city sounded like when almost everybody in it was between selves. About carrying somebody and being flattened by them and loving them and resenting the weight in the same breath. About grief dressed up as duty. About how a song could come out of a family and still keep one man's fingerprints on it.

He played it once and lost the last line.

Again. Better.

Again. Wrong chord in the bridge.

Again.

Again.

Sometime after three, the song stopped feeling like a confession he was prying out of himself with a screwdriver and started behaving more like a structure. Not finished forever. Just standing up long enough for him to walk around it and see what still needed bracing.

He wrote faster after that.

Not because inspiration had finally descended. Because the choices kept shrinking. Once a line told the truth, the neighboring lies could not crowd it so easily. That saved time.

He crossed out a phrase that sounded like his grandfather. Crossed out another that sounded like a songwriter in a better apartment pretending to be broke for atmosphere. Crossed out one line because it was smart and therefore useless. Kept the line after it because it embarrassed him a little and named an actual thing.

The floor disappeared under paper.

He stopped noticing the heat.

At one point he looked up and realized the dark at the window had thinned from black to charcoal. Not dawn yet. Just the city's edges preparing themselves. The first delivery truck knocked over a bottle somewhere down the block. A bird tried one uncertain note and thought better of it. The air through the bad screen came in a degree cooler than it had an hour before. Not enough to call mercy. Enough to register change.

He drank the rest of the tap water and filled the glass from the kitchen sink without putting the guitar all the way down, neck trapped in the crook of his elbow like a child he didn't trust the room with. When he came back, he stepped on one of the torn-out pages and it stuck briefly to his bare heel before sliding off.

He read a line he'd written two hours earlier and hated it on sight.

Good.

That meant he was moving.

He crossed it out hard enough to tear the paper, copied the chord progression onto a fresh page, and started the verse again.

This time the song came in cleaner.

Not purer. Just less defended.

His voice had gone rough from use. The pads of his fingers burned. The apartment smelled like paper dust, old dishwater, graphite, warm string metal, and the faint penny tang that rose off the fretboard when sweat got into it. Clarence's record still waited on the turntable. The photograph kept catching light at one corner and spitting it back. Under the bed, the silver case threw a colder shine whenever he turned too far that way. The notebook had become proof. The frame had become evidence. The guitar was a tool under strain. Even the microwave with its stupid 12:00 looked less like malfunction than insult.

He sang the whole thing through.

Verse.

Turn.

Chorus.

Second verse.

Bridge that nearly collapsed, then didn't.

Last chorus, pared down harder than he'd had it before because too many words had been his other hiding place.

The room answered.

Not with applause. Not with revelation. With accommodation.

The fridge cycled off in the middle of the second verse and left the line standing naked. A pipe knocked once, then held. The bus below breathed at the curb and became part of the measure before moving on. The blinds gave one dry rattle at the bridge and then quieted. Even the city, which had no reason to care what one broke man was doing above Moreland before dawn, seemed willing for one pass to put its noise between his lines instead of across them.

When he stopped, the silence that followed did not feel empty or accusatory.

It sat in him like work he had actually finished.

He stayed still so he would not spook it.

The phone on the table lit when the screen woke under his hand: 4:52 a.m.

The microwave kept insisting it was 12:00.

He looked from one clock to the other and laughed, this time like a man who had actually found something funny.

"All right," he said.

The notebook lay open under his hand. The page was scarred with arrows and brackets and cross-outs. A whole night's argument preserved in graphite. In the margin, beside the place where he had cut an entire couplet and replaced it with four blunt words, he wrote one more:

true

Not because the song was finished. Not because he trusted himself suddenly and forever. Just because that version had stopped flinching. The page looked work-made now, not bestowed. Something he could fold and carry into daylight if he had to.

He played it again to be sure.

The hands knew more now.

Not everything. Enough.

When he set the guitar down at last, the room looked wrecked. Paper everywhere. Pencil on the table. Water glass half-empty. Record still on the turntable. Lamp burning over a sink full of dishes as if that had been the central domestic project all night. He looked like hell, felt worse, and could not remember the last time he had been that tired without also being numb.

The photograph waited on the table.

He reached for it with both hands.

Up close, in the first raw hint of morning, his father looked less saintly than he did at night. The cheap gloss caught every flaw. Dust in the inside corner of the frame. A faint bend along one edge of the print. Stage lights too harsh for the low ceiling they were working under. Shirt collar slightly off. Sweat dark under one arm. Mouth open not in holiness but in effort. A working musician in a small room, trying to carry something across.

Track Seven had not made him smaller.

It had made him measurable.

Joey stood, crossed to the wall above the milk-crate turntable, and found the nail by feel.

For years he had let the frame hang crooked because the crookedness had come to feel like part of the object, part of mourning, part of the contract. Straightening it had felt like meddling with a relic. Like correcting a thing that wasn't his to correct.

Now it looked childish.

Not the grief. The arrangement.

He hung the frame back on the nail.

Then he put two fingers to either side and adjusted it until the bottom edge sat level with the top of the window trim.

He stepped back.

The difference was small and absolute.

His father did not become easier to understand because of it. The night had not solved why he wrote the song, why he hid it, why he left Joey a lineage built partly out of truth and partly out of omission. The room was still cramped. Rent was still late. Morning would bring all the same practical humiliations back through the door with it. Pike's clock had not softened. Hunger had not turned into breakfast. None of that had changed.

But the man in the photograph had changed scale.

Not shrine-sized anymore.

Not clean enough for warning.

Not useful enough for excuse.

Just a man who had carried the old songs far enough to add one of his own.

Joey stood in the wreck of the room and looked at him until the window gave up a little more light and the frame stayed level.
