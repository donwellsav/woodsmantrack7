Veritas at the Cart

Three nights after the hallway, Joey brought the cash envelope with him because there was only so long a man could leave five hundred dollars' worth of maybe in a kitchen drawer before it started acting like a second landlord.

He had not told himself I am going to pay Duke tonight. He had told himself I am taking the envelope out of the apartment. He had told himself I am sick of opening that drawer just to make sure the money still exists. He had told himself I am tired of the silver guitar seeing me do it.

That last part was truer than he liked.

The silver guitar stayed under the bed where he had shoved it like contraband from a cleaner life. But the apartment was too small for anything to stay fully hidden. When he bent to the kitchen drawer, he caught the pale flash of its tuning pegs from the bedroom doorway. It looked patient. That was enough to irritate him.

The envelope rode flat inside his shirt now, warm and dampening at the edges from his chest. He had left the wooden guitar at the apartment, leaning by the chair where the old glue ridge would still catch his thumb if he reached for it. Left the silver one under the bed where it belonged. Tonight he was carrying paper, not music. Paper and appetite and the whole ugly week since Cass, in that bleach hall, had laid Duke's room out in plain terms.

That week had sanded him raw and done nothing useful with it.

He had busked enough to stay in motion and not enough to feel better. He had eaten badly, slept worse, and looked at the notebook often enough to make the notebook seem harassed. Clarence's record sleeve still slouched by the turntable with side B waiting in it like a question written in pencil. Every object in the apartment had picked up a tone with him. The fork in the sink. The half loaf sweating in its plastic on the counter. Pike's notice on the table with three days left before filing, its red language gone soft at the folds. Cass's sentence kept coming back at bad moments. Real things still happen in crooked places. Duke's bourbon from Thursday kept coming back too, the glass arriving like a favor, the Woodsman name laid on him too gently. Joey had spent several days learning that clarity did not make anger smaller. It just stripped off its costumes.

The Landmark sat half lit and not yet holy. Before doors, it always looked less like a room of destiny than a business caught dressing itself. A couple of smokers leaned by the side wall with their heads bent together, one of them flicking ash into a crushed beer can. Somebody inside dragged a case across the boards. A snare got tightened, loosened, tightened again. A guitar string whined upward toward pitch and missed twice before it found home. The room before customers arrived always told the truth in a rougher voice. No applause yet. No amber forgiveness. Only labor.

Traffic kept working Moreland in both directions. A MARTA bus sighed at the stop, let off three people who all looked like they had been argued with by the day, and pulled out again. The city smelled like fryer oil, hot concrete, cigarettes, damp brick, and the sweet metallic edge of rain that still might not come. August had begun to think about ending but had not signed anything.

He told himself he had come early because if he was going to do something stupid, he preferred time enough to inspect it first.

The cart stood at the curb where no cart had been the last time he came in early.

Stainless steel body. Small umbrella. One cheap clamp lamp throwing a hard yellow cone across the counter. A flat-top griddle murmuring to itself. A handwritten menu taped crooked beside the mustard bottles, as if the whole arrangement had been held together by duct tape, onions, and repeat customers.

Veritas stood behind it with a paperback open in one hand.

She did not look up right away. That was the first insult.

The second came when she said, still reading, "You were going to order a sandwich and a black coffee. Four dollars."

Joey stopped.

The paper bag already sat on the counter. Beside it, a lidded coffee cup steamed into the damp air. Not theatrical steam. Ordinary steam. The kind that came off diner coffee and gas-station coffee and church-basement coffee and all the other cheap cups that kept bad days moving.

He looked from the bag to her face to the book. Cracked spine. Cheap cover. Her thumb holding the place halfway through like this was only what happened while waiting for the next customer.

"I hate you on sight," he said.

"That's not your order," she said. "That's just the mood."

He opened the bag because hunger had never once respected dramatic timing. Foil-wrapped sandwich. Napkin. Nothing fancy. Exactly the hot cheap thing he always bought when he needed food to travel farther than dignity. The smell hit first: toasted bread, onions, grease, pepper, a little mustard sharpness. It took him back for one flash to standing in Mae's diner with a plate in front of him he had not earned enough to refuse. Not the whole memory. Only the bodily part. Heat. Salt. Relief arriving before gratitude had time to organize itself.

The coffee smelled like coffee, not omen.

He put four one-dollar bills on the counter one at a time.

The bills were damp at the corners from his pocket. One of them had a strip of tape across George Washington's forehead. He remembered, stupidly and clearly, his mother flattening singles on the kitchen table after church fish fries, smoothing them with the side of her hand before sliding them into an empty oatmeal can labeled LIGHT. Money had always looked slightly ashamed in his family once it got below a certain amount.

He would not owe Veritas even the shape of a favor.

She slid the money into the cash box without comment and turned a page.

That, more than if she had grinned or vanished or offered some line about fate, made Joey want to break something. The world around them remained offensively ordinary. Brake lights. Bus air brakes. A couple walking past arguing about parking. Somebody inside the Landmark laughing too hard at a joke that had not earned professional support. If the sky had split open or the cart had burst into blue fire, he could have filed the moment faster. But there was only a paper bag, a paid-for coffee, and the woman who had wrecked his month standing there like a woman at work.

He took the sandwich out, leaned against the side of the cart, and ate two angry bites in silence because his body had declined to join any principle committee that night.

The food was hot enough to hurt. Good enough to make him resent it.

Then he said, "It was you."

Veritas kept her eyes on the page. "What was me."

He laughed once, sharp and humorless. "Don't. Don't do the straight-face thing. The park. My guitar. My father's guitar. You took it while I was passed out on a bench looking like death with less organization. Then your friend shows up looking like somebody's dead-rock-star uncle and runs me through a truth test with gold and silver like he got ordained by a fever dream. That was you. You set that up."

"Yes."

She said it so flatly that for a second the word failed to land. Joey stared at her.

"Yes," he repeated. "That is the whole speech."

"You asked a yes-or-no question."

He pushed off the cart. "You stole from a man with twenty-five dollars, a rent envelope, and one decent guitar left in the world, and you're going to act like we are clarifying terms on a receipt."

"It was theft," Veritas said.

The admission stopped him almost as hard as the first yes.

No defense in the wording. No mythic varnish. No apology either. Only accuracy.

"Right," he said. "Good. Great. Wonderful. Then we agree on the noun."

He realized he was still holding the sandwich and hated that too. He shoved it back into the bag harder than necessary. Grease bloomed through the paper under his thumb. The dark spot pulled up the empty space under the bench so fast it made his stomach dip. The black case should have been there. The faded sticker. The zipper that never sat right. The cheap red edge of Pike's notice sticking from the pocket. He had looked once, then again from another angle, as if grief could be corrected by geometry. For one clean minute in that park his father's guitar had existed only in memory: the tacky glue ridge on the lower bout, the smell of old felt and smoke, the neck settling into his palm like a hand he had known longer than language. Father gone once by dying and then almost gone again by theft.

"What do you want me to say?" she asked.

"Why."

That finally made her look up.

Not guilty. Not amused. Exact.

"It had to be real," she said. "Not a classroom question. Not some clean hypothetical you could answer and feel noble about later. Your guitar. Your rent. Your actual life. Hungry enough to get stupid. Tired enough to sleep in public. Scared enough to think the wrong thing might save you. That's the only kind of test that tells the truth."

Joey felt heat rise through him so fast it almost cleared his head by force.

"You do not get to call that a test just because you liked the results."

"I didn't say I liked them."

"You liked being right."

"No." She turned the book over on the counter, page down now, as if granting the argument both hands. "I liked that the answer meant something."

He could see the park again just from hearing her say it that way. Concrete under one palm. Heat already in the bench slats. The fountain coughing behind him. His hand finding nothing under the bench where the case should have been. Looking once, then again from another angle. Forty cents in his pocket. The red rent notice gone with the case. The old glue ridge on the lower bout of the wooden guitar alive in memory because memory was all he had for one awful minute. The runner in the faded Braves visor stretching like disaster was somebody else's cardio. The city continuing in both directions.

"It meant I am poor," he said.

"It meant more than that."

"It meant I woke up thinking my father's guitar was gone."

"Yes."

"It meant the rent envelope went with it."

"Yes."

"It meant I stood up in that park with forty cents in my pocket and thought maybe the only thing my family had managed to carry forward had finally left me because I was tired enough to lose sight of it for twenty minutes."

"Yes."

He threw a hand out toward the street, the club, the city, all of it. "And this is the part where you tell me that was good for me."

"No," Veritas said. "This is the part where I tell you it was true."

Traffic passed. A motorcycle rattled the block. Somebody inside the Landmark cracked the door, dumped a bucket into the alley drain, and shut it again. The smell of old beer moved through the humid air for a second and thinned. Then a voice on the stage tested one line against the mic. Even through brick Joey could hear the room reject it. Not cruelly. Just without need. The sound came out bright and dead and dropped straight to the floor.

He looked at Veritas and saw, as he had from the first day in the park, that the worst thing about her was not that she came from elsewhere. It was that she kept refusing the comfortable lie for people who badly needed one.

"Truth is not the same as permission," he said.

"No."

"Truth is not the same as you getting to interfere with somebody's actual life and then call it instruction."

"No," she said again. "It isn't."

He waited for her to add something that would let him hate her cleanly. A justification with bad perfume on it. A line about destiny or lessons or the universe having office hours. She gave him none.

Instead she said, "People with money get hypotheticals. People without it get answers."

That landed because it was filthy and near true.

Joey folded his arms. "That's a pretty sentence for robbery."

"It's not pretty." She nodded toward the bag in his hand. "You paid for your food because you wanted the boundary to stay visible. That matters. So did what you chose in the park. Cost is what makes the line show up."

He looked down at the grease already spreading through the paper, his own fingertips printing through it. Cost. Line. Boundary. Cheap food and expensive language. He hated how easily she could make the nearest ordinary thing get up and testify.

"You really think stealing my father's guitar proved I'm a good man."

"I didn't say good."

He looked back up slowly. "Careful."

"I said it told the truth."

"What truth."

"That when the choice got expensive, you knew which thing wasn't for sale."

The sentence hit the exact place anger had been standing guard over all week.

Joey looked away first, toward the Landmark sign buzzing unevenly above the door. One letter was always dimmer than the others. Duke kept promising to fix it with the confidence of a man who understood the sales value of deferred repair. Joey remembered his father once taking the back off a busted kitchen clock and getting it to limp along another eight months with a butter knife and two insults. Men in his family had always preferred maintenance to replacement. Maybe because replacement cost money. Maybe because once you accepted a new thing, you had to admit the old one had become mortal.

"That is not some revelation," he said. "It's my father's guitar."

"Exactly."

"So anybody would choose it."

"No."

The answer came too fast. Joey turned back.

"No?" he said.

"No." Veritas rested one hand on the closed book. "Anybody says anybody until the rent is due and the fridge is empty and something bright enough shows up pretending to solve the rest of the month. Then it gets specific."

The gold guitar flashed through him even now, impossible and obscene in the park light, all glitter and answer. The weight of it in his arms had felt like bad theology made portable. He had sold it, yes. Sold it fast. Sold it because a thing that valuable and not-his felt dangerous anywhere near the apartment. Sold it because rent had teeth and groceries had arithmetic and Pike did not take spiritual testimony. But he had not kept it. Had not called it his.

He said, "And if I had taken the gold."

Veritas did not blink. "Then your father's guitar would be gone and the gold one would be yours and that would be exactly what you deserved."

He actually laughed at that, a single disbelieving bark that made a passerby glance over and keep moving.

"Jesus."

"No."

"That isn't funny."

"I know."

"You would've left me with some gilded prop and no real guitar and called that justice."

"I would've left you with your answer."

He felt the old anger try to break into ten different smaller angers so he would not have to hold the real one.

"My father would've said no too," he said. "To the gold. To all of it. He would've said that's not mine. He would've put the thing back in your hands and told you to get serious."

Veritas said, "I know enough to say it."

Everything in him went still.

Not because of the words themselves. Because of the confidence under them. No flourish. No effect. Just knowledge spoken in the same tone she had used for the coffee price.

A chair scraped loudly inside the Landmark. Then the bass note of somebody checking a cable rolled through the wall and into the sidewalk under Joey's shoes. He could feel it in the ball of his foot, in the paper cup in his hand, in the place below his ribs where the envelope sat. The room was waking up one blunt sound at a time.

"How," he said.

She picked the book back up and found her place with one finger.

He stepped closer to the cart. "No. Don't. How do you know enough to say that? You never met my father. He has been dead six years. You don't get to reach into that sentence like it belongs to you."

Veritas turned a page.

"You're carrying the envelope tonight," she said.

For half a beat he did not understand, and then his hand had already moved on its own, touching the inside of his shirt where the cash lay flat against his ribs.

He hated the reflex almost as much as he hated her seeing it.

"That," he said, "is not an answer."

"No. It isn't."

"So you're not going to tell me."

"Not yet."

He stared at her. "There is no practical difference between not telling and not telling yet."

"There is if time matters."

"To who."

"To you."

He almost walked away then on principle. Should have. Any healthy man with a functioning sense of scale would have taken his sandwich, thrown the coffee in the street, and let the gods audit somebody else.

But Joey had never been healthy in the relevant departments, and his sense of scale had been permanently damaged by music before he learned long division.

So he stayed.

"And what," he said carefully, "does the envelope have to do with my father."

Veritas looked up again. "You know what you would not sell. Good. That part is clear. Now you're deciding what you'll pay for permission."

The Landmark door thudded as somebody came in behind them. Voices spilled out, then cut off. Another guitar string whined toward pitch. Joey heard the player correct by instinct, then overshoot, then come back. Even through the wall he could tell who was listening to the room and who was only listening to the instrument. The room always answered in delays. It had its own opinions about who arrived asking and who arrived taking.

He could feel the envelope against his chest like a hand trying not to press.

"That's not what this is."

"What is it."

He opened his mouth and found that every available answer sounded either childish or incriminating.

To get heard. To get in the room. To get near Randall. To stop losing to the front tables. To matter once in a place that mattered back.

He said none of those.

Veritas waited.

Finally he said, "It's an investment."

That got one of her smallest expressions, not a smile, just the facial equivalent of a pencil mark in the margin.

"In what."

He looked toward the club again. The sign buzzed. The dim letter flickered once and held. A soundcheck laugh leaked through the wall, then the dull thump of a kick drum. Joey could almost see the half-built room from outside: the empty front tables, the bartender stacking glassware, Duke at his corner table with the ledger open, the stage looking honest for the last few minutes it ever got each night.

"The room."

"The room does not need your money," she said. "Duke does."

That one found him hard because Cass had already handed him the same blade, and hearing it twice made denial look like clerical error.

"I know what Duke is," he said.

"No. You know one of the things he is."

"And you know all of them."

"I know enough."

He wanted to ask enough for what, but that road led nowhere he could spend tonight.

Instead he said, "You think I should walk away."

"I think you should know what you're doing while you do it."

"I already do."

"No." Her eyes moved once, deliberately, from his face to the bag in his hand and back again. "You know you want in. That is different."

He felt anger reach for jokes and come back empty.

"What exactly is your preferred outcome here," he said. "I storm off, reject the crooked room on moral grounds, go home, keep eating heroic amounts of nothing, and become correct in private."

Veritas shrugged. "If that were what you wanted, you would already be walking."

The streetlamp hummed above them. Somewhere down the block, a siren rose and then dropped away.

She was right about the walking, and the fact of it made him want to kick the cart over.

"What do you want from me," he said.

The question came out lower than the rest. Less performative. That annoyed him too.

Veritas's answer did not.

"I want you to stop calling preservation the same thing as truth."

He stood there.

"Keeping what's mine is not a moral failure," he said.

"No."

"But you want it to be incomplete so you can sound superior."

"I don't care about sounding superior."

"That makes one of us."

Veritas let that pass.

"One question," she said. "You know what you refuse to lose. But every time something asks what is yours, you reach back instead of forward."

The fragment gave one mean little pulse in his memory, the same one it always gave when somebody got too near it. Not melody exactly. More a live wire bent into the shape of maybe. He saw, for half a second, the notebook on his kitchen table with three lines crossed out so hard the paper had scarred. Heard himself humming something in the apartment and shutting it down the instant it started sounding like a first thought instead of an old one.

He tightened his grip on the bag.

"You don't get to talk like you know what writing costs."

Something shifted in her gaze then. Not softness. Not pity. Something older and more tired than either.

"I know what it costs you to avoid it."

That was the nearest she had ever come to cruelty for sport, and because it came this near truth it worked.

He looked away before his face could make some humiliating disclosure in public.

Cars kept passing. Somebody laughed at the corner. Grease popped on the cart's flat-top in tiny bursts. The city, as always, refused to suspend operations for revelation. Inside the Landmark somebody tested a chorus line and the room gave it back flattened and cheap. Joey heard it and knew, with the reflex Duke had beaten into him over two Thursdays, that the singer was pushing for feeling instead of letting the room come to it. The place answered by making the whole thing smaller.

When he spoke again, the anger was still there. It had just narrowed enough to hold.

"You could've asked," he said.

Veritas frowned slightly.

"In the park," he said. "You could've asked me. You could've said, Joey, if everything got expensive, what would you keep. You could've used your words like a person."

"No," she said.

"Why not."

"Because you would have answered from the part of you that likes itself."

He let out a breath through his nose.

That one he could not even call unfair.

She opened the book again. Conversation over on her side, apparently. That irritated him enough to nearly restore the earlier confusion, but not quite. The fog had burned off too far for that.

He took another bite of the sandwich because one indignity at a time was the only system he trusted.

The food had cooled enough to taste now. Salt. Grease. Cheap bread. Pepper. Onion. Something almost gentle about the ordinariness of it. He drank the coffee. Black, burnt, exactly right for a night that needed no sweetness added.

"You are impossible," he said.

"Often."

He should have asked what the book was. He should have asked what she was. He should have asked why any force in the universe had decided his life required this much supervision. Instead he heard himself say, "If I go in there tonight, it isn't because you won something."

Veritas turned one more page. "I didn't say it was."

"It's because sometimes the room does hear."

"Yes."

He looked at her sharply. That answer, more than some others, she had given him without argument.

Not blessing. Not permission. Just fact as she understood it.

That mattered. He hated that it mattered.

"And Duke still runs a machine."

"Yes."

"And I'm still angry at you."

"Yes."

"And if you ever take my guitar again, I will commit a felony with religious conviction."

At that she looked up once more.

"I don't doubt it," she said.

Joey swore under his breath, took the bag in one hand and the coffee in the other, and stepped back from the cart.

He did not thank her.

He did not forgive her.

He also did not throw the food away, which felt truer than any dramatic exit available to him.

The Landmark waited across the patch of parking lot and broken pavement, sign buzzing, door opening and closing on other people's ordinary ambitions. Inside was Duke, the ledger, the room, the whole warm crooked apparatus. Inside was the possibility that Cass had not been wrong. That Duke had not been wrong. That Veritas had not been wrong either, which was the most irritating possibility of all.

The room gave up another burst of sound as the door opened for someone going in: a snare crack, two bars of somebody feeling for key, then a voice dropping quickly to speaking level when it heard itself fail. Even unfinished, the place had standards. Even crooked, it still answered music with either body or indifference. Joey hated that about it. Loved it too. There was no clean version left.

The envelope rested against his ribs. The paper bag warmed his fingers with grease. The coffee heat moved into his palm like a small controlled injury.

He crossed toward the door no less angry than before.

Only clearer. And underneath the anger, in the place Veritas had just pried open, the fragment hummed like a question he kept answering with his father's voice instead of his own.
