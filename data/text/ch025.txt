Track Seven

The apartment sounded busy in every direction except the one that meant company.

The fridge hummed. Water knocked through the old pipes in the wall with the sore rhythm of a building being asked to stay useful after it had already made its objections. A MARTA bus sighed at the stop on Moreland, doors folding open and shut like a man too tired to commit to anger. Somebody below him laughed, coughed, laughed again. A car rolled by with bass heavy enough to tick the window glass in its frame. Atlanta had gone from day heat to night heat, which was less relief than a change in who got blamed for the sweat.

Joey stood just inside the door with the wooden guitar still on his shoulder and the Landmark still all over him. Bleach. Old beer. Fryer grease. Duke's office light. Cass's worn-out face. His father's photograph on that wall. None of it had stayed where it belonged. It had followed him home and sat down.

The room answered him with its usual inventory. Loose papers furred across the table, Pike's old red notice folded under the newer white sheet as if trouble had learned to breed paper in his kitchen. Dishes in the sink. The window fan dead still. Under the bed the silver case caught a strip of streetlight and threw it back too cleanly. The blinds were bent in one corner from the summer he had tried to fix them with tape and only made their failure look intentional. The room still looked exactly like the sort of place a gold guitar failed to rescue. Over the stove the microwave blinked 12:00 at a night already half spent, as if stalled time had won the management job.

He did not turn on the lamp. Streetlight came through the blinds in tired gold bars and laid the apartment out in stripes: chair, dish rack, turntable, Clarence's record beside it, the cheap frame above the player holding his father halfway inside a song.

The photograph was crooked.

It was always crooked. Joey had straightened it once in the first weeks after the funeral, when he still believed order might shame grief into behaving. By morning it had listed again. He had left it alone after that. Superstition if he was honest. Laziness if he was mean. Something smaller and uglier if he was both.

Tonight the tilt looked personal.

He shrugged off the strap. The sweat-dark welt it left across his shirt felt like another hand. He leaned the wooden guitar beside the chair and rubbed at the mark, only making the cloth cling colder to his skin. Duke's voice still sat in his ears, patient as sin. Cass's flat tired accuracy. And behind both, worse than either, the sight of his father on Duke's wall among the public faces, as if the man had been walking in and out of the very rooms Joey had spent years trying to keep him separate from.

He looked at the photograph until it quit being decoration and went back to being evidence.

When Joey got angry enough, his mind got historical. That was one of its less charming habits. Give it pain and it started lecturing. He could feel it gearing up now, ready to deliver a whole speech about Southern room circuits and bar saints and old singers chewed up by small rooms, anything that would let him circle the feeling instead of stepping into it.

He said, to stop that before it started, "Don't."

The room didn't.

The frame stayed crooked above the record player, his father inside it with that mid-song gone look Joey had spent half his life mistaking for certainty. Eyes shut. Mouth open. Guitar held at the angle Joey knew from the kitchen and the apartment and the hospital room and every other dumb relic the dead left for the living to misuse.

He crossed the room.

Up close, the frame looked exactly like what it was. Five-dollar dark wood from a dollar store on Ponce. Cardboard back bowed from humidity. Dust thick at the top edge. One corner blanched white where it had hit the floor during the move to this apartment, damage Joey had ignored because poor men learned early which injuries counted.

He lifted it off the nail.

The act felt rude. Too large for the size of it. Not because it weighed much. Because this was the first time in years he had touched the thing as an object instead of a shrine.

He turned it over.

Yellowed tape at the edges. Brown paper split along one fold. Blue ink under the cloudy plastic backing.

His father's handwriting.

Not the broad public signature from checks and flyers, not the brisk working hand he used when he wanted to look like a man keeping appointments. This was the smaller domestic script Joey knew from grocery lists, pill schedules, cassette cases, church-potluck labels, the backs of envelopes. The hand of a man who had spent most of his life shuttling between art and errands and getting full citizenship in neither.

There was a date first.

Then THE LANDMARK.

Then the list.

Joey read it standing because sitting felt too much like agreement.

The first title he knew. The second too. By the third his mouth had gone tight. The same songs. The ones his father had taught him in kitchen light and church-basement fluorescence and living rooms that smelled like fried onions and borrowed furniture. Hymns, standards, the old carried things Joey had built his working faith out of. The songs he played because they were true and because they were inherited and because inheritance had the useful feature of never asking the inheritor for anything more dangerous than fidelity.

Same order too, more or less. His father had sequenced them the way Joey sequenced them. Pace. Hush. Open ground. Lift. The family resemblance sat not just in the repertoire but in the shape of the night. That hurt more than it helped.

"You knew," Joey said.

Not to the room. To the back of the frame.

The words came out low and immediate, like a man reaching for the first hard object after a blow.

"You knew what that place was."

The Landmark was in the handwriting now, not in Duke's mouth. That made it harder to argue with. His father had not been lured there by rumor or duped by flyer copy. He had written the room down himself. Had stood on that stage. Carried those songs through that door. Known, apparently, that rooms like that were not stories men traded in record shops but actual places with sticky floors, bad wiring, and somebody at the door taking names.

He read the list again, slower.

One title tugged at him and let go. Something in the pattern was off, but it was off the way an extra note sounded off through a wall, not enough yet to name. He was still too hot from the first hit to make room for the second.

He flipped the frame back over.

His father's face came back out of the dim with its infuriating calm.

That gone look again. Not absent exactly. Somewhere else on purpose. Joey had spent years admiring it and hating it in equal measure. It made surrender look like authority. It made music look like a door only certain men knew how to walk through without losing their grocery money and disappointing everybody who depended on them.

He set the photograph face-down on the table.

Not gently. Not hard enough to count as violence. Just enough to reverse the direction of the looking.

Clarence's record sat three inches away in its paper sleeve, patient as old evidence. Joey had brought it home weeks ago and let it live beside the turntable like one more honorable accusation. He had listened to it in Clarence's shop, listened again here, recognized the old songs, admired the room around them, and filed the whole record under things his father had left behind. Important. Sad. Manageable.

He did not touch it yet.

Instead he sat on the edge of the bed, elbows on knees, and listened to the apartment try to keep acting like an apartment.

The fridge motor swelled, then settled. A TV in the next unit laughed too hard at something designed to be easy. Someone ran water in the sink next door and the pipes in his own wall answered with a dull tin complaint. Out on Moreland a motorcycle climbed through gears and flattened into distance. Atlanta was never quiet at night. It only reassigned the noise. Day heat became brick heat. Traffic became delivery traffic, then bar traffic, then the short private emergencies of people trying to get home before their lives caught them.

The stale air in the room still held the street in it. Brick dust. Grease. A seam of cigarette smoke he had not invited and would not fully get rid of till morning. The apartment had a way of collecting the city in thimblefuls and never pouring any of it back out.

He leaned against the wall.

The guitar stood two feet away beside the chair. He could have reached the neck without fully unfolding. He didn't. That mattered tonight. The guitar had been his answer to too many things already. He did not want it volunteering before the question finished arriving.

His eyes kept going to the face-down photograph.

Then to the record.

Then away again.

He let his head fall back against the wall and shut his eyes, which was a mistake, because Duke's office came up at once: cracked door, yellow light, Cass saying, Was it ever supposed to be a real competition, Duke answering, People hear their own fantasy in better lighting, and above all the wall of photographs, his father among the room's older ghosts as proof that Joey's clean division had never been anything but personal convenience.

A smell rose out of nowhere and made him open his eyes again. Cold coffee. Not real cold coffee. Memory cold coffee. Duke's paper cup on the desk, brown at the lip. The smell had a wire running straight to the old kitchen table, to mornings when his father tuned the wooden guitar with a mug cooling beside him while the first bus brakes sighed out on the street and Joey sat half awake in school clothes pretending that watching another man make coffee and find G counted as scholarship. His father used to tap the mug with one knuckle while thinking. Once for maybe. Twice for no. Three times if the song, or the bills, or the day had gone beyond language.

The memory left as fast as it came. The pressure of it stayed.

"Standards are enough," Joey said, just to hear how the sentence sounded now.

It sounded practiced.

It sounded like something repeated so often nobody had bothered to test it anymore.

He got up because sitting had started to feel like softening.

At the fridge he opened the door, looked at light on condiments, a heel of bread, two beers, the Tupperware civilization in the back developing its own foreign policy, then shut it again. The microwave blinked 12:00 in judgment or stupidity or both.

Some time passed. Enough for the streetlight to move one slat over. Enough for the neighbor's TV to go dark. Enough for the bus noise to thin.

He came back to the table.

He picked up the photograph again.

This time he read like a man auditing a lie. Each line. Each title. Each blue-ink loop and blunt stop. THE LANDMARK. The old songs. The order.

Third from the bottom.

MORNING IN ATLANTA.

He went so still the room seemed to jar around him.

The title did not belong.

It sat on the set list like something that had walked in from another house. Not showy. Not badly phrased. Just wrong beside the others. The hymns and standards wore their age like public property. Even when his father personalized them, their bones came from somewhere outside the house. This title had an address in it. A time of day. A city. Not Zion. Not Jordan. Not some old river roomy enough to absolve whoever sang across it. Atlanta. Morning. Asphalt dark with night sweat. Fry oil waking in the diner vents. The first bus kneeling at the curb. Street sweepers, church signs, delivery trucks, a sky the color of dishwater before it decided whether to go blue. A title planted in one place and one man's angle on it.

He read it again.

MORNING IN ATLANTA.

Not a standard.

Not a hymn.

Not one of the songs his father had ever set in his lap and said, Here. This one knows the way home.

His father had written a song.

Not a fragment lost under his breath. Not a line abandoned because dinner had to be bought or somebody's lungs had to be paid for. A full song. One he had written down in his own hand. One important enough to slot into the middle of a night built mostly out of inheritance.

And he had never told Joey.

That part arrived a beat later and cut deeper.

He laughed once and stopped because the sound had nothing to do with laughter.

"No," he said.

Not denial. Insult.

"No."

Years of kitchen instruction rearranged themselves in three heartbeats. The old songs first. Standards are enough. Tradition is the point. These songs have already survived more pain than we can improve on. Joey had taken all of that for doctrine because doctrine had been useful. Useful to grief. Useful to fear. Useful to the kind of cowardice that put on filial piety and got handled gently because the father was dead.

His father had crossed the line himself.

Had done the exact thing Joey treated like vanity, betrayal, some unlicensed swerve out of the family lane.

Worse, he had done it and then gone on teaching the safer version.

Joey set the frame down flat and had to pin it with one hand because his fingers had started slipping.

"You couldn't mention that?"

The question came out smaller than he meant it to. Like a son asking about forgotten milk, not about the frame he had built his whole borrowed life inside.

He tried again, uglier.

"One song and you never thought that was family information?"

The room held still around that. Not silent. Still. The fridge hummed. A siren bent itself around a corner a few blocks off. Somewhere in the building a toilet ran too long. Atlanta kept doing what Atlanta did after midnight, but Joey felt the apartment pull tight around his sentence as if the ordinary noise had stepped back to listen.

He grabbed Clarence's record.

The sleeve rasped under his fingers. Worn mouth. Blunted corners. Paper gone soft where too many hands had used the same entrance and exit. Clarence had handled it like something humble and holy at once. One copy to play, one to keep, because some records were made for sound and some for proof. Joey had brought home the proof and never really read it.

He turned it over.

Track list.

Finger down the lines.

One through six: the old standards. The songs he knew. The same carried repertoire that had let him relax the first time he heard the record, because recognition did what recognition always did when it went unchallenged: it closed the case early.

Track Seven: "Morning in Atlanta."

And beside it, in smaller print, the line he had somehow never made himself take in: written by Z. Woodsman.

Eight through ten: back to the standards.

He had missed it.

Not because it had been hidden. That was what made the humiliation so clean. The proof had been sitting in plain sight on the sleeve like a dare too patient to announce itself. Joey had missed it because nine of the ten songs told the story he preferred. Father as carrier. Father as steward. Father as a man too humble or too pure or too reverent to trespass into authorship. Joey had seen the pattern he wanted and quit reading as soon as the pattern started soothing him. People did that. Quit looking the second the story became shelter.

He had been living beside Track Seven for days.

Maybe weeks.

The room turned meaner.

He took the record from the sleeve and went to the turntable.

Clarence's player was better. Joey's was honest about its flaws. The right speaker had a paper buzz at certain notes. The belt sometimes needed persuading. One foot sat on a folded matchbook because otherwise the whole thing leaned left and mistracked the way bad rooms did when nobody had the money to level them. His father had repaired it once, years ago, kneeling on the floor with a butter knife and cursing gently enough for a child. Joey could still see the small wedge of concentration between the man's eyebrows, smell solder and dust, hear him say, Hands know before pride does, then laugh because even he could hear how sermonish that sounded.

Joey set the vinyl down.

The platter started slow, caught, then turned true.

Needle to Track Seven.

A breath of static.

Then his father's voice.

Same voice and not.

That was the first blow. No disguise. No secret side career in another genre. No late evidence that Zeke Woodsman had once wanted to be flashy and failed his way back into piety. The family resemblance was immediate. Same wooden pulse under the guitar. Same plain voice that sounded, at first pass, as if it had no tricks because all the tricks were being spent on honesty. Same small-room acoustics. Same breath at the ends of lines. Same habit of letting a consonant arrive half a beat late when feeling wanted more time than the measure allowed.

And from the first phrase Joey knew this was different from the rest of the record in a way he could not explain away.

It was closer to the skin.

That was one difference. The standards had history standing between them and the singer. Good history. Working history. Generations of mouths and hands and weather had worn them into common use. Even when grief moved through them, grief had company. This song didn't. It had no centuries behind it. No communal varnish rubbed over the sharp places. It sounded like one man's actual morning pushed into air still warm from sleep and grease and worry. Something private made audible before time had a chance to sand it down.

Across the room the wooden guitar answered on two notes, a faint sympathetic shimmer from the body leaned beside the chair. Joey turned at the sound and then back to the speaker, pulse jumping stupidly. Physics, not haunting. He knew that. Still, the small ring went straight through him. The instrument recognized the house it came from before he could stop it.

The apartment changed around the song.

Not magically. Worse. Materially. The paper buzz in the right speaker rode the high notes like an old scar opening. The fridge motor found the key and became accompaniment against its will. When a bus sighed at the curb below, the long pneumatic exhale folded under the measure as if the city had been there on the original morning and had only been waiting for the right title to admit it. The blinds quivered once in their frame. Streetlight on the sink shifted as if even bad apartment light listened differently when somebody in the room finally risked a specific thing.

Joey stood with one hand on the turntable stand and felt the title working through him from the inside out.

Morning in Atlanta.

He could hear the place in it without needing every word. He did not need lyrics. He knew the difference between a song set nowhere and a song that had taken one city's weather into its lungs. There was coffee in the shape of the phrases. Kitchen grease. Bus brakes. The thin mean optimism of dawn before the bills woke up. That Atlanta mixture of labor and humidity, holiness and commerce, weariness and hustle pressed up against one another before eight a.m. It sounded like the old songs had stepped out onto an actual street and had to admit where they were standing.

It was not polished enough to flatter anybody.

That was what broke him.

If the track had been perfect, capital-P perfect, Joey could have hidden in awe. Could have turned the whole thing into another shrine. Fine, he could have thought. My father had a secret genius. One more mile of distance. One more reason I am only the son.

But the song was not shrine material.

It was rough in the right places. Personal in a way that left splinters. The melody leaned toward the old bones and then cut where the feeling required, not where tradition had already worn the groove smooth. The voice stood a little nearer its own exposed nerve than it did on the standards. It did not sound like a man trying to beat history. It sounded like a man adding his weight to it and knowing the addition cost more than repetition.

That was the other thing the room answered to. Poor as the apartment was, it could not stay generic around something that specific. The song made the place local. Made the cracked paint, hot window glass, and sink full of dishes feel less like shame than raw material. Even the air changed species. Before the song it had been stale. During it, it was used.

Joey's hands shook.

Not fear exactly. Not cold. Recognition was the cleaner word, though nothing about it felt clean in the body. Recognition with grievance in it. Recognition with teeth.

His father did it.

The thing Veritas had heard before Joey would admit it. The thing Mercury had mocked him for not doing. The thing Joey had spent half his life treating like ego, treason, some door only dishonorable men tried. His father had crossed that line first. Not outside the tradition. Inside it. Not by throwing the old songs away. By carrying them so far into his own life that one morning there had been nowhere left to put the next feeling except into a song no one else had already blessed.

And then he had put it on a record that barely sold and never told his son.

Permission arrived braided with betrayal.

Joey hated the permission almost immediately for how useful it was.

Because if Zeke Woodsman had written one original song, the neat church Joey had built in his head was gone. No faithful-son excuse left. No clean father he was merely preserving against the vulgarity of invention. There was only lineage, and lineage, it turned out, included risk. Which meant Joey had not been protecting the family line by refusing to write. He had been hiding behind an edited version of it.

The anger came braided too.

At his father first.

Then at himself for needing the dead to stay simple in order to keep his own cowardice respectable.

The kitchen table came back in a burst. His father saying the old songs were enough for people who needed them. His thumb tapping the mug. The crack in the linoleum by the stove. Joey, maybe thirteen, trying a line of his own under his breath and killing it the second Zeke looked up. Had his father heard? Had he known and chosen silence? Had he mistaken silence for guidance? Had he thought omission was mercy?

Why didn't you tell me.

The question hit so hard it almost came out loud.

What came out was, "What, one was enough trouble for a lifetime?"

His own voice sounded harsh in the room, older and smaller than he meant it to.

He said it again, quieter.

"Or was I supposed to find it like this."

The song kept going.

Joey listened the way men listened when verdict and inheritance came in the same envelope: still in the body because stillness was the only control left. The track moved through him without handing him a clean moral. His father did not sound noble. He did not sound corrupted. He sounded human, which was much more inconvenient. Human enough to risk. Human enough to keep a secret. Human enough to make a thing that was not perfect and not borrowed and not protected by age, then live beside it as if it had not revised every sentence he later handed his son about what counted.

That last part hurt worst.

Not because it proved malice. Joey did not think the dead were that organized. It hurt because it proved complexity, and complexity was a rotten gift to receive after burial. Saints were useless but stable. Humans kept moving after you thought you had them arranged.

The track neared its end.

Joey felt that before he heard it, the way some songs announced their last measure in the muscles. His throat tightened. His hand on the stand tightened too, hard enough to make the cheap veneer click.

He had spent years treating his father as proof that preservation was the purest form of love.

Now the song was telling him something worse and better: preservation had never been the whole job. Not even for the man who had taught him to think it was.

The last chord resolved.

For half a breath the room held the aftersound.

Then the needle found the runout and began its small circular hiss.

Joey did not move.

Before the song, the apartment had been poor in plain readable ways. Thin walls. Bad air. Bent blinds. Fridge hum loud enough to count as company. The kind of room where a man could explain every object by money and every silence by fatigue.

After it, the room was crowded. The photograph face-down on the table. Blue ink on cheap backing paper. Clarence's record turning under the needle. The city named inside a song his father had kept to himself.

He looked at the blank patch of wall above the turntable.

Then at his hands.

The hands were the worst part. They looked the same. Same fingers. Same calluses. Same old nicks at the cuticles. But the story those hands had been allowed to tell about themselves was gone.

His father had crossed the line first. There was no saint left to hide behind.

The hiss went on, small and dry and stubborn, the sound a machine made when it had run out of song and not yet been told to stop.

He let it keep circling.
