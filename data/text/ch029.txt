Wings

Before Joey saw the wings, he heard them.

Not the room out front. That room announced itself too easy. Ice in glasses. A laugh too loud on the way out, then cut shorter when its owner heard it. Silverware ticking a plate. A flare of feedback while somebody onstage checked a cable and apologized to nobody. The wings had smaller sounds, uglier ones. The hot insect whine of a work light. A bottle cap spinning under a folding chair until it found the wall. A case latch catching, slipping, catching again. Somebody on the other side of the curtain taking one steadying breath and then another, because talent had never once exempted a body from being a body.

He stood in the narrow backstage pocket with the wooden guitar strapped on, the leather settled into the same old notch on his shoulder. One folding chair. One milk crate. Black curtain brushing his arm, cinderblock cool through his shirt between the shoulder blades. A coil of cable against his boot. The air held dust, old wiring, stale beer, fryer grease from the kitchen, and that damp mineral smell Atlanta got after rain had only slapped the heat flatter instead of killing it. Every time somebody came through the hall from the front, the city came in with them for half a second: wet denim, cigarettes, rain-dark brick, exhaust off Moreland, pomade woken back up by humidity.

Showcase night, Joey thought, did not smell important.

It smelled overused.

That helped.

He had been in the Landmark long enough to know which parts Duke sold as destiny and which parts were just labor under better bulbs. Out front Duke would be moving table to table with that church-usher smile, turning months of buy-ins and small humiliations into a cleaner public story. Back here the room admitted what it was. Heat. Cables. Waiting. Men in black T-shirts pretending not to be nervous. Women with stage makeup on and nowhere useful to put their hands.

He flexed his left hand once.

Then again.

The wooden guitar answered with the dull familiar weight he trusted more than comfort. The neck sat in his palm the way it had sat in his father's and, before that, in hands Joey only knew by story and habit. The finish had gone smooth where use had worn it smooth. Honest shine. His father used to say that about old wood and work boots and cast-iron skillets, anything that had quit trying to look new and settled for being useful.

The silver guitar was still in the apartment.

He could feel its absence as clearly as the wood against his ribs. Clean thing. Shiny thing. Easy thing. He had left it leaning by the wall on purpose, because tonight he could not stand one more object in the room that sounded better from ten feet away than in a man's hands. The song he had now would either stand in old wood or not stand at all.

From beyond the curtain came Duke's voice, warm and bright and busy making strangers feel they had done the city a favor by buying two drinks and coming early.

Joey did not listen to most of the words. He knew the job they were doing. Thanks for being here. Thank you to our people. Great room tonight. Great energy. Great city. Every so often the public charm broke just enough for the other Duke underneath it: tell a server to hold an aisle, ask for the stage wash down a hair, keep Randall's table fed and facing forward. Duke could take money already spent and hand it back to the spenders as belonging. He could also keep a room pointed where he wanted it pointed. That was part of his gift. Part of the danger too.

Joey let the sound wash by without entering him.

That, at least, had changed.

Three weeks ago he would have been hungry for cues, for proof the room had not already decided his size before he stepped into it. Tonight he was listening for something meaner and more private. The exact shape of his own fear. Where it lived. Whether it had moved out of his throat and into his hands. Whether hearing and winning were going to split in his body the way Cass said they could.

The answer was not flattering.

His mouth had gone dry. His shoulders were too high. Every few seconds his mind tried to jump ahead and bargain with later. What if Randall didn't hear it. What if Duke cut him off clean and smiling. What if the room heard the song and then handed the official thing somewhere safer anyway. What if the applause and the result split so hard he could feel the seam in his teeth on the train home. What if he gave them the real thing and still woke tomorrow to the same late-rent math and Pike's filed silence sitting on the far side of morning. The edge of the folded page kept brushing his chest when he breathed too high, reminding him the thing had been made by hand before it ever asked the room for anything.

He shut that down as best he could.

Future was greedy. It always wanted evidence before the work was done.

He ran his thumb once over the worn edge of the pickguard and got a fast memory in return: 4:52 a.m., notebook open, torn pages at his feet, the apartment hotter than honesty deserved, the photograph turned facedown and then back up, his father's one original song no longer theory but fact. The memory did not bloom. It only struck and was gone. Enough to remind him that the hardest part had already happened in a room much smaller and uglier than this one. He had said the thing to himself first. The rest was weather.

The curtain shifted.

He turned.

Cass was there with his old case over one shoulder.

He knew the case before he fully knew her in the half-light. Stickers worn down to ghost language. Corners gone soft. The left latch still a little wrong. He had seen it over her shoulder on the block an hour earlier, but the half-glimpse had not prepared him for the thing itself standing here between them. The sight of it hit so clean he had to look at the handle, then the latch, then the handle again before he could get his eyes up to her face.

The case opened three memories at once.

First Thursday, standing in this same building with ten dollars less in his pocket and his case tucked hard to his leg as if the handle itself might keep him from looking as scared as he was. The pawn-shop counter, fluorescent light turning the world flat while he laid miracle down and let arithmetic take it. The hallway outside his apartment, hauling the case up one flight at a time because the stairwell smelled like wet cardboard and old cooking grease and he had not yet learned how tired a man could get and still keep choosing music.

By the time he got to Cass, his chest hurt a little.

She had changed for stage without turning unreal. Black jeans, black shirt, boots. Makeup sharp enough to survive the lights without shouting from the wings. But the room version of her had not fully locked in yet. He could still see the day in her. Humidity curling a small piece of hair near one ear. Exhaustion held low in the face, where discipline had to work around it. A dusting of gold highlighter at her cheekbone because the room liked a little proof that labor could still look expensive.

"You look worse," she said.

The line was so dry it almost steadied him on contact.

"That seems hard to measure in a room like this."

"It's not hard," she said. "It's visual."

"Glad we're starting with tenderness."

A small movement crossed her mouth and quit before it became a full smile. That was how tired she was. Not too tired to joke. Too tired to waste the extra energy it took to finish one.

She slid the old case off her shoulder and set it on the floor between them with more care than the cinderblock deserved.

Joey looked down at it.

"You went back," he said.

"Yeah."

"Why?"

Cass glanced toward the curtain first, as if to make sure the room stayed where it belonged for another second, then looked back at him.

"Because I couldn't stand that being the last place it made sense."

She said it without flourish. That helped. The truth in a low voice landed harder than a speech.

Joey nodded once, slow.

He knew better than to rush toward gratitude. Gratitude would have treated the scene like it was finished before the object had finished changing shape. The case on the floor was still becoming something else under his eyes.

Cass stayed where she was, one shoulder against the wall now, arms folded not in defense but in containment. Holding herself together while the room tugged from the other side of the curtain.

"I want you to know something before I go out there," she said.

Joey waited.

"If the music had been enough by itself, I wouldn't have needed Duke."

The sentence came out flat and exact. Not defensive. That made the subtext impossible to miss. She was not asking for pardon. She was asking not to be misread one last time.

"My music is good," she said. "That isn't ego. It's inventory. The songs are good. The set is good. I can hold a room through bad monitors and half-drunk tables and still stand at the merch corner after and act like I have another hour in me. But good isn't always enough in this room. Not in this business. Not in this city. Sometimes good still needs a door."

She listened to Duke beyond the curtain for half a beat, as if checking a measurement against the original thing.

"Duke is a door," she said. "An expensive, complicated, morally questionable door. He can keep the right table in its seat through your second verse. He can make a scout look up when he'd rather check his drink. Still a door."

Joey looked at her and heard what sat underneath it. Not just justification. Fatigue. Class math. Years of learning the difference between talent and access the long way and being expected to act like that difference did not wound.

"I'd have played it straight," he said. "I don't know if that makes me a better person or just too broke to be corrupt."

That got the full smile, brief and unwilling.

"Both," she said. "Definitely both."

"Yeah," Joey said. "Probably both."

The work light buzzed overhead. Beyond the curtain somebody at the front tables laughed too hard, then stopped when they heard themselves. Joey looked at the old case again and, because the honest sentence was there and he had finally gotten too tired to decorate honest things once he found them, he said, "Your music's real."

Cass's face did not soften. It opened a fraction.

He kept going.

"Duke is still Duke. The arrangement is still the arrangement. The buy-in is ugly. Any door that makes you crawl a little is ugly. But the songs aren't fake because the room is crooked."

She held his eyes.

"Mine too?"

There it was. The real question, stripped clean. Not general reassurance. Not artist chatter in the half-dark. She meant the whole thing. The careful set construction. The management. The bargain. The ambition. The way the room had turned parts of her into product and the way she had cooperated because bills still came and careers still had hinges and hunger had never once respected purity.

"Yours too," Joey said.

The answer sat between them with a little weight on it. Not tender. Not flirtation. Recognition, which was rarer and cost more.

Cass dropped her eyes first, but not out of shyness. To the case. To the practical thing on the floor that had brought them to the sentence.

"Open it," she said.

He bent and worked the left latch.

It stuck exactly where it used to. His thumb found the angle on instinct. Second try. The smell came up as soon as the lid gave: old lining, trapped summer, a little dust, dry wood, one faint sour thread of spilled beer from some night he had wiped at instead of properly cleaning. Bus rides, stairwells, Thursdays. Work done without witnesses.

Inside, under the work light, lay the gold guitar.

He had thought seeing it again would feel like temptation. Or shame. Or one more test he was already too tired to pass. Instead the first feeling that rose in him was arithmetic. The pawn-shop counter. Twelve hundred in dirty bills. His own hand pushing Duke's buy-in money across the table. Cost, not glamour. Price made visible. Not the answer. The receipt.

He touched the edge of the case lining with two fingers and stopped there.

"It doesn't fix anything," Cass said.

"I know."

"I'm not handing it to you like a cure."

"I know that too."

"It also cost me my float," she said. "Rent cushion. Grocery cushion. The little strip between a week and a problem. I'm not asking you to make that noble."

"Good."

He looked up at her and saw how much effort it cost her not to let the room's favorite words through the curtain.

"Why bring it, then?" he asked, though he already knew most of the answer.

She blew air out through her nose.

"Because it sounds truer than what I was going to use."

He waited.

"And because after soundcheck I couldn't walk out there and help Duke make the wrong version of tonight look official."

That one landed deeper because she had not dressed it up.

From the front, Duke's voice lifted over the room again, easy as if he had invented ease. Joey caught a few phrases through the curtain. Community. Special night. Real music. Then the mic dipped and Duke's real working voice cut through for a second, telling somebody to move the bucket and keep the side path clear. The language made Joey want to laugh and spit at the same time.

"Did he hate it?" Joey asked.

"The phone call?"

"The concept."

Cass gave him a look that almost qualified as warm.

"He did not love my tone."

"That sounds like Duke."

"He prefers to be the one who tells a room what changed."

"Yeah," Joey said. "That tracks."

She moved closer to the case then, not intimate, just near enough that the tightness of the space registered honestly. Powder, sweat, damp fabric, old beer soaked into the wall, hot bulbs overhead. No glamour back here. Two musicians and the practical junk around them, waiting to be fed to the room.

"Listen to me," she said.

His humor went away on command.

"The room may hear you tonight," she said. "I think it probably will. That is not the same as the official result, and it's not the same as Duke letting the paper version of the night out of his hands."

The knot under Joey's breastbone loosened a little, not because the warning made him safe, but because it named the danger right. He had spent too much of this whole stretch getting lied to by kinder versions of things.

"I know," he said.

Cass shook her head once. "No. Know it in your body. If the room hears you and Duke still hands the official thing somewhere else, don't lie backward about it afterward."

That shut him up. The warning landed because it was the right one, and because he could hear how much it had cost her to learn it.

"Winning would matter," he said.

Cass's mouth tightened. Not disagreement. Recognition.

"Rent still rent," he said. "I'm not stupid enough to pretend otherwise. But it stopped being the whole category after that night in the apartment. After I quit hiding behind my father. Whatever happens out there, I know what the song is."

No joke. No historical side road. When Joey got honest the language always got plainer. He could feel it happening and let it.

Cass studied him the way musicians studied each other when one of them said something about a note that could either be true or pure self-mythology.

"Good," she said.

It did not sound like comfort. It sounded like a musician checking whether a string would hold.

He said, "You sound like you've had to tell yourself that before."

A short breath left her.

"More than once."

That was all. Enough.

"Your music's real," he said again, quieter this time.

Cass gave him the driest half-smile in Atlanta.

"That is dangerous information to hand me before a set."

"You already knew it."

"Knowing it and hearing somebody else say it are not the same thing."

That one he understood immediately and without any defensive furniture.

"Yeah," he said. "Found that out recently."

The silence held. Two people seeing the damage and the talent in each other at the same time and refusing to flatten either one.

Joey noticed her hands then. Callus at the fingers. A torn bit of skin by one thumb she had probably worried loose earlier and then ignored. Rings off for the set. Powder on the back of one knuckle. Those hands steadied him more than any encouraging speech could have. Labor first. Everything else later.

"How's the song holding up?" she asked.

"Depends which hour you ask."

"I'm asking this one."

He shifted the guitar strap though it did not need shifting.

"It's there," he said. "Which is not the same as easy."

"No," Cass said. She nodded toward the case. "Nothing real gets easier in this room. It just gets louder."

That sat with him.

A voice at the curtain called five minutes. Then a different voice corrected it to two. Backstage math. Joey almost laughed on reflex and then didn't. Duke would be out front building her entrance now, setting the room up to receive the version of the night he preferred. But the room on the other side of the fabric had a sound Joey had heard on enough Thursdays to know. Anticipation tightening. Tables settling. People turning toward the stage before they meant to admit they cared.

Cass bent and lifted the gold guitar partway out of the case, checking the strap pin, the tuning, the cable. Work, not symbolism. Joey watched the movement and got another quick burst of memory: the pawn-shop owner laying the instrument down on glass, the way fluorescent light had flattened everything except the guitar itself, the mean little miracle of Duke's ledger swallowing the cash that had come from it. He had thought selling it was the point of no return. Maybe it had only been the price of arriving here clear-eyed. The silver guitar would have looked cleaner under the work light. Good thing it was home.

"You using that as a symbol?" he asked.

Cass looked up sharply.

"Please don't say symbol back here."

"Fair."

"I'm using it because it sounds truer than what I was using." She glanced toward the curtain. "And because I am tired of helping this room sand every edge off a thing until it behaves."

Joey nodded.

There was nothing to improve in that sentence.

The crowd beyond the curtain shifted again, but differently this time. A real hush, not yet silence. Duke saying Cass's name with that full warm flourish he saved for acts he could market cleanly. Joey did not need to see the room to know heads would already be coming up, drinks paused halfway, bodies angling toward the stage because they trusted her or wanted to or had been trained to.

Cass closed the case lid, then opened it again and gave the left latch one more firm push. Second try. Like always. The sound of it clicking home went straight into Joey's sternum.

Memory attached itself to stupid practical details. That latch had its own history. He had checked it outside Duke's office the first Thursday as if a secure case might equal a secure self. He had checked it in Harmony Park with rent money under the blanket. He had checked it on the apartment floor after coming home too tired to do anything right. Now Cass's thumb found the same stubborn angle.

She straightened with the case handle in one hand.

"And afterward," she said, "don't joke it smaller."

Joey nodded.

Enough.

A stagehand in a black T-shirt stuck his head through the gap.

"Now," he said.

Cass lifted the case strap over her shoulder. The work light caught the worn edge of the handle, the metal on the latches, the line of her jaw. Joey saw the whole route of it at once. Pawn-shop counter. Wet sidewalk. Phone pressed to her ear while she told Duke the night had changed. Backstage cinderblock. Curtain. Stage. No clean redemption anywhere in the chain. Just a musician deciding one object would not stay where the room had left it.

At the edge of the wings she stopped and looked back at him.

Not tender. Not soft. Certain.

"Then let it," she said.

He nodded.

She turned.

As soon as the curtain opened enough to take her, the room changed pitch on the other side of it. Joey heard the intake before the applause. Heard recognition move through bodies before a note had been played. The gold guitar had not even touched light yet, and already the crowd was leaning toward the shape of a story Duke thought he knew how to sell.

Cass stepped into it anyway.

Then she passed through the slit in the curtain with his old case over her shoulder, and the room took hold of her first.
