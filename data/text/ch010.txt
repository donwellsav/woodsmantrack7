Eggs and Goldenrod

He heard the spatula before he pushed through the diner door.

Metal scraping the flat-top. Then the off-time clink of mugs meeting badly behind the counter. Ceiling fans chopping the heat into smaller heat. A MARTA bus sighing at the curb on Moreland. Somebody laughing himself into a smoker's cough. Somebody else asking again for more toast in the tone of a man who believed repetition ought to count as rank. Morning in Little Five, or near enough. The city was already up and mean about it.

Joey stood outside a second longer than he had any reason to, one hand on the glass, and tried on the feeling of a man who had slept.

It didn't fit.

The apartment had given him maybe an hour total, broken into ugly ten-minute payments. The gold and silver guitars had spent the night propped by the record player because there was nowhere else to put them, and because shoving them under the bed had felt disrespectful in a way he could not yet explain. The silver one caught every passing headlight and laid a hard white stripe across the room: over the milk-crate records, over the sink stacked with dishes, once right across his father's crooked photograph so the old man looked briefly crosshatched and expensive. The gold one worked the other side of the street. Joey had thrown his red flannel over the case, but the thing still announced itself under the cloth. When he was eleven and the light bill was due, his mother used to slip whatever cash she had scraped together under a folded church bulletin until morning, as if Jesus might help it keep quiet. It never had. Covered money still changed a room. The guitar did too.

He had spent most of the night in the chair watching those two impossible cases change his apartment just by leaning in it. Beside him, the wooden guitar looked smaller, more scarred, and somehow steadier for the comparison. The fan was still dead. The fridge was still mean. Pike's notice stayed spread on the table where Joey had opened it again after peeling the recovered bills off it and slipping the money back into the thin envelope from his case. The room now contained enough theoretical value to save him and still looked exactly like the sort of place a landlord would take back without pausing between bites of lunch.

By dawn he had quit pretending sleep was coming and come here instead.

Mae's diner was what it had always been and would probably keep being until the block learned a more profitable way to lie about itself. Vinyl booths patched with gray duct tape gone glossy with age. Counter worn satin-smooth by ten thousand forearms. A floor that never quite forgot grease no matter how often it got mopped. The coffee lived on the burner long enough to form opinions. The place smelled like fryer oil, old toast, bleach, and the kind of labor that started before daylight and would still be on its feet when the bars closed.

He liked the booth by the window because he could watch the street from it while the street mostly forgot to watch back. He slid into it now with the stiffness of a man whose shoulders still remembered carrying three guitar cases up a narrow stairwell and across a room too small for any of them.

Mae was moving before he got all the way down.

Mid-fifties, maybe. Eleven years behind this counter and a whole unadvertised life before that. Hair pinned up. Pencil behind one ear. Dish towel over one shoulder like she might at any minute need to settle a dispute with steam, grease, or foolishness. Her voice was the downbeat of the room. When she called for bacon or a refill or table three's check, the place shifted around it without resentment. Regulars turned. Plates moved. The diner kept time with her whether it admitted it or not.

She set a mug of coffee in front of him without asking if he wanted one. Her eyes did a fast inventory while she did it: yesterday's shirt, beard shadow, the set in his shoulders, the hollowed-out corners of his mouth that said he was trying to run a body on intentions.

"You eat yet?"

Joey curled both hands around the mug for the heat before he drank. The ceramic was thick and chipped at the lip. It pulled loose a memory of his father after late gigs, sitting over diner coffee with both hands around the cup before he even looked at the menu, thawing his fingers one joint at a time. Coffee don't wake you up, he'd said once, flexing his left hand. It just talks your hands into forgiving you. Joey took a sip now and felt the first honest warmth of the morning move into him.

"Define eat."

"Food in your mouth. You chew it. You swallow it. That count?"

"Then no."

Mae planted one hand on the table edge and leaned in just enough to make the next sentence official.

"When is the last time you ate a meal. Real one. Not a granola bar. Not some sad handful out a bag. A meal."

Joey looked into the coffee as if the answer might be floating there. "I had eggs yesterday."

"No you did not." She did not even have to think. "You had eggs three days ago. I was here. I set them down. You think I don't notice who hasn't eaten?"

"Mae."

"I'm bringing you eggs."

"I can't pay for-"

She cut him off with one flat look. "Boy, I know you can't pay. That's why I ain't asking. I'm bringing you eggs and you're gonna eat them and shut up about it. When you got money again, and you will because you're broke, not stupid, you're gonna tip me real nice. We clear?"

The words yes ma'am made it halfway up Joey's throat and died there because Mae would only have thrown them back at him sharpened.

"We clear," he said.

"Good."

She was already moving away before the last consonant left him.

The coffee hit his empty stomach like an argument. Not bad. Necessary. Joey sat with the mug between his palms and let the room bring him back by force.

Two construction guys in safety yellow were halving a biscuit at the counter with the gravity of diplomats drawing a border. Sweat had already darkened the cloth between their shoulder blades. The woman in scrubs from the park came in wearing the hospital on her face and ordered toast to go without quite focusing her eyes on Mae. An old man in a Braves cap worried at the crossword with a pencil worn down to civic disappointment. Somebody in the kitchen cursed the grill with affection. Somebody else laughed. Out on Euclid a delivery truck backed up with the long mechanical crying of reverse gear. The door opened, poured in hot pavement and stale cigarette smoke, then shut again.

Nothing in the room suggested gods had passed through Harmony Park the night before.

That helped.

Then it didn't.

He sat there with coffee in his hands and those two impossible guitars back at the apartment, and his mind did what it always did when money got involved: stopped asking what a thing meant and started asking what it could do. Sold. Split. Hidden. Carried. Stretched to Friday. Poverty had run that scale into him till he could play it blind.

He only hated that reflex on mornings when he had the spare calories.

Mae brought the plate down in front of him with enough force to announce breakfast without making it look like anger.

Two eggs, edges crisped in butter, yolks high and bright. Toast sweating a little into the plate. A little cup of jelly that always made him feel seven and broke in equal measure.

"Eat before it gets cold."

Joey looked at the plate and had the humiliating physical thought that he might love her a little.

The first bite undid him more efficiently than Mercury had.

Hot yolk, salt, grease, toast. His body recognized rescue before his pride could object. He made himself slow down after the first three bites because wolfing food in public had its own small humiliation attached to it, and he had already met quota for the week. Even so, the eggs disappeared toward the part of him that had been making all the executive decisions since yesterday.

Mae passed once with the coffee pot and saw the speed of the damage.

"Mm-hm," she said, not slowing. "That's what I thought."

Joey kept eating.

Nobody in the diner looked at him for more than a second at a time. That was part of Mae's mercy too. Feed a man, yes. Audit his hunger, no. The construction guys argued about the Falcons. The nurse checked her phone and frowned at it like she was considering a career change on principle. Somebody asked Mae if she had any decent hot sauce left and she told him he could use what was in front of him or go home and invent his own. Laughter moved through the room and settled back down. The place had its own rhythm, and it kept it whether Joey's life was making sense or not.

By the time he looked up again, a little of the world had come back into focus. Not enough to make it kind. Enough to make it readable.

That was when he saw the bulletin board by the door.

Dense with paper. Flyers layered over flyers, old staples, torn corners, phone-number tabs ripped away until only ragged paper teeth remained. Guitar lessons. Dog walker needed. Room for rent. Three different bands promising one-night-only and meaning something looser than that. A geological record of neighborhood hopes, half of them already dead and still hanging there out of habit.

And in the middle, a little crooked, on goldenrod paper that caught the eye by being exactly as loud as it needed to be:

HARMONY SHOWCASE / THE LANDMARK

The yolk drying on his plate was nearly the same color as the paper on that board. One of them was breakfast. The other was trouble wearing a heading.

The rest of the flyer gathered itself beneath the title in the lean, efficient language of opportunity with a cover charge. One night only. Label scout in attendance. Limited performer slots. Buy-in required. One slot remaining.

Joey stopped chewing.

The diner sounds thinned around the paper without actually going away. Spatula. Fans. Cups. Mae calling for a refill. The kitchen door banging once with a bus tub against it. Everything still there, just falling back the way a room sometimes fell back when somebody's hand had lifted over the strings and everybody felt, before the first note, that something was about to declare itself.

He stood with the plate half-finished and went to the board.

The goldenrod paper was cheap. You could tell by the texture. Slightly furry at the cut edge where the blade had gone dull. One corner already softening in the heat. Black letters bled a little into the grain. It had the same tooth as the handbills his father used to bring home folded in a shirt pocket after gigs, corners damp from sweat, names of rooms stamped on them big enough to matter for one night and then not at all. None of that made this one less dangerous. The thing still held the bright, ugly voltage of a door somebody wanted noticed.

The Landmark.

Eight blocks away.

A room he had passed and not entered. A name he knew by residue: neighborhood talk, a couple of offhand references from his father that had never added up to a usable story, one old Landmark handbill that had lived for weeks in the kitchen junk drawer under church bulletins and rubber bands, the way music leaked out on certain nights and already sounded expensive halfway down the block. The kind of room people said still mattered with just enough embarrassment to prove they believed it. The kind of room where the walls might do a song a favor or strip it to bone, and either way give you an answer.

His eyes dropped to the buy-in number.

Then did the math.

Then ran it again because the first answer annoyed him.

It did not work.

Not with the twenty-five back in the envelope.

Not with forty cents in his pocket.

Not with fourteen days left before Pike could file.

Not with eggs he had just eaten on credit so informal it was technically kindness and therefore more dangerous than debt.

He did the math a third time anyway, because Joey Woodsman had built half his adulthood in the thin alley between no and maybe if nothing else breaks.

Mae came by with the coffee pot and found him there.

"You looking at that showcase thing."

It was not a question.

Joey kept his eyes on the paper. "You know about it?"

"Duke's been running that five, six years now." Mae topped off his cup though he had not asked. "Every kid with a guitar and a dream sits in that booth, stares at that flyer, does the math, and comes up short. Same as you just did."

Joey turned a little. "Anybody get anywhere from it?"

Mae snorted once. "I pour coffee, Joey. I don't track music careers."

Then she paused, rag in hand, and wiped a clean spot cleaner on the counter while she thought.

"But no," she said. "Not that I heard. And I hear everything in this neighborhood. People tell me shit they wouldn't tell Jesus."

That got half a smile out of him.

"That's either a compliment or a confession."

"It's morning. It's both."

He looked back at the flyer.

The paper had not changed. The number had not shrunk. The pull of it stayed put anyway.

It bothered Joey how fast the miracle had turned into door money.

He went back to the booth and sat down.

Mae was refilling ketchup bottles at the service station. She gave him one look and kept working.

"Mae," he said.

"You can ask."

Joey frowned. "I didn't say I had a question."

"You got that look."

"What look."

"The one where your brain done outrun your circumstances and now both of them are fixing to drag me into it."

He nodded once because the accuracy deserved payment and he was still out of cash.

"If you were me," he said, "and you had a gold guitar you could sell for enough to cover the buy-in and, say, maybe rent for a while..." He let the sentence trail so it could pretend not to be confession. "Would you sell it?"

Mae stopped with the ketchup bottle in her hand.

"A gold guitar."

"Hypothetically."

Mae looked at him for a beat longer than the word deserved. She did not ask where such a thing would come from. She did not ask whether he had hit his head. She only set the bottle down and gave him the kind of look women like Mae gave men like Joey when the men had asked a dumb question wrapped in good grammar.

"Hypothetically," she said, "I'd sell the gold guitar, pay my rent, put the rest where I couldn't make a fool decision with it, and keep playing the wooden one."

Joey looked at his plate.

Mae went on.

"Because gold guitars are for looking at and wooden guitars are for playing. And you're a man who plays."

He turned the fork over in his hand once, then back. The handle was cheap stainless, bent a little near the neck. It flashed him, stupidly, to his father tapping time against a sugar caddy in some diner years ago while explaining why some church rooms made a voice bloom and some swallowed it whole. Joey had been too young for acoustics and exactly old enough to understand that rooms had opinions. He looked past Mae now to the flyer by the door and felt the old hunger rise in him, not just for a stage, but for a room that would answer back.

"What if the gold guitar is the only reason I can get in the door?"

Mae did not answer right away. She picked up the coffee pot again, served two men at the counter, took exact change from one of them without looking down, and came back.

Then she said, quietly enough that it landed harder:

"Honey, if it takes a gold guitar to open it, that ain't your door."

That should have settled it.

It didn't.

Joey knew, even while the sentence was still warm in the air, that part of him was already drafting arguments against it. The park let a song drift into fountain spray and bus brakes and strangers moving on. A room made it stay put and answer for itself. The flyer had the shape of that answer behind it, and he was weak to the shape.

He finished the eggs slower now. Thought while he ate.

Twenty-five dollars back in the thin envelope on the table beside Pike's notice.

Forty cents in his pocket.

Gold in the apartment.

Silver in the apartment waiting to be mistaken for an improvement.

Wood in his hands whenever the question was music instead of survival.

Fourteen days before Pike could file, no matter how many gods had recently wandered through his week.

The numbers stayed bad.

The flyer stayed there.

When the plate was clean except for toast crumbs and a drag of jelly, Joey reached into his pocket and took out the folded sheet music he used for everything he did not want to dignify with a real notebook page. The paper had gone cloth-soft at the corners from pocket sweat and repeat handling, the way his father's old set lists used to look by the end of a week. One side already held penciled arithmetic, half a chord map, and one line Joey had written in the dark last month and crossed out before it could get loud.

He drew a small box in the margin.

Inside it he wrote:

THE LANDMARK

Then he stared at the two words as if writing them down had made them either more possible or more foolish. Hard to tell which. Maybe both. Most ambitions started there.

He folded the page back up and left it in his pocket.

Mae was at the register pretending not to watch him prepare to be poor with dignity.

Joey took one of the paper guest checks from the little silver spindle by the napkins and turned it over. On the back he wrote, in block letters big enough to count as accountability:

IOU ONE TIP + EGGS - JOEY

He left it under the coffee cup.

Mae saw it and snorted without looking directly at him.

"That your legal document?"

"Binding under Georgia law."

"Get out my diner."

He stood, slid out of the booth, and headed for the door with the small loose ease of a man who had finally put eggs in his body.

At the bulletin board he slowed.

The goldenrod flyer had slipped a little more crooked in the draft from the door.

Joey lifted two fingers to it.

Straightened it.

Not much.

Just enough to make the way through it look better behaved than it was.
