Clarence's Shelf

He heard the shop before he saw it.

Not music. Not yet. A floor fan with one tired bearing. The papery drag of a page turning somewhere past the front bins. The thin hiss of a needle riding dust. Outside, Moreland kept doing what Moreland did in August: buses kneeling hard at the curb, brakes complaining, sneaker soles slapping gum-soft sidewalk, fryer grease rolling out of a place down the block, somebody laughing too loud outside the smoke shop as if volume alone could make the day less hot. But all of it came through Clarence's door dulled and padded, as if forty years of sleeves and cardboard and wood had decided the city could enter only one soft layer at a time.

Joey stepped inside and felt the day loosen its hand on the back of his neck by maybe ten percent, which in August counted.

There was no air-conditioning. You could tell that immediately. No artificial chill, no fluorescent cold pretending at mercy. The cool in Clarence's place came from brick shade, old plaster, dust, and the stubborn thermal patience of a thousand records standing shoulder to shoulder between the room and the sun. The shop smelled like paper sleeves, dry glue, old wood, cracked plastic, and the sweet stale edge of time made physical.

The cash envelope in Joey's shirt pocket got louder the second he crossed the threshold.

Out on the sidewalk it had been relief. In the pawn shop it had been arithmetic. In here it felt like he had brought pawn money into a room that still believed in reverence. He could still smell the pawn counter on his own fingers if he lifted his hand high enough: metal, polish, glass, old electricity. Twelve hundred dollars against his chest and forty cents in his pocket and the recovered twenty-five from the case, and none of it made him feel solvent. It made him feel handled.

He should have gone home first. Put the envelope under the chipped sugar jar or in the back of the dresser or anywhere that did not leave it pulsing against his sternum with every heartbeat. He should have sat at the apartment table under Pike's notice and looked the number in the face until it either became practical or stopped feeling like blasphemy. Instead he had turned toward Clarence's without really deciding to, because the thought of taking miracle money straight back into that hot apartment had felt wrong in a way he could not name without making it worse.

Clarence sat behind the counter on his stool with a book open in his hand and his glasses low on his nose. Mid-seventies maybe. Lean gone permanent. A face worn into patience rather than softness. The stool looked custom-made until you realized the opposite was true: Clarence had simply sat there long enough to train the wood into agreement.

He did not look up when Joey came in.

That was part of the shop too. Not rudeness. Structure. Clarence did not do retail reassurance. He let people arrive and reveal what kind of company they were before he offered any of his own.

Joey drifted toward the local shelf at the back, fingers trailing lightly over the plastic lips of the sleeves without pulling anything free. He had been coming in here off and on for years. Sometimes to cool down. Sometimes to browse. Sometimes because grief liked rooms where other people's ghosts got filed alphabetically and handled with better manners than the living usually managed. The smell of the sleeves pulled up one of those memories that was not big enough to become a scene, only a pressure: his father beside a bin years ago, tapping Joey's knuckles lightly and saying not the middle, son, the edges, edges are how you keep a thing decent. Joey had forgotten the sentence until the cardboard smell brought it back whole.

He let his hand fall.

The envelope sat hot and flat under his palm when he checked it through the shirt.

Too much money. Not enough money. Both true.

Gold guitar gone. Silver still under the bed. Wooden in the apartment by the chair where it belonged. Duke's flyer still goldenrod in his mind. Mae had already told him what kind of room took miracle money as tribute. The warning had only gotten more accurate since he sold the thing, which was rude of it. Accuracy usually was.

And still the wanting had not moved.

He heard Clarence close the book.

Not sharply. Just the padded thump of paper meeting paper.

Then the slow scrape of stool legs.

Joey turned in time to see Clarence disappear through the hanging bead curtain into the back room. No greeting. No how you been. No comment on the heat or the neighborhood or the face Joey had brought in with him, the face of a man who had just made himself possible in a way he did not trust.

The fan kept ticking. The street kept breathing beyond the propped door. Joey stood between the bins with one hand still near his shirt pocket and had the strange sensation that he had already been read.

Clarence came back carrying a sleeve in both hands.

He set it on the counter without flourish and resumed the stool.

"There," he said.

That was all.

Joey came forward because there was nothing else to do.

The sleeve was twelve inches of proof.

Small local label. Cheap matte cardboard worn silky at the corners from being handled and re-shelved and not quite forgotten. A color photograph gone faint at the edges. A young man with a guitar standing in what looked like a backyard or maybe a park, hard to tell because the green behind him had blurred itself into one southern wash of leaves and heat. Shirt sleeves rolled. Collar sitting wrong in the ordinary way collars sat wrong on men who owned one decent version of decent and wore it until the cloth admitted defeat. The face was his father's and not his father's at once. Younger than Joey had ever actually known him. Not yet thinned by the hospital. Not yet bent around omission and late bills and whatever private arithmetic had turned him into the man in the crooked frame over Joey's record player.

The shock did not arrive as tears. It came as the room suddenly going flat around the cardboard in his hands.

The room lost a dimension. The bins, the counter, the fan, the washed-out square of street beyond the door. All of it thinned around the cardboard in his hands.

His father's name sat printed across the bottom in plain black type, which was somehow worse than if it had been fancy. Under it, in smaller letters, a title Joey did not fully register because his eyes had already snagged on the liner note block reproduced from blue-ink handwriting.

His father's hand.

Not the big public signature from checks and Christmas cards. Not the ceremonial version he used when somebody wanted him to sign a church bulletin or a set list for a friend who had already had two beers and turned sentimental. This was the working hand. Grocery-list hand. Cassette-label hand. The same quick practical slant Joey remembered on masking tape stuck to shoeboxes and on notes left by the phone that said things like milk if cheap and call Clarence if he still has that Hopkins record. The sight of it pulled up the kitchen table in one clean burst: August window over the sink clouded with steam, his father standing at the table writing with his left shoulder raised because the fan never hit that side of the room, Joey doing homework badly while the pen scratched over an envelope that had once held a bill and now held a song title.

Ten tracks on the back. Titles he knew in his fingers before he knew them in print. Titles he had learned as shapes in the hand, not as a list. Seeing them typeset like that made the family repertoire feel less like blood and more like public record, which should have steadied him and instead made him a little nauseous.

He did not read all the way down. Not yet.

The first few names were enough to tell him what he was looking at, and once grief got agreement from evidence it tended to stop asking useful questions.

"Where'd this come from?" he said, though the sleeve had already answered most of it.

Clarence slid his bookmark into place with the same care he used on records.

"He brought it in himself."

Joey looked up.

Clarence nodded toward the front door. "Walked in the same way you just did. Through that door. Stood right there. Set a little stack on the counter and said, 'Would you carry these.'"

"That's it?"

"That was the speech."

Joey looked back at the sleeve.

He could picture it too easily. That was the problem. Not some younger, brasher version of his father. Not a man in full artist plumage. Just his father carrying records under one arm the way he used to carry groceries, asking the question plain and hoping plain would count for something.

"How many'd he bring you?"

Clarence settled back on the stool. "Enough for it to count as hope and not enough for it to count as business."

Joey gave out one breath that was almost a laugh.

"That is a real Clarence answer."

"You asked the wrong old man if you wanted numbers first."

The fan clicked in the corner. Outside, a bus sighed and pulled away. Heat showed white in the doorway like something visible and mean.

Joey turned the sleeve over again. The back had a short label note, a player list that was mostly his father and the guitar, and the reproduced handwritten paragraph low on the sleeve like a private thought somebody had let into print by mistake. The whole object had the dignity of a thing done carefully once because there was probably no money to do it twice.

These songs had not lived only in the apartment. Not only in the kitchen with the window over the sink fogging in summer. Not only in the car with the windows down and his father tapping the wheel at red lights. Not only on the porch when the air finally turned breathable after ten and the bugs made a second rhythm under the tune. They had existed in public. On cardboard. In bins. On a shelf strangers could reach without asking the Woodsman family permission.

His father had left evidence.

Joey set the sleeve on the counter carefully, as if any quick movement would count as disrespect.

"You knew him well?"

Clarence made a face that suggested he distrusted the phrase on principle.

"I knew what kind of man he was in a record shop. That's different."

"What's the difference?"

"A record shop shows you what people revere when nobody's asking them to explain themselves." Clarence folded one hand over the other on top of the closed book. "Some people come in hunting. Some browsing. Some stealing with their eyes because that's all they can afford. Your father came in listening."

Joey waited.

Clarence lifted one hand and mimed a pair of fingertips moving along a spine.

"With his fingers," he said. "Not to show he cared. Because he did."

The sentence landed with the clean insult of recognition.

Joey's own hand had gone back to the edge of the sleeve without him noticing. He pulled it away.

"A lot of people do that."

"No." Clarence's voice stayed mild. "A lot of people perform that. Different thing."

Silence opened and sat there.

Through the front door came the softened bark of a dog and the rattle of a skateboard hitting one rough seam in the sidewalk. Somebody outside said damn in the tone of a person who had dropped something small and necessary. The city kept doing business out there. Inside, the record on the counter made a smaller room of its own.

Joey heard himself ask, "Did anybody buy it?"

He could hear the real question under it even if he hoped Clarence might not. Did anybody hear him. Did it land anywhere but home. Had they made a private saint out of a man the world had simply not needed.

Clarence's eyes moved once past Joey toward the front of the store, toward nothing visible.

"Two copies went out for real," he said. "Same afternoon. Same pair."

Joey waited.

Clarence let the waiting sit one beat longer than politeness required. He was not cruel. He simply believed people ought to mean their curiosity if they were going to spend it.

"Man came in first," he said. "Dressed like he had specific opinions and money enough to obey them. Not flashy. Worse than flashy. Intentional. He went straight to the folk shelf like he'd already walked the store in his head. Picked up every record, front and back, read the notes, put it back where he found it, square as a survey line."

Something recent and impossible brushed the back of Joey's mind, and he refused to follow it.

"And the woman?"

"Gold in her hair." Clarence looked toward the back wall where the oldest stock lived. "She wasn't interested in the records first. She was interested in the room. Walked the aisles touching sleeves, cabinets, the edge of the counter. Not shopping. Taking the temperature."

Joey kept his face arranged. If he started naming impossible people out loud in a record shop in the middle of the day, the last two days would quit passing for strange and start passing for insanity. He was not ready to give reality that much ground.

Clarence went on.

"She asked me the oddest question anybody's asked in forty years of me sitting in this building."

"What question?"

"What the oldest record in the shop was."

Joey frowned. "Oldest as in most valuable?"

"No. Oldest as in oldest." Clarence's look made plain what he thought of people who confused those categories. "Not rarest. Not priciest. The one that had stayed."

The envelope in Joey's pocket seemed to thicken.

"What'd you show her?"

"Blind Willie McTell seventy-eight. Nineteen twenty-seven. Hairline crack that doesn't travel if you treat it like a living thing." Clarence's voice flattened into recollection. "She held it in both hands and shut her eyes. Stood there quiet for half a minute. Opened them, said thank you, and set it back exactly where it belonged."

Joey looked down at his father's record again. The cheap print. The faded green. The handwriting block. Public and small enough to fit in a bin.

"And they bought these?"

"Bought two." Clarence nodded. "The man found the first and showed it to her. She nodded like something had been confirmed. He found the second and brought both copies up front. I asked if he wanted two of the same record. He said, 'One to listen to and one to keep.'"

"You asked if he knew the artist?"

Clarence's mouth moved by maybe half an inch. Approval, or the nearest thing he carried in stock.

"I did."

"And?"

"He looked at me like I'd asked if he knew what breathing was."

That should have felt reassuring. Instead it felt invasive. Like strangers had somehow known how to handle part of his dead better than Joey had known they were even in the room with it.

He looked down at the blue-ink liner notes until the hand blurred.

If the city had held some version of his father all this time, if there had been records on shelves and stories in shops and rooms Joey had never entered, then his own life started looking less like fidelity and more like hiding in duty's clothes.

Clarence rose again, slower this time because his knees had opinions and expected to be heard. He bent beneath the counter and came back with an opened copy in a plain inner sleeve.

"This one doesn't leave the building," he said. "That one can."

He meant the worn shop copy in his hands and the cleaner shelf copy on the counter.

That simple.

Joey watched him carry the record to the turntable at the end of the counter, the good old one Clarence kept level on purpose. The speakers bolted high on either side of the room were not impressive speakers. They were honest ones. Good enough to tell the truth if the record had any.

Clarence brushed the vinyl with a velvet pad, not for ceremony but because dust was real and negligence was a choice. The motion tugged up another quick memory from nowhere useful: his father standing over the turntable at home with the little record brush, telling Joey not to drop the needle like you're punishing it. Joey had not thought of that in years. Apparently the body kept its own archive.

Clarence held out his hand.

Joey gave him the cleaner sleeve without thinking.

The pass from one hand to the other did something the envelope in his shirt had not done. It restored scale.

Clarence set the needle down.

The first sound was static.

Only a breath of it, but enough to make the years audible. Dust. Groove wear. Old room. Old pressing. Old time. Then his father's voice came through the speakers.

Lower than Joey remembered.

That was the first shock. Or maybe only less domesticated. On the porch, in the kitchen, in the car with the windows down, the voice had always arrived wrapped in family acoustics. Here it came stripped of that padding. Not household sound now. A man's voice laid into grooves and sent outward. The guitar under it was plain and exact. No vanity to the picking. No extra curl of phrase trying to convince anybody of anything. The song entered the shop and the shop answered by getting out of its way.

The fan in the corner kept ticking, but softer now, as if even that one bad bearing had decided to behave. The sunlight at the front windows quit insisting on itself. The rows of sleeves behind Clarence stopped being inventory and turned into witnesses. Outside, a truck changed gears and somebody shouted down the block, but the noises came in folded small, like margin notes under the track.

Joey had expected grief.

What hit him first was labor.

How much work lived in the ease of it. The timing of the breath. The choice not to ornament. The hand refusing to show off when the song itself could do the job. On the porch Joey had always heard those performances as natural. In the speakers he heard the years inside them, the cuts made on purpose, the craft hidden in the plainness. It was like finding the joinery in a table you had mistaken for simple.

A tiny brush of nail against string took him cleanly back to the apartment kitchen one winter evening, his father warming stiff fingers around a coffee mug before playing the first song because cold changed the hand and the hand changed everything. The memory lasted less than a second. Enough to sting. Not enough to sit down and become a full flashback. The song kept moving, and the room with it.

Atlanta got into the sound without ever naming itself on the track.

Not sirens. Not traffic. Something harder to name. Small rooms. Church fellowship halls with bad coffee in the back. Bar corners where the audience did not decide to listen until halfway through the second verse. Patios on hot nights where ceiling fans just pushed warm air around. The music did not sound like private inheritance anymore. It sounded rubbed against the city.

The song ended.

Clarence lifted the needle before the silence could start acting important.

Joey realized he had gone motionless at the counter, one palm flat against the wood as if the shop might otherwise tilt.

"He came in here the same way you do," Clarence said.

"What's that mean?"

"Like the room already belonged to the music and he was just checking whether we'd figured that out yet."

Joey almost laughed and didn't.

He looked at the shelf copy still lying on the counter, the one Clarence had said could leave.

"You kept this all these years?"

Clarence shook his head once. "Storage is what you do to something you don't trust yourself to need. This wasn't storage."

"What was it?"

"Keeping." He said the word without sermon weight. "Knowing a thing isn't done because commerce got bored with it."

Joey looked up.

Clarence's face had not softened. It had only become exact.

"If you're asking me whether your father got anywhere with it," he said, "the answer is no in the way people usually mean that question."

There it was.

The hard part under the comfort.

Joey did not speak. The cash envelope against his chest suddenly felt hot enough to leave a mark through the shirt. Gold turned into paper. Paper waiting to become Duke's money, maybe. Duke's room, Duke's ledger, Duke's permission. Meanwhile his father had made a real record, put it in the city, and still lost the world part of the argument.

Clarence rested one hand on the turntable lid.

"He made a real record," he said. "Played real rooms. Sang real songs. The music was real. That's the easy part. The world part was harder. The world was not obliged to reward him for being honest."

Joey swallowed.

That was the trouble with facts stated plainly. They did not leave you anywhere to stand except inside them.

"So what am I supposed to do with that?"

Clarence shrugged one shoulder. "You're grown. Figure it out."

The answer was so plain it nearly counted as mercy.

Joey looked back down at the sleeve. The printed photograph. The ordinary shirt. The handwriting made public. Now that the first shock had loosened a little, his eyes traveled farther than they had before. Only a little. Not all the way with comprehension. Just enough to notice that the second side had been handled harder than the first: wear around the center hole, a thumb-soft patch on the printed list, somebody going back there on purpose.

He could have tilted the sleeve another inch and read the track list like evidence.

He didn't.

He was already standing in too much of his father's life for one afternoon.

Clarence saw his glance and, for once, said nothing at all.

That silence mattered.

Joey laid one hand on the shelf copy as if asking the object itself whether it wanted to come with him.

"How much?"

Clarence gave him a look flat enough to plane wood.

"Put your money away."

"Clarence."

"I said put your money away." His eyes flicked once, pointedly, to Joey's shirt pocket. "Whatever you sold to walk in here sounding like a man who just bit through foil, I don't want that money for this."

Joey opened his mouth and shut it again.

Because there was no lie available that would not insult both of them.

The refusal hit harder than it should have because Pike would take it. Pike would flatten the bills with that square hand of his and the clock would keep moving unless Joey gave him enough to slow it.

Clarence nudged the sleeved copy an inch closer across the counter.

"Take that one. The open copy stays here. You've still got a turntable."

"Barely."

"Barely counts if the needle lands."

The line was dry enough to count as affection. In Clarence that was practically an embrace.

Joey let out a breath that almost made it to laughter and didn't. He picked the record up carefully, tucking the sleeve under his arm with more reverence than he had shown the gold guitar. That fact did not improve his opinion of himself.

"Thanks," he said.

Clarence settled back down onto the stool with the small grunt of a man resuming a long argument with gravity.

"Don't thank me yet." He reopened the book. "You still have to live with what it tells you."

That was closer to a blessing than Clarence was likely to offer in public.

Joey stood there a second longer than he needed to. Not because he wanted more explanation. He didn't. The record had already widened his father in a direction he was not ready to manage. Private grief had been hard enough. Shared residue was worse. If the city remembered the man, then Joey's inheritance was not a sealed family relic. It belonged to something larger and less containable.

He nodded once and headed for the door.

Outside, the heat met him like punishment resuming after a pause.

The block came back all at once. A MARTA bus idling at the curb with its door open. Sun thrown hard off windshields. Someone selling incense from a folding table farther down. The medicinal reek from the tattoo place. Brake dust. Old brick. A siren somewhere not close enough to matter unless it was yours. Joey stepped into all of it with the record under one arm and the envelope still flat against his chest, and for a second the two weights seemed to argue inside him about what counted as value.

Across the street somebody shouted for somebody named Tasha. A bike chain clicked. Heat climbed off the sidewalk through the soles of his shoes. The cardboard sleeve tucked under his arm had almost no physical weight to it, but it pressed at him harder than the money did.

He looked down at the cover.

His father stared back from younger years and cheaper printing, public now in a way Joey had never been asked to survive before.

He started walking.

Three steps later, from the dim behind him, he heard the breath of needle static again.

He stopped without turning.

Then came his father's voice once more, but changed now by distance and doorway and whatever seam Clarence had set the stylus into. Barer. Less settled. Not revelation exactly. More like pressure shifting in a room before weather breaks. Something in the second side the shop itself wanted back on.

Joey kept one hand on the sleeve under his arm and stood there one beat too long in the heat, listening without understanding.

Then he walked on.
